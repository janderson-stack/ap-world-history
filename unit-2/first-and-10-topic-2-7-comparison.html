Comparing the Networks

Three great trade networks connected Afro-Eurasia between c. 1200 and c. 1450: the Silk Roads, the Indian Ocean, and the trans-Saharan routes. Comparison reveals both their shared patterns and their important differences — and builds the analytical foundation for AP writing.

Comparison Causation Argumentation

Before You Read

For each network, track: What technology made it possible? What goods did it carry? What religion did it spread? What cultural blending resulted? Good comparison requires precise observation of each case.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to identify one meaningful similarity and one important difference between any two of the three trade networks — with specific evidence from both — and explain why the comparison matters.

1

Comparison

What All Three Networks Shared

Despite their geographic and technological differences, the Silk Roads, Indian Ocean, and trans-Saharan networks all performed the same basic function: connecting distant societies through long-distance exchange. In each case, luxury goods moved across vast distances to meet elite demand. In each case, religions spread through merchant contact. In each case, diasporic communities of merchants settled permanently in foreign places. In each case, wealthy intermediary states emerged to tax and control the flow of goods.

This pattern — trade creates connection, connection spreads culture, culture transforms societies — is one of the core analytical frameworks for AP World History Unit 2. Recognizing it across three different contexts demonstrates historical thinking at its best.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Identifying similarities requires you to go beyond surface-level observation. The fact that all three networks "carried goods" is too obvious. The meaningful similarity is that all three created diasporic communities, spread religions through commerce, and generated state power from trade revenues. Precision makes AP comparisons earn full credit.

2

Key Concept

How the Networks Differed: Technology and Goods

The differences between the networks are just as historically significant as the similarities. The most obvious difference is technology: Silk Road merchants used camels and horses on overland relay routes; Indian Ocean sailors used dhow ships and monsoon winds; trans-Saharan merchants used specialized camel caravans across desert terrain. Each required different infrastructure, different knowledge, and different organizational systems.

The goods traded also differed. The Silk Roads were famous for silk and porcelain moving west, horses moving east. The Indian Ocean carried spices, textiles, gold, and ivory. The trans-Saharan routes were built on gold moving north and salt moving south. These differences reflect the natural endowments and economic needs of the regions connected by each network.

Silk Roads

Camels + relay horses, silk + porcelain, Buddhism westward, overland routes

Indian Ocean

Dhows + monsoon, spices + textiles, Islam to Southeast Asia, maritime routes

Trans-Saharan

Camel caravans, gold + salt, Islam southward, desert routes

AP Thinking — Causation

The differences between networks were not random — they were caused by geography. The Indian Ocean's monsoon winds dictated maritime technology; the Sahara's aridity dictated camel-based transport; Central Asian steppe terrain dictated the relay system. Environment shaped commerce, and commerce shaped the institutions that sustained it.

3

Key Concept

Cultural Differences: Which Religions Spread Where

The dominant religions spread by each network differed. The Silk Roads primarily carried Buddhism eastward into Central Asia and China, though Islam also traveled overland routes. The Indian Ocean network was the most powerful vehicle for Islam's spread into Southeast Asia and East Africa — merchant-led, gradual, and voluntary. The trans-Saharan routes spread Islam southward into West Africa through elite conversion and scholarly prestige.

The cultural blending that resulted from each network also differed. Swahili civilization — a synthesis of Bantu African and Arab Islamic cultures — emerged specifically from Indian Ocean trade. The great West African intellectual tradition of Timbuktu emerged from trans-Saharan Islamic contact. The Silk Roads produced cities like Samarkand, blending Persian, Turkic, Islamic, and Chinese cultural elements.

"Each network produced its own kind of cultural blend — the same mechanism of merchant contact producing different results in different geographic and cultural contexts."

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Comparison is not just describing two things. It means making an argument about what a similarity or difference reveals. Why does it matter that all three networks spread religion? It reveals that commerce and religion were not separate domains — they reinforced each other. Why does it matter that they spread different religions to different places? It reveals how geography shaped the direction of cultural influence.

4

Key Concept

How to Write AP Comparison

AP comparison questions ask you to identify a similarity AND a difference, with specific evidence for each. The key is to be precise. Vague similarities ("all three networks traded goods") earn less credit than specific ones ("all three networks led to the permanent settlement of diasporic merchant communities in foreign port cities").

The best comparison sentences follow this structure: "[Network A] and [Network B] both [similar pattern], as shown by [specific evidence from A] and [specific evidence from B]. However, they differed in [specific dimension], since [A did X] while [B did Y]." This structure forces you to use evidence and make your reasoning explicit — exactly what AP graders are looking for.

"When Portuguese ships entered the Indian Ocean in the 1490s, they didn't discover a world of isolated societies. They encountered a trading world that had been operating for centuries without them."

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

All three Unit 2 trade networks — Silk Roads, Indian Ocean, trans-Saharan — shared the same core pattern: merchant-led exchange created diasporic communities, spread religions, and generated state power from trade revenue. They differed in technology (camels vs. ships vs. caravans), goods (silk vs. spices vs. gold-salt), and which religions they spread where. AP comparison requires specific evidence from both cases and an argument about what the similarity or difference reveals.
