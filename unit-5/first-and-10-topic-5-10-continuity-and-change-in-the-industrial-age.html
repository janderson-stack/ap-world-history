01

Evidence of Change

A New Economic and Connected World

Stand at the year 1900 and look back to 1750, and the most obvious story is transformation. The biggest economic change was the rise of industrial capitalism: wealth increasingly came from factories, machines, and invested capital rather than from land alone. Continued improvement in manufacturing methods made consumer goods cheaper, more available, and more varied than ever before, and standards of living rose — at least for some.

Technology shrank the world. Railroads, steamships, and the telegraph made exploration, development, and communication possible in interior regions across the globe, driving a surge in trade and migration. A message that once took weeks by ship could cross an ocean by wire in minutes; goods and people moved at speeds no earlier generation could have imagined.

If you only counted these developments, you would conclude that the world was remade. And in its machines, markets, and networks, it largely was. But counting what is new is only half of a historian’s job.

AP Thinking — Continuity and Change

Industrial capitalism and new technology are your strongest evidence of change. Pick the single development you think changed daily life the most — and be ready to say why it counts as a genuine break with the past.

02

Evidence of Continuity

Old Hierarchies and Unfinished Fights

Now look underneath the machines. Industrialization created new classes — a rising middle class and an industrial working class — but steep hierarchy itself persisted. A small group held most of the wealth and power while the majority labored for wages. The faces and the language changed; the basic pattern of the few over the many did not.

The struggle for rights and representation also continued rather than began. Labor unions organized workers to demand better wages and hours, and feminist movements pressed for suffrage and education — extending fights over who counts politically that reached back to the revolutionary era. The questions were old; industrialization just raised the stakes.

Even the era’s biggest political forces were carryovers. Enlightenment ideas about reason, natural rights, and the social contract did not stop in 1800 — they kept circulating and kept preceding revolutions. The age of revolution and the building of new nation-states that began in the 18th century rolled on through the 1800s, and nationalism remained a major force shaping states and empires, from the unifications of Germany and Italy to independence movements in Latin America.

AP Thinking — Causation

Enlightenment thought and nationalism kept driving political change long after the 1700s. Choose one idea and trace how it caused a specific political change in this period — a revolution, a new nation-state, or an expanded set of rights.

03

The Argument

Arguing the Extent of Change

Here is the move that turns a list into an argument. The Learning Objective does not ask whether things changed — of course they did. It asks to what extent. That word forces you to take a position and weigh the two sides against each other.

A strong response commits to a defensible claim: that change was far-reaching, or limited, or — most powerfully — uneven, transforming economies and technology while leaving social and political hierarchies stubbornly familiar. Whatever you choose, you back it with specific evidence of both change and continuity, and then you explain why the balance tips the way your thesis says it does.

The best arguments concede the other side: “Although steep hierarchies persisted, the economic and technological break was so deep that…” That qualification is not a weakness — it is the historian’s honesty, and on the AP exam it is exactly what earns the complexity point. The goal of 5.10 is not to declare “everything changed” or “nothing changed,” but to argue, with evidence, how much — and for whom.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Write a one-sentence claim that takes a position on the extent of change. Make it defensible — something a reasonable person could disagree with — and signal that you will weigh both change and continuity.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Industrialization transformed economies, technology, and global connections between 1750 and 1900 — but steep social hierarchies, the fight for rights, and the Enlightenment and nationalist currents of the 1700s carried on. A strong answer weighs that change against that continuity and argues how much really changed, and for whom.
