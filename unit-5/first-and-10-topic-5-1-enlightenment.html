Topic 5.1 — The Enlightenment  |  AP World History: Modern

First & 10 Reading

The Age of Reason

The Enlightenment was one of the most consequential intellectual movements in world history — a 17th and 18th century movement that challenged the foundations of traditional authority by claiming that reason, not scripture or tradition, should be the basis for understanding the world. Enlightenment thinkers argued that individuals possessed natural rights that no king or church could legitimately take away. But who counted as "individuals"? The Enlightenment's claim to universalism was real — and its limits were just as real.

Contextualization

The World the Enlightenment Challenged

To understand the Enlightenment, you need to understand what it was pushing against. The world of c. 1650–1750 was organized around three overlapping systems of authority: monarchy (kings ruled by divine right — God had appointed them, and questioning their authority was tantamount to questioning God); the Catholic Church (which controlled education, marriage law, inheritance, and the interpretation of natural events); and inherited social hierarchy (aristocracy held legal privileges from birth; serfs and peasants owed labor and tribute regardless of their talent or work).

These systems were not simply corrupt or inefficient — they had internal logic and deep historical roots. Divine right monarchy justified itself through centuries of theological argument. The Church provided social welfare, education, and the framework for making sense of suffering and death. Aristocratic hierarchy was stabilized by custom, law, and religious sanction. Challenging any one of them meant challenging all three at once.

What made the Enlightenment possible was a combination of factors: the Scientific Revolution (Newton, Copernicus, Galileo) had demonstrated that reason and observation could reveal truths that scripture had not anticipated; the printing press had created a reading public that could access challenging ideas without clerical mediation; the Protestant Reformation had fractured the Church's intellectual monopoly; and the experience of colonial encounter had raised new questions about the universality of European cultural norms.

AP Thinking — Contextualization

The Enlightenment was not an isolated European intellectual development — it was a response to specific historical conditions that had been building since the 15th century. The Scientific Revolution provided the method (reason and observation); the printing press provided the infrastructure; colonial encounter provided a challenge to European assumptions about civilizational hierarchy. Effective contextualization for the Enlightenment requires identifying which of these prior developments made Enlightenment thinking possible.

2

Key Concept

Natural Rights, Social Contract, and Popular Sovereignty

Three ideas sit at the core of Enlightenment political thought, and the AP exam will expect you to distinguish them precisely.

Natural rights (associated most directly with John Locke, 1689) argued that individuals are born with certain rights — to life, liberty, and property — that exist independently of any government or religious authority. These rights are not granted by a king; they cannot be taken away by a king. Government's only legitimate function is to protect them. If a government violates natural rights, the people have the right to overthrow it.

The social contract (developed by both Locke and Rousseau, though differently) argued that government derives its authority from an agreement between the governed and the governors — not from God or hereditary right. Locke's social contract was between individuals and a government formed to protect their natural rights; Rousseau's version emphasized the "general will" of the community as the source of legitimate authority. Both versions were revolutionary: they made government accountable to the people, not to God.

Popular sovereignty — the idea that legitimate authority comes from the people — followed logically from the social contract. If government serves the people's interests and can be revoked by the people, then the people are the ultimate source of political authority. This was a direct challenge to divine right monarchy, which located authority in God's appointment rather than popular consent.

AP Thinking — Causation

These ideas were causes of Atlantic Revolutions. The American Declaration of Independence (1776) explicitly invokes Lockean natural rights ("life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"). The French Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789) opens with natural rights and popular sovereignty. Tracing the causal chain from Enlightenment ideas to revolutionary documents to political action is one of the most important causation arguments in AP World History.

3

Key Concept

The Philosophes and Their Ideas

The philosophes were the public intellectuals of the Enlightenment — writers, thinkers, and polemicists who popularized Enlightenment ideas through pamphlets, novels, encyclopedias, and letters. They were not a unified school but a loose intellectual community that debated ferociously with each other.

John Locke (1632–1704, England): natural rights, social contract, government by consent. His Second Treatise on Government (1689) argued that government exists to protect natural rights and can be legitimately overthrown if it fails.

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778, Geneva/France): popular sovereignty, general will, critique of civilization. His Social Contract (1762) argued that individuals should submit their will to the "general will" of the community — a more collectivist vision than Locke's.

Voltaire (1694–1778, France): religious tolerance, freedom of speech, critique of the Catholic Church. "Crush the infamous thing" — his call to destroy clerical power — made him the most dangerous philosophe in the Church's eyes.

Baron de Montesquieu (1689–1755, France): separation of powers. His Spirit of the Laws (1748) argued that a good government separates legislative, executive, and judicial power — a structural argument that directly influenced the American Constitution.

Mary Wollstonecraft (1759–1797, England): women's natural rights. Her Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) argued that if natural rights applied to all humans, they applied to women — a logical extension of Enlightenment premises that most male philosophes refused to accept.

Adam Smith (1723–1790, Scotland): laissez-faire economics. His Wealth of Nations (1776) argued that free markets, not mercantilist regulation, produced prosperity — an economic Enlightenment that would shape capitalism.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Rousseau and Locke agreed on the social contract but disagreed about what it meant. Locke's version protected individual rights against government overreach; Rousseau's version asked individuals to subordinate their will to the community's. This difference would eventually produce two different political traditions: liberal democracy (Locke) and more collectivist or even authoritarian "general will" thinking (Rousseau). A strong comparison argument explains WHY the difference existed — Locke was writing to protect property-owning individuals from royal power; Rousseau was writing to address the alienation of individuals from corrupted society.

4

Key Concept

The Limits of Enlightenment Universalism

The Enlightenment claimed to be universal — its truths about reason and natural rights applied to all humans. But in practice, Enlightenment thought was deeply constrained by the social position of the people who produced it.

Women: virtually every Enlightenment thinker except Wollstonecraft assumed that women were naturally less rational than men and therefore not entitled to full political rights. Rousseau — the theorist of popular sovereignty — wrote extensively about why women should stay home and raise citizens rather than participate in public life. The French Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen famously excluded women from citizenship; Olympe de Gouges responded with the Declaration of the Rights of Woman and Female Citizen (1791) — and was executed by the revolutionary government in 1793.

Enslaved people: the most glaring contradiction. Virtually all Atlantic Revolutionaries lived in economies partly or entirely dependent on enslaved African labor. Thomas Jefferson wrote that "all men are created equal" while enslaving over 600 people during his lifetime. Enlightenment arguments about natural rights were explicitly applied to argue for the abolition of slavery — by Condorcet, by the Abbé Raynal, and eventually by the enslaved people of Saint-Domingue who launched the Haitian Revolution. But white revolutionaries in France, Britain, and the United States refused to apply universal rights to the enslaved, revealing that Enlightenment universalism was, in practice, not universal.

Colonial subjects: Enlightenment thinkers debated whether non-European peoples were capable of reason and therefore eligible for natural rights. Most concluded — with devastating consequences — that they were not yet ready for self-governance, providing intellectual justification for colonialism.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Was the Enlightenment genuinely revolutionary, or was it a rationalization of existing privilege? A strong argument acknowledges both sides: the Enlightenment produced real ideas about rights and equality that genuinely threatened existing hierarchies — and that were used by enslaved people, women, and colonial subjects to demand inclusion. But the Enlightenment also produced sophisticated intellectual justifications for racial hierarchy, gender exclusion, and colonial domination. The most powerful insight: the contradiction between Enlightenment universalism and Enlightenment exclusions was itself a source of future radicalization.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The Enlightenment (c. 1650–1800) challenged traditional authority through three key ideas: natural rights (Locke — individuals are born with rights government cannot take away), social contract (Locke and Rousseau — government derives authority from the people, not from God), and popular sovereignty (the people are the ultimate source of political authority). These ideas were spread through print culture — the Encyclopédie, pamphlets, novels, and salons — to educated elites across the Atlantic world. But Enlightenment universalism had profound limits: women were mostly excluded (Wollstonecraft being the major exception); enslaved people were excluded by most white philosophes despite the logical implications of natural rights; colonial subjects were rationalized as not yet capable of self-governance. These contradictions would fuel the most radical revolution of the era — the Haitian Revolution — and shape political debate for centuries.
