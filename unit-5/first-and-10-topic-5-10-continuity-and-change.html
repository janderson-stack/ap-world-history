01

Contextualization

What CCOT Requires — and Why It's Hard

The CCOT framework — continuity and change over time — asks a deceptively simple question: what changed, and what stayed the same? The difficulty is that the answer depends on where you stand. From the perspective of a British factory owner in 1900, the period 1750–1900 was one of unprecedented progress — industrial prosperity, expanding political rights, abolition of slavery, and the world's greatest empire. From the perspective of an Igbo farmer in colonial Nigeria in 1900, the same period meant the sudden arrival of British colonial administrators, forced labor, the suppression of local political authority, and the reorganization of the local economy around palm oil exports for British industries.

Both perspectives are historically accurate. The challenge of CCOT is to hold them simultaneously — not to choose one narrative of progress or one narrative of exploitation, but to explain the relationship between the two. The period's liberation and its exploitation were not separate phenomena occurring in different places. They were often structurally connected: the industrial technology that produced European prosperity also produced European imperial power; the same commodity chains that enriched Lancashire mill owners extracted value from the West African or Indian producers of raw materials.

A strong CCOT argument for 1750–1900 does not say "it was both good and bad." It says specifically: what changed, for whom, caused by what? What continued, for whom, maintained by what? And what was the relationship between the changes and the continuities?

AP Thinking — CCOT Framework

CCOT: Before identifying changes and continuities, establish your analytical framework. A strong CCOT argument requires three elements: (1) identifying what changed; (2) identifying what continued; and (3) explaining the relationship between them — were they independent phenomena, or were they causally connected? The relationship is the argument.

02

Key Concept

The Major Changes: Industrialization, Revolutions, and New Nation-States

The period c. 1750–1900 produced transformative economic change: in 1750, most of the world's people were employed in agriculture; by 1900, Britain, the United States, Germany, France, Belgium, and Japan had industrial economies in which mechanized factory production dominated manufacturing and global commodity markets integrated formerly regional economies. The scale was historically unprecedented: Britain's output per person roughly quadrupled between 1760 and 1900; the United States became the world's largest industrial economy by 1890.

Political change was equally dramatic. The Atlantic revolutions — American (1776), French (1789), Haitian (1804), Latin American (1808–1825) — challenged the principle that political authority derived from hereditary monarchy and divine right, establishing republican and constitutional forms of government across the Western hemisphere. Nationalist ideology drove the formation of new nation-states: German unification (1871), Italian unification (1861), Greek independence (1821). By 1900, constitutional monarchy or republic had replaced absolute monarchy as the standard form of government in Europe and the Americas.

Technological change compressed time and distance in ways that changed the texture of human experience: the steam railroad (1820s onward) moved people and goods at previously impossible speeds; the electric telegraph (transatlantic cable, 1866) allowed messages to travel at the speed of light across oceans; the oceangoing steam ship made global trade faster and more reliable. And social change produced new classes — the bourgeoisie and the industrial working class — new institutions (public schools, labor unions, mass-circulation newspapers), and new ideologies (liberalism, nationalism, socialism, Social Darwinism) that competed to explain and shape the industrial world.

AP Thinking — Causation

Causation: The major changes of 1750–1900 were not independent events — they were causally connected. How did industrialization drive political change? How did the steam railroad and telegraph enable both industrial integration and imperial control? Tracing the causal connections between economic, technological, political, and social change is the foundation of a strong CCOT argument.

03

Key Concept

The Major Continuities: Patriarchy, Empire, and Rural Poverty

The most fundamental continuity of the period was patriarchy. In 1750, women in virtually every society were legally and politically subordinate to men. By 1900, the picture had changed at the margins: New Zealand had granted women's suffrage in 1893, the Married Women's Property Act (1882) had given British married women property rights, and first-wave feminist movements were gaining momentum. But the fundamental structure of gender subordination remained intact globally. Women could not vote in the United States, Britain, France, Germany, or Japan. Women's wages were lower than men's in every economy. Legal subordination of wives to husbands persisted in most jurisdictions. The century of revolutions that proclaimed human equality consistently excluded women from those proclaimed rights.

Imperial systems did not contract — they expanded. European powers controlled roughly 35% of the world's land surface in 1800; by 1900, they controlled approximately 84%. The scramble for Africa (1880s–1890s) partitioned virtually the entire continent among European powers in less than two decades, accomplished using industrial military technology — breech-loading rifles, machine guns, and gunboats — that gave European forces overwhelming advantage over African armies. The same period that produced democratic revolutions in the Atlantic world produced the most extensive colonial domination of Africa and Asia in history. These were not separate phenomena: imperial domination abroad and democratic expansion at home were often justified by the same language — the "civilizing mission" framed colonial subjects as not yet capable of self-governance.

And for most of humanity, rural life and agricultural poverty persisted. Despite urbanization, the majority of the world's population in 1900 remained in rural areas engaged in agriculture. Industrialization was geographically concentrated; for most people in Africa, Asia, Latin America, and Eastern Europe, daily life in 1900 was not fundamentally different from daily life in 1750. The abolition of chattel slavery was real — but indentured labor, debt peonage, and convict labor systems replaced it with nominally voluntary but structurally coercive arrangements that produced similar outcomes for workers with less formal legal protection.

AP Thinking — CCOT and Complexity

Complexity: The relationship between the period's changes and continuities is not simple. How were the industrial revolution and imperial expansion causally connected — did the same forces that drove industrialization also drive colonial expansion? How did the abolition of chattel slavery relate to the emergence of new forms of coercive labor? Showing the causal connections between change and continuity is what moves a response from descriptive to analytical.

04

Key Concept

Building the CCOT Argument: Thesis, Evidence, and Synthesis

A CCOT thesis for the period c. 1750–1900 must do three things: identify what changed, identify what continued, and make an evaluative claim about the significance or nature of the relationship between them. Weak: "From 1750 to 1900, many things changed, including industrialization and political revolutions, but other things stayed the same, like gender inequality." This is a list. Strong: "While industrialization and Atlantic revolutions transformed the economic and political structures of Europe and the Americas — creating new classes, new nation-states, and new ideologies of individual rights — these changes intensified rather than reduced global inequality: imperial expansion, the persistence of patriarchy, and the replacement of chattel slavery with new forms of coercive labor demonstrate that liberation for some was structurally connected to continued subordination for others."

The key move in the strong thesis is the causal claim: it is not saying that change and continuity happened simultaneously (obvious) — it is arguing that they were causally connected: that liberation for some required exploitation of others. That is an argument someone could agree or disagree with. CCOT requires not just describing the pattern but evaluating what it means.

Regional comparison strengthens CCOT analysis. The same period looked radically different depending on where you stood: in Britain, it was the Industrial Revolution and expanding political rights; in West Africa, it was the end of the slave trade followed by colonial partition; in India, it was the consolidation of British colonial rule and de-industrialization of the textile industry; in Japan, it was rapid state-directed industrialization that avoided colonization — followed by Japan's own imperial expansion. Comparing these regional experiences reveals that the period's "revolutions" were not a uniform global experience but were unevenly distributed and often produced by the exploitation of the regions that did not benefit from them.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Argumentation: Construct a CCOT thesis that makes a specific evaluative claim about the relationship between the period's changes and continuities. The thesis should not just say that both change and continuity occurred — it should argue something specific about their relationship. Were the changes and continuities independent, or were they causally connected — and if causally connected, how?

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The period c. 1750–1900 produced transformative changes — industrialization, Atlantic revolutions, new nation-states, abolition of chattel slavery, new social classes — and fundamental continuities — patriarchy (women lacking suffrage in most countries in 1900), imperial expansion (European powers controlling 84% of world land by 1900), rural agricultural life for most of humanity, and new forms of coercive labor replacing chattel slavery. A strong CCOT argument explains not just that both change and continuity occurred, but the causal relationship between them: the same industrial technology that produced European prosperity enabled European imperial expansion; democratic ideology proclaimed universal rights while systematically excluding women and colonial subjects.

Check Your Thinking Three Questions — Three AP Skills
  • Q1 Causation Explain how industrial technology caused European imperial expansion in the period c. 1750–1900. Be specific about the technologies and the causal mechanism: how did specific industrial developments (railroads, telegraphs, weapons technology) make imperial control of Africa and Asia possible in ways it had not been in 1750? What does this causal relationship reveal about the connection between industrialization and imperialism?
  • Q2 Continuity and Change Compare the experience of c. 1750–1900 from the perspective of two different world regions: one that industrialized (choose: Britain, Germany, or Japan) and one that was colonized (choose: India, West Africa, or Latin America). For each region, identify one major change and one major continuity during this period. Then explain: were the changes in the industrializing region and the continuities in the colonized region causally connected — did one produce the other?
  • Q3 Argumentation Write a CCOT thesis for the period c. 1750–1900 that makes an evaluative argument about the relationship between the era's changes and continuities. Your thesis should: (1) identify specific changes and continuities; (2) make a claim about their relationship — were they independent or causally connected; and (3) evaluate their overall significance. Avoid the weak "many things changed, but some stayed the same" structure — argue something specific that someone could agree or disagree with.
