01

Contextualization

The World Before Workers Could Organize

In the early decades of industrialization, workers had almost no legal mechanisms to improve their conditions collectively. Trade unions were illegal in Britain under the Combination Acts (1799, 1800), which prohibited workers from organizing to demand higher wages or shorter hours. In the United States, courts routinely ruled that union organizing was a criminal conspiracy in restraint of trade. The legal framework treated the employment relationship as a voluntary contract between free individuals — which meant any collective action by workers to change the terms of that contract was interference with free market principles.

Factory owners could fire organizers, blacklist workers who had joined unions, and call in police or military when workers struck. The political system offered little alternative: in Britain, working-class men did not have the vote until the Reform Acts of 1867 and 1884; in the United States, while working-class white men had formal voting rights, political machines and corporate interests dominated both parties.

Working-class people in the industrial era thus faced a fundamental question: if individual bargaining gave them no power, and the political system offered little, how could they change their conditions?

02

Key Concept

Trade Unions, Strikes, and Collective Power

The labor movement built collective power where individuals had none. The Combination Acts in Britain were repealed in 1824, legalizing trade unions for the first time — though unions remained legally restricted and employers found other ways to suppress them. Craft unions organized skilled workers by trade (carpenters, printers, ironworkers); industrial unions organized all workers in an industry regardless of skill. The American Federation of Labor (AFL, 1886), led by Samuel Gompers, focused on "pure and simple unionism" — collective bargaining for wages, hours, and conditions, without revolutionary politics.

The Great Railroad Strike of 1877 was the first major labor uprising in the United States: railroad workers walked off the job in response to wage cuts, paralyzing rail traffic in multiple states. State militias and federal troops were called in; dozens of workers were killed. The strike was broken but demonstrated both the potential power of organized workers and the government's willingness to use military force against them.

The Haymarket affair (Chicago, 1886) began as a strike for the eight-hour workday; after police opened fire on strikers, someone threw a bomb, killing several officers. Eight anarchist leaders were tried and convicted in a proceeding widely seen as unjust — four were hanged. Haymarket traumatized the American labor movement and was used to associate unionism with radicalism for decades.

The Pullman Strike (1894) — in which railroad workers nationwide refused to handle Pullman Palace Car Company cars in solidarity with striking Pullman workers — was broken when President Cleveland sent federal troops over the objection of the Illinois governor. The strike's leader, Eugene Debs, was imprisoned; after his release he became a socialist. By 1900, the labor movement had achieved some gains — shorter hours in some industries, safety regulations, the legal right to organize in most jurisdictions — but remained politically marginalized in the United States while labor and socialist parties were gaining power in Britain and Europe.

03

Key Concept

Slavery, Cotton, and the Industrial Economy

Abolitionism — the movement to end slavery — was one of the most important reform movements of the industrial era, and it had a direct and complicated relationship with industrial capitalism. The cotton gin (Eli Whitney, 1793) made short-staple cotton profitable, which massively expanded American cotton cultivation — and with it, demand for enslaved labor. By 1850, cotton was the United States' largest export, providing two-thirds of American export earnings. The textile mills of Lancashire and Lowell ran on American cotton — which meant they ran, indirectly, on enslaved labor. The relationship between British and American industrial capitalism and American slavery was not incidental: it was structural.

British abolitionists in the early 19th century argued against slavery partly on moral grounds (the Evangelical Christian tradition) and partly on free-labor economic grounds (that free labor was more productive than enslaved labor). The British Empire abolished slavery in 1833 (effective 1838), largely due to abolitionist organizing, the 1831–32 Baptist War rebellion in Jamaica that demonstrated enslaved people's resistance, and a £20 million payment to slaveholders as compensation.

In the United States, the abolitionist movement intensified through the 1840s–1850s, led by figures including Frederick Douglass (formerly enslaved, the movement's most powerful public voice), William Lloyd Garrison (publisher of The Liberator), and Sojourner Truth. The American Civil War (1861–1865) — which ended with the Thirteenth Amendment abolishing slavery — was, among other things, a conflict between two economic systems: industrial wage labor in the North and slave-based plantation agriculture in the South. The Cotton Famine (1861–1865) — when the Civil War cut off American cotton to British mills — forced the Lancashire textile industry to seek cotton from Egypt and India, restructuring global cotton supply chains in ways that shaped those economies for generations.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Comparison: Compare the British and American abolitionist movements. Both argued against slavery — but they operated in different contexts (a slaveholding empire vs. a slaveholding republic), used different tactics, and achieved abolition through different means. What does comparing these two movements reveal about the relationship between abolitionism and political power?

04

Key Concept

Education, Public Health, and the Reform Impulse

Industrialization generated a broad reform impulse that extended beyond labor and abolitionism. Public education reform produced mandatory schooling laws: Britain's Education Act 1870 (the Forster Act) established a system of public elementary schools for children who were not served by existing church schools; subsequent legislation made attendance compulsory and free. In the United States, mandatory schooling laws spread through the states between 1852 (Massachusetts) and 1918, when the last state (Mississippi) required school attendance. The argument for mandatory education combined humanitarian concern (children working in factories rather than learning) with industrial-era economic logic: an industrial economy needed workers who could read instructions, do arithmetic, and follow written rules.

Edwin Chadwick's Report on the Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population (1842) documented the direct connection between poverty, poor sanitation, and disease. The germ theory of disease (Pasteur and Koch, 1860s–1870s) provided scientific foundation for sanitation reform. London's Great Stink 1858 — when the Thames, used as an open sewer, became so foul that Parliament was nearly unable to function — directly produced investment in Joseph Bazalgette's sewer system. Public sewer systems, clean water supplies, and building codes spread through industrial cities through the latter half of the 19th century, dramatically reducing death rates from cholera, typhoid, and other waterborne diseases.

Temperance — the movement to limit or prohibit alcohol consumption — was one of the largest mass movements of the industrial era, especially in the United States. Temperance advocates argued that alcohol destroyed working-class family life and economic stability; the WCTU (Women's Christian Temperance Union, founded 1874) was the largest women's organization in 19th-century America. The temperance movement reflected the moral reform impulse of the era: industrialization had created social dislocations that seemed to require moral as well as structural responses.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Workers built collective power through trade unions and strikes, achieving some reforms while facing violent repression (Haymarket, Pullman); abolitionism challenged slavery's structural connection to the industrial cotton economy and achieved legal abolition in Britain (1833) and the United States (1865); and reform movements transformed education, public health, and social life — partly from humanitarian concern and partly because industrial capitalism required healthy, literate, orderly workers.
