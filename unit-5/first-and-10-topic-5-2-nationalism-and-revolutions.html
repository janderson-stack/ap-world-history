First & 10 Reading

The Revolutionary Atlantic

Between 1776 and 1825, a series of connected revolutions transformed the Atlantic world — beginning in British North America, spreading to France, exploding in Haiti, and sweeping through Spanish and Portuguese America. All of these revolutions drew on Enlightenment ideas about natural rights and popular sovereignty. But they applied those ideas to very different societies with very different results — and the Haitian Revolution, in which enslaved people claimed those rights for themselves, revealed the deepest contradiction at the heart of the revolutionary Atlantic.

Contextualization

What Made Atlantic Revolutions Possible

Atlantic Revolutions did not emerge from ideas alone — they emerged from specific structural conditions that made revolution both possible and necessary for particular groups.

In British North America, the Seven Years War (1756–1763) had both demonstrated the colonists' military capacity and created a debt crisis that led Britain to tax its American colonies for the first time. "No taxation without representation" was not just a slogan — it was a constitutional argument that drew directly on Enlightenment ideas about government by consent. When the British Parliament refused to treat American colonists as full subjects with parliamentary representation, it handed American revolutionaries both a grievance and a principle.

In France, structural conditions were even more acute: a nearly bankrupt state, a rigid aristocratic tax exemption, food shortages, and a king (Louis XVI) who had bankrupted France partly by funding the American Revolution. The fiscal crisis forced Louis to convene the Estates-General in 1789 — an institution that had not met since 1614 — and the unresolved tensions between the Third Estate (everyone who was not noble or clergy) and the privileged orders exploded into revolution.

In Saint-Domingue (Haiti), the context was the most extreme: the most profitable colony in the world, built entirely on the labor of 500,000 enslaved people, governed by a small class of white planters who wanted the benefits of French revolutionary liberty — for themselves — while maintaining slavery for everyone else.

AP Thinking — Contextualization

Atlantic Revolutions were shaped by specific structural conditions — fiscal crisis, war, colonial taxation, and the social contradictions of slave-owning revolutionary societies. Enlightenment ideas provided the language of revolution; structural conditions provided the trigger. Understanding both is essential for causation arguments about why revolutions happened when they did.

2

Key Concept

American and French Revolutions — Enlightenment in Practice

The American Revolution (1776) was the first successful Atlantic Revolution — the first time Enlightenment ideas about natural rights and popular sovereignty were translated into a working political state. The Declaration of Independence (1776) explicitly invoked Lockean natural rights ("life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness") and the social contract ("governments are instituted among men, deriving their just powers from the consent of the governed"). The Constitution (1787) applied Montesquieu's separation of powers. The Bill of Rights protected individual liberties against state power.

But the American Revolution also had profound limits: it preserved slavery, restricted voting to property-owning white men, and dispossessed indigenous peoples from their lands. Thomas Jefferson — the primary author of the Declaration's assertion that "all men are created equal" — enslaved over 600 people during his lifetime. The Revolution was radical within its intended scope (independence from Britain, republican government) and conservative in its exclusions (slavery, gender, indigenous rights).

The French Revolution (1789) was more radical in its initial promises and more violent in its execution. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (1789) declared universal natural rights and popular sovereignty. The Revolution abolished feudalism, the aristocratic tax exemption, and (temporarily) the monarchy. The radical phase — the Terror (1793–1794) — executed thousands, including Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette, in the name of the general will. The Revolution also abolished slavery in French colonies in 1794 — briefly making France the most radical Atlantic revolutionary power.

AP Thinking — Causation

Both revolutions were caused by a combination of Enlightenment ideas AND specific structural conditions. The American Revolution required both Locke's natural rights theory AND British taxation policy after the Seven Years War. The French Revolution required both Rousseau's popular sovereignty AND the near-bankruptcy of the French state. Neither ideas alone nor structural conditions alone explain why revolutions happened when they did — the AP causation skill requires identifying both.

3

Key Concept

The Haitian Revolution — The Most Radical Atlantic Revolution

The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) was the most radical of the Atlantic Revolutions — the only one that was simultaneously a revolution against colonialism, a revolution against slavery, and a revolution for the legal equality of all people regardless of race. It was led by enslaved and formerly enslaved people who explicitly claimed the rights that French and American revolutionaries said belonged to all humans — but refused to extend to them.

The revolution began in August 1791 with a slave uprising led partly by the Vodou ceremony at Bois Caïman, where enslaved people made a covenant to fight for freedom. Toussaint Louverture — a formerly enslaved man of exceptional military and political genius — emerged as the dominant leader. He built a disciplined army that defeated the Spanish, the British (who invaded hoping to take the colony for themselves), and eventually the French army sent by Napoleon Bonaparte to reimpose slavery in 1802.

The context of hypocrisy: when the French Revolution abolished slavery in 1794, it was partly because Toussaint had allied with France after Spain offered him freedom in exchange for military service. When Napoleon sent 50,000 soldiers to reimpose slavery in 1802, the Haitian revolutionary forces defeated them (partly through yellow fever, which decimated the French army). Haiti declared independence on January 1, 1804 — the first Black republic in the Western Hemisphere and only the second independent state in the Americas.

The significance: Haiti's independence was a direct application of Enlightenment principles that European and American revolutionaries refused to apply. France demanded enormous reparations (150 million francs) from Haiti as the price of diplomatic recognition — money that Haiti did not finish paying until 1947, structurally impoverishing the country. The Haitian Revolution terrified slaveholders throughout the Atlantic world, leading to tighter restrictions on enslaved people and the suppression of information about Haiti in slave-holding societies.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Comparing the Haitian Revolution to the American Revolution reveals the most important pattern in Atlantic Revolutions: every revolution claimed universal rights while simultaneously defining some group as outside those rights. The American Revolution excluded the enslaved; the French Revolution excluded women (briefly) and eventually tried to exclude the enslaved; the Haitian Revolution explicitly included everyone — and was treated as a threat by every other Atlantic revolutionary state as a result. The comparison reveals that universalism was, in practice, always partial.

4

Key Concept

Latin American Independence and Nationalism

Latin American independence movements (1810–1825) completed the Atlantic Revolutionary wave. They were largely led by creoles — people of European descent born in the Americas — who resented Spanish-born peninsulares monopolizing senior colonial offices, resented Enlightenment-inspired regulations on colonial trade, and were inspired by the American and French examples.

Miguel Hidalgo launched Mexico's independence movement in 1810 with his "Grito de Dolores" — a call to revolt that drew heavily on indigenous and mestizo populations, making it more socially radical than most other Latin American independence movements. Simón Bolívar, the "Liberator," led independence movements across Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Bolivia, explicitly invoking Enlightenment ideas about natural rights and republican government.

But Latin American independence produced conservative outcomes in most cases: creole elites replaced peninsulares at the top of the social hierarchy, but the casta system, the Catholic Church's power, and the plantation economy all continued. Indigenous peoples and enslaved Africans gained formal legal equality in most new constitutions — but formal equality and social reality diverged sharply.

Nationalism — the ideology that emerged from Atlantic Revolutionary movements — held that people sharing a common culture, language, history, or ethnicity should have their own self-governing state. Nationalism was initially linked to liberal and democratic ideals (the "right of peoples to self-determination"); by the late 19th century, it had become deeply intertwined with ethnic exclusion, imperialism, and militarism. For AP World History, nationalism is important primarily as a destabilizing force in multiethnic empires (Ottoman, Habsburg, Russian) throughout the 19th century.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Did Atlantic Revolutions live up to their Enlightenment ideals? A strong argument requires specifics: the American Revolution created a functioning republic (lived up to one ideal) while preserving slavery (failed catastrophically on another). The Haitian Revolution fully applied natural rights — and paid an enormous economic price for it. Latin American independence transferred power from one elite to another without fundamentally challenging social hierarchy. The strongest answer acknowledges that "living up to ideals" is not binary — all revolutions partially succeeded and partially failed, in ways that reflect whose interests they were designed to serve.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The Atlantic Revolutions (1776–1825) applied Enlightenment ideas to create new political systems — but applied them very differently depending on who was doing the applying. The American Revolution (1776) created the first Enlightenment-based republic, drew on Locke and Montesquieu, but preserved slavery and restricted rights to white property-owning men. The French Revolution (1789) was more radical — abolishing feudalism, declaring universal rights, briefly abolishing slavery — but descended into the Terror and Napoleon's authoritarian rule. The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804) was the most radical: enslaved people claiming universal rights that white revolutionaries refused to extend to them, creating the first Black republic in the Western Hemisphere. Latin American independence (1810–1825) followed — led by creoles who replaced Spanish-born elites without fundamentally transforming social hierarchies. From these revolutions emerged nationalism — the idea that shared culture or history should form the basis for political self-determination — a powerful new ideology that would destabilize multiethnic empires for the next century.
