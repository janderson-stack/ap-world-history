Map Notes

Two Worlds, One Region

Topic 1.3 requires students to think across several connected zones: South Asia, mainland Southeast Asia, and maritime Southeast Asia. These two maps focus on Southeast Asia’s mainland and maritime patterns, while the regional context below connects those patterns back to South Asia’s Delhi Sultanate and Vijayanagara Empire.

The Khmer Empire left map dominated mainland Southeast Asia from its hydraulic capital at Angkor in what is now Cambodia. It was a land-based empire built on rice agriculture, river systems, water management, and Hindu-Buddhist kingship. The Majapahit Empire right map was a maritime power centered on Java that influenced island and strait routes in the Indonesian archipelago. It was built on spice trade, naval power, and commercial networks.

Read these maps together: one shows what land power looked like in this region; the other shows what sea power looked like. Both are essential for understanding how South and Southeast Asia connected to the wider world.

Map Key & Regional Context

Delhi Sultanate South AsiaThe first major Islamic state on the Indian subcontinent, established in 1206 CE. It controlled much of northern India but never unified the entire subcontinent; Hindu kingdoms such as Vijayanagara held power in the south.
Vijayanagara Empire South IndiaA major Hindu kingdom established in 1336 in the southern Deccan. It positioned itself as a defender of Hindu tradition against northern Islamic expansion and became a sophisticated center of art, architecture, and commerce.
Khmer Empire Mainland Southeast AsiaCentered at Angkor in modern Cambodia. At its peak around 1200 CE, it controlled much of mainland Southeast Asia. It built one of the largest and most sophisticated pre-industrial hydraulic urban systems in the world and shifted from Hindu to Buddhist religious orientation over time.
Majapahit Empire Maritime Southeast AsiaA Javanese maritime empire at its height in the fourteenth century. It influenced maritime Southeast Asian trade networks and competed for power across the island world, including routes connected to the Strait of Malacca. It was a Hindu-Buddhist state in a region where Islam would later become increasingly influential through maritime trade.
Strait of MalaccaThe narrow chokepoint between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra through which much of the maritime trade between the Indian Ocean and South China Sea passed. Influence over this strait meant access to revenue, merchants, and long-distance exchange.
Monsoon Wind RoutesThe seasonal wind systems that reversed direction twice yearly, carrying ships from Arabia and India toward Southeast Asia in one season and back in another. These predictable winds helped make the Indian Ocean one of the most commercially active maritime systems in the medieval world.

Geographic Takeaway

This region’s diversity — Hindu, Buddhist, and Islamic kingdoms within a few hundred miles of one another — was not an accident. It was the product of geography: the monsoon winds made the Indian Ocean a crossroads, and crossroads produce cultural mixing, not cultural uniformity.

Map Question

How did geography shape the religious and political diversity of South and Southeast Asia?

AP Skill: Causation & Comparison

Using both maps and what you know about this topic, explain how the geographic features of South and Southeast Asia — the Indian Ocean, the Strait of Malacca, the monsoon winds, and the division between mainland and maritime zones — helped cause the remarkable religious and cultural diversity of this region between 1200 and 1450.

  • Why does the Strait of Malacca matter so much to states and merchants in this region?
  • How did the monsoon winds shape who came to Southeast Asia and why?
  • Why is Angkor Khmer a land-based empire while Majapahit is a maritime empire? What does geography explain?
  • Islam reached much of Southeast Asia by sea, not primarily by conquest. What does the map tell you about why?
  • Compare the two maps: which state had more concentrated power? Which had broader reach? Why?

Draft Response

Write 3 to 5 sentences. Reference both maps — mainland and maritime — and use at least one specific geographic feature, such as a strait, a wind system, or a river valley, to explain causation. Focus on how geography helped cause diversity, not just what the diversity was.
