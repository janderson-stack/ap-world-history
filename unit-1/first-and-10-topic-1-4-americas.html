Empire Without Horses

How the Mexica and Inca built powerful American states before 1450 by organizing tribute, labor, agriculture, roads, and religion in ways that developed independently from Afro-Eurasian political systems.

A Separate World

The civilizations of the Americas developed separately from Afro-Eurasian trade networks for thousands of years. The Mexica and Inca did not know Song China existed, and Song China did not know the Americas existed. Yet powerful states still emerged.

This matters because it reveals something important about human societies: when populations grow, agriculture expands, and leaders organize labor and resources, complex civilizations tend to develop even in very different environments.

AP Contextualization: Topic 1.4 is important because it asks students to compare independent development in the Americas with state-building across Afro-Eurasia.

The Mexica and Tenochtitlan

By the fifteenth century, the Mexica had transformed themselves into the dominant military power in central Mexico. Their capital, Tenochtitlan, stood on an island in Lake Texcoco connected by broad causeways and canals.

The Mexica built a tribute empire. Conquered peoples supplied food, cloth, labor, military goods, and luxury items to the capital. Their agricultural system, including chinampas, helped sustain one of the largest cities in the Americas.

“The Mexica did not simply conquer territory. They organized the movement of tribute, food, labor, and belief.”
AP Comparison: The Mexica tribute system can be compared to other empires in AP World that extracted resources from conquered peoples while allowing local rulers to remain in place.

The Inca and the Andes

The Inca Empire stretched across the Andes Mountains of South America. Geography shaped every part of Inca administration. Roads, bridges, relay runners, and storehouses helped the state control enormous distances.

Instead of depending primarily on tribute payments, the Inca relied heavily on mit'a labor. Communities owed work to the state. Labor built roads, supported armies, farmed state lands, and maintained infrastructure.

Administrative information was recorded through quipu, a sophisticated knotted-cord system used to organize state information.

“The Inca turned mountains from barriers into systems of communication and control.”

Other American Civilizations

The Americas were also home to the Maya, who developed sophisticated mathematics, astronomy, and writing systems, and to Cahokia, the largest pre-Columbian city north of Mexico.

These societies demonstrate that political hierarchy, urbanization, monumental architecture, and agricultural surplus emerged across many regions of the Americas before European arrival.

AP Continuity & Change: Maya political systems changed over time, but Maya culture and people continued long after the decline of Classic Period city-states.

Why This Matters

The Americas topic matters because it expands the meaning of civilization beyond Afro-Eurasia. The Mexica and Inca solved many of the same state-building problems faced by rulers elsewhere in the world: how to organize labor, move resources, justify authority, and govern large populations.

“Different tools. Same problem: building power.”

Understanding these civilizations before 1492 is essential because the arrival of Europeans would dramatically reshape the Americas in the centuries that followed.
