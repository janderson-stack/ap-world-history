Map Notes

Reading the World of Islam

This map reveals the single most important geographic fact about Dar al-Islam: it was not a place. It was a network. No single empire ever controlled all of the territory shown here. What connected these lands was not a government — it was a shared faith, a common legal tradition, a sacred language, and the trade routes that Muslim merchants had built across three continents.

Between 1200 and 1450, this vast world experienced both catastrophic destruction and remarkable resilience. The Mongol invasion shattered the political center at Baghdad in 1258 — but the network survived, because networks do not have a single center to destroy.

Map Key

Core Islamic Lands (Heartland) Arabia, Persia, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Levant — the original territory of the Abbasid Caliphate and the geographic and cultural center of Dar al-Islam before the Mongol destruction of Baghdad in 1258.
Expansion Zones (1200–1450) Sub-Saharan West Africa (Mali Empire), East Africa's Swahili Coast, the Indian subcontinent (Delhi Sultanate), and maritime Southeast Asia — regions drawn into Dar al-Islam primarily through trade and Sufi missionary activity, not conquest.
Mongol Invasion Path The Mongol armies swept westward from Central Asia, destroying Baghdad (1258) and threatening to unravel the entire Islamic heartland. Their advance was halted by the Mamluk Sultanate at the Battle of Ain Jalut (1260) in Palestine.
Post-Mongol Power Centers Mamluk Egypt, the Delhi Sultanate, and the emerging Ottoman state in Anatolia — the new anchors of Islamic political power after the fall of Baghdad. Each became a regional center of scholarship, law, and trade.
Indian Ocean Trade Network The maritime routes connecting East Africa, Arabia, India, and Southeast Asia — dominated by Muslim merchants and driven by the predictable monsoon winds. This network survived the Mongol destruction intact.

Geographic Takeaway

Dar al-Islam stretched across more territory than any other civilization in 1200 CE — but it held together not through military force but through shared faith, trade, and scholarship. That is why it could survive the destruction of its political center.

AP Skill: Causation & Contextualization

Looking at the map, Dar al-Islam stretched from West Africa to Southeast Asia — a distance of more than 8,000 miles. No single government ever controlled all of this territory. Using the map and what you know about this topic, explain what forces actually connected these distant regions into a single civilization.

  • If no army controlled all of Dar al-Islam, what did hold it together?
  • Why would a Muslim merchant from Morocco be trusted in a market in India?
  • The Mongols destroyed Baghdad — the political capital. Why did Dar al-Islam survive?
  • What does the Indian Ocean trade route's location tell you about how Islam spread into Southeast Asia?
  • Which regions on the map were likely converted by armies — and which by merchants or missionaries?

Draft Response

Write 3–5 sentences. Use at least one specific geographic reference from the map. Focus on what connected these distant regions — and why those connections proved more durable than political control.
