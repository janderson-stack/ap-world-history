Human Activity, Environmental Change, and the Climate Debate

How human activity — industrialization, agriculture, urbanization, and fossil fuel use — degraded the environment, intensified resource competition, and sparked debates about climate change from 1900 to the present.

Causation — KC-6.1.II.A

Degrading the Environment: Deforestation, Desertification, Air Quality, and Freshwater

Topic 9.2 traced how the environment shaped human populations through disease. Topic 9.3 reverses the direction: here, human activity shapes the environment. The CED states that human activity "contributed to deforestation, desertification, a decline in air quality, and increased consumption of the world's supply of fresh water." Each of these is a cause-and-effect story — a specific human activity producing a measurable environmental change.

Deforestation accelerated dramatically after 1900. In the Amazon basin, vast tracts of tropical rainforest were cleared for cattle ranching, soybean farming, and logging. In Southeast Asia, tropical forests were destroyed for palm oil plantations. The causes were economic: global demand for agricultural products, timber, and land. The effects were environmental: loss of biodiversity, disruption of carbon cycles that regulate the atmosphere, displacement of indigenous communities, and soil degradation that made the cleared land increasingly unproductive.

Desertification — the expansion of desert conditions into previously arable land — intensified across the Sahel region of sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and northern China. Overgrazing, deforestation, and intensive agriculture stripped the soil of vegetation cover, leaving it exposed to wind erosion. The cause was human land use; the effect was a shrinking supply of farmable land in regions already under population pressure, pushing communities into competition over what remained.

A decline in air quality accompanied industrialization and urbanization worldwide. Coal-burning factories and power plants produced thick smog that blanketed cities. London's Great Smog of 1952 — a week-long fog of coal smoke and industrial pollution — killed thousands and led directly to clean-air legislation. Los Angeles became synonymous with automobile-generated smog. Across rapidly industrializing nations, air pollution became a public health crisis caused by the same fossil fuel combustion that powered economic growth.

Increased consumption of the world's supply of fresh water strained rivers, lakes, and underground aquifers. Agricultural irrigation consumed the largest share — the Green Revolution's high-yield crops (Topic 9.1) required massive water inputs. Industrial processes and growing urban populations added further demand. The Aral Sea shrank dramatically after Soviet-era irrigation projects diverted its feeder rivers to cotton fields. Aquifer depletion in India, the American Great Plains, and northern China threatened the long-term viability of food production in those regions.

AP Skill — Causation

KC-6.1.II.A names four forms of degradation in a single sentence. On the AP exam, you may be asked to explain the cause of one or more (what human activity produced it) and the effect (what environmental or social consequence followed). Naming the degradation is not enough — you must trace the causal chain.

Causation — KC-6.1.II.A

Competition Over Scarce Resources

The second half of KC-6.1.II.A makes a claim about consequences: as environmental degradation reduced the supply of arable land, clean water, and other resources, "humans competed over these and other resources more intensely than ever before." Resource competition was not new — societies had fought over land and water for millennia. But the scale and intensity of competition after 1900 reflected the combined pressure of population growth, industrialization, and environmental decline.

Water scarcity became a source of tension between nations sharing river systems. Disputes over the Nile, the Jordan, the Tigris–Euphrates, and the Mekong involved competing agricultural, industrial, and urban demands. Dam-building by upstream nations reduced flow to downstream populations, creating diplomatic confrontation and, in some cases, threats of military action. The cause was not simply that there was less water — it was that human activity (irrigation, urbanization, industrial use) was consuming more while environmental change was reducing supply.

Land competition intensified as desertification and deforestation shrank the supply of productive agricultural land. In the Sahel, competition between pastoralist herders and settled farmers over shrinking grazing and farming land contributed to social tensions and, in some regions, violent conflict. In Southeast Asia, commercial deforestation displaced indigenous communities and created disputes over land rights that remain unresolved.

Fisheries, minerals, and energy resources also became objects of intensified competition. Overfishing depleted ocean stocks, leading to international disputes over fishing rights and maritime boundaries. Competition for oil, natural gas, and minerals shaped geopolitics throughout the 20th and 21st centuries — resource competition driven not by environmental degradation alone, but by rising demand from industrializing economies worldwide.

AP Skill — Causation

The CED phrase "more intensely than ever before" is a change-over-time signal. It means the AP exam expects you to explain not just that competition existed, but that it escalated — and to explain what caused the escalation (environmental degradation + population growth + industrial demand).

3

Causation — KC-6.1.II.B

The Climate Debate: Greenhouse Gases, Pollutants, and International Responses

The environmental degradation described in Sections 1 and 2 had local and regional effects. But one form of environmental change became global: the accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. Throughout the 20th century, the burning of coal, oil, and natural gas released carbon dioxide, methane, and other heat-trapping gases at an accelerating rate. These pollutants accumulated in the atmosphere and contributed to a measurable rise in global average temperatures.

By the late 20th century, a scientific consensus emerged linking human activity — particularly fossil fuel combustion — to climate change. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), established in 1988, synthesized research from thousands of scientists worldwide. Its reports concluded that human-caused greenhouse gas emissions were the dominant cause of observed warming.

This scientific consensus "contributed to debates about the nature and causes of climate change." The debates were not only scientific — they were economic and political. Nations whose economies depended on fossil fuel extraction resisted emissions limits. Developing nations argued that wealthy industrialized countries had produced most historical emissions and should bear the greatest responsibility for reducing them. Industries funded campaigns questioning the science. The debate was about causes (was human activity responsible?), effects (how severe would the consequences be?), and responsibility (who should pay?).

International responses reflected these tensions. The Kyoto Protocol (1997) set binding emissions-reduction targets for industrialized nations but exempted developing countries. The United States signed but never ratified it. The Paris Agreement (2015) adopted a different framework: voluntary national pledges from nearly all participating nations. It was broader in participation but weaker in enforcement. Critics from both sides questioned whether voluntary commitments would be sufficient — and whether the agreement fairly distributed the burden between nations that had caused most emissions and those that were most vulnerable to climate effects.

AP Skill — Contextualization

KC-6.1.II.B says emissions "contributed to debates." On the AP exam, explaining the debate is as important as explaining the science. Why was there disagreement? Because causes (who produced the emissions), effects (who suffers the consequences), and costs (who pays for the transition) were unevenly distributed across nations. That is contextualization — explaining why the debate existed, not just that it did.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Topic 9.3 reverses 9.2's direction: here, human activity shapes the environment. Industrialization, agriculture, and urbanization caused deforestation, desertification, air pollution, and freshwater depletion. That degradation intensified competition over scarce resources. And the release of greenhouse gases sparked global debates about the causes and effects of climate change — debates shaped by who caused the problem and who bore the cost. That is the ENV thematic focus from the human-impact side: populations shape their environments, and the consequences reshape politics.
