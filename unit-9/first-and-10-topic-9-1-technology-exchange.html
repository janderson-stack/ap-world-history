Advances in Technology and Exchange

Causation — KC-6.1.I.A

Distance Collapsed: Communication and Transportation

In 1900, a message from London to Calcutta took days by telegraph and weeks by letter. A journey between the two cities took months by ship. By the end of the 20th century, the same message could arrive in milliseconds, and the same journey could be completed in hours. The technologies that produced this transformation — radio, cellular communication, the internet, air travel, and shipping containers — did not merely speed up old processes. They redefined what "distance" meant.

Radio communication emerged as a mass medium by the 1920s. For the first time, governments, advertisers, and entertainers could reach millions of people simultaneously. Radio collapsed the distance between the state and the citizen — and between the propagandist and the audience. By mid-century, cellular communication began to extend personal connectivity beyond fixed lines, and by the 1990s the internet created a global information network that made instantaneous communication, commerce, and political organizing possible from nearly anywhere on earth.

Transportation changed just as dramatically. Commercial air travel, which became routine after the 1950s, turned journeys that had once taken weeks into hours — making international business, tourism, and migration vastly easier. But the quieter revolution may have been the shipping container. Standardized in the 1960s by Malcom McLean, the container cut shipping costs by over 90%, reduced loading times from days to hours, and enabled the globalized supply chains that define modern trade. A metal box that no one celebrated at the time quietly made the global economy possible.

AP Skill — Causation

The CED states that new modes of communication and transportation "reduced the problem of geographic distance." Notice the phrasing: not "eliminated" distance, but "reduced the problem" of it. The AP exam rewards students who explain how a technology changed a relationship — not just that it did.

2

Causation — KC-6.1.I.D

Energy Technologies: Petroleum and Nuclear Power

The 20th century ran on petroleum. The internal combustion engine, powered by oil, reshaped transportation, industry, agriculture, and urban form across the globe. Oil became the world's most strategically important commodity — nations fought wars over it, built alliances around it, and organized their economies to secure its supply. Petroleum raised productivity and increased the production of material goods on a scale that previous energy sources (coal, water, animal power) could not match.

Nuclear power emerged as a mid-century alternative. Its promise was enormous: abundant electricity from a compact fuel source, without the carbon emissions of fossil fuels. By the 1970s, nuclear plants provided significant shares of electricity in France, Japan, the Soviet Union, and the United States. But nuclear technology carried catastrophic risks. The Chernobyl disaster (1986) in the Soviet Union contaminated large areas of Ukraine and Belarus and exposed systemic safety failures. The Fukushima disaster (2011) in Japan demonstrated that even technologically advanced nations faced nuclear risks from natural disasters.

Energy technologies illustrate the TEC thematic focus precisely: petroleum and nuclear power raised productivity and increased material goods (intended), but created strategic dependence, environmental damage, and catastrophic safety risks (unintended).

AP Skill — Causation

When the CED says energy technologies "raised productivity and increased the production of material goods," it is describing an intended consequence. The AP exam often asks students to pair intended consequences with unintended ones — Chernobyl and Fukushima are usable evidence for the unintended side.

Causation — KC-6.1.I.B

The Green Revolution: Feeding a Growing World

By the early 1960s, global population was growing faster than food production. Experts predicted massive famines in Asia and Africa within a decade. The response was the Green Revolution — a package of high-yield crop varieties, chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and expanded irrigation that transformed agriculture across the developing world.

The results were dramatic. In India, the mass adoption of Mexican dwarf wheat (developed by agronomist Norman Borlaug) helped avert predicted famines. In Mexico, similar programs had already demonstrated that yields could double or triple with the new seeds and inputs. Commercial agriculture increased productivity and sustained the earth's growing population — global food production outpaced population growth for the first time in history.

But the Green Revolution also generated profound unintended consequences. High-yield varieties required chemical fertilizers and controlled irrigation — inputs that small farmers often could not afford. Commercial agriculture squeezed smallholders out of competitive markets. Chemically and genetically modified monocultures reduced crop diversity and increased vulnerability to blight. Soil degradation, water depletion, and chemical runoff became widespread environmental problems. The technology that saved millions from famine also restructured rural economies in ways its designers had not anticipated.

AP Skill — Causation

The CED states that the Green Revolution and commercial agriculture "increased productivity and sustained the earth's growing population as it spread chemically and genetically modified forms of agriculture." The final clause — "as it spread chemically and genetically modified forms of agriculture" — is not a positive or negative judgment. It is a factual description of the mechanism. The AP exam may ask you to explain the consequences of that mechanism.

Causation — KC-6.1.I.C

Medical Innovations: Vaccines, Antibiotics, and Longer Lives

Vaccines against polio, measles, smallpox, and other diseases dramatically reduced childhood mortality across the 20th century. The World Health Organization declared smallpox eradicated in 1980 — the first human disease eliminated by deliberate global effort. Antibiotics, beginning with penicillin in the 1940s, transformed the treatment of bacterial infections, saved millions of lives, and made modern surgery possible.

The intended consequence was clear: medical innovations increased the ability of humans to survive and live longer lives. Falling infant mortality and rising life expectancy contributed to rapid population growth, particularly in Asia and Africa.

But medical innovations also spread unevenly. Wealthier nations achieved near-universal vaccination and antibiotic access decades before many developing nations. The result was persistent global disparities in life expectancy — a gap that narrowed over the century but never closed entirely. And the population growth that medical advances enabled created new pressures on resources, infrastructure, and governance that shaped the rest of the 20th century.

AP Skill — Continuity and Change Over Time

Medical innovations changed survival rates and life expectancy (change), but global disparities in access persisted throughout the century (continuity). The AP exam rewards students who can hold both in the same argument.

5

Causation — KC-6.1.III.B

Reproductive Technology and Demographic Change

More effective forms of birth control — including the oral contraceptive pill, approved in the United States in 1960 — gave women greater control over fertility for the first time in history. The ability to delay or prevent pregnancy transformed reproductive practices: women could pursue education, enter the workforce, and plan family size in ways that previous generations could not.

The demographic consequences were sweeping. Declining rates of fertility spread across much of the world, particularly in industrialized nations and urban areas. Total fertility rates fell below replacement level in much of Europe and East Asia by the late 20th century. The implications included aging populations, shifts in the global distribution of working-age labor, and political debates about immigration, pension systems, and economic growth.

Birth control is often studied alongside medical innovations, but the CED treats it separately (KC-6.1.III.B) because its primary effect was not on survival but on fertility — on how many children were born, not on how many survived. Together, medical innovations (more people surviving) and birth control (fewer children born) reshaped the world's demographic profile across the 20th century.

AP Skill — Causation

The CED states that more effective birth control "gave women greater control over fertility, transformed reproductive practices, and contributed to declining rates of fertility in much of the world." Notice three distinct effects in one sentence — control, transformation, decline. The AP exam may ask you to explain any one of them with evidence.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Every technology in Topic 9.1 carries both intended and unintended consequences. Radio connected people — and enabled propaganda. The Green Revolution fed billions — and degraded soil. Vaccines saved lives — and contributed to population pressures. Birth control gave women control over fertility — and reshaped entire demographic structures. The TEC thematic focus is the through-line: technology shapes human development in ways its creators intended, and in ways they did not.
