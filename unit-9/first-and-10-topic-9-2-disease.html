Disease, Environment, and Population

How environmental factors — sanitation, density, climate, urbanization, and travel networks — shaped disease patterns and affected human populations from 1900 to the present.

Diseases of Poverty: Malaria, Tuberculosis, and Cholera

In the 20th century, medical science advanced faster than at any point in human history. Vaccines eradicated smallpox. Antibiotics conquered bacterial infections that had killed millions. Life expectancy climbed across every region. Yet diseases associated with poverty — malaria, tuberculosis, and cholera — persisted. They persisted not because medicine failed, but because the environmental conditions that sustained them remained in place.

Malaria kills through mosquitoes, and mosquitoes breed in standing water. In tropical regions where poverty prevented drainage systems, insecticide programs, and healthcare access, malaria remained one of the world's deadliest diseases throughout the 20th century. Wealthier nations in temperate climates eliminated malaria through environmental management — draining swamps, spraying insecticide, screening windows. The disease persisted where environmental conditions and poverty intersected.

Tuberculosis thrived in crowded, poorly ventilated urban environments — exactly the conditions created by rapid industrialization and urbanization. As cities in Asia, Africa, and Latin America grew explosively in the 20th century, TB found new populations. Drug-resistant strains emerged as treatment programs were interrupted or underfunded, making TB harder and more expensive to treat.

Cholera — a waterborne disease — persisted wherever contaminated water supplies and inadequate sewage systems remained. Major outbreaks continued throughout the 20th and 21st centuries in South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and the Caribbean. The environmental conditions that sustained cholera were as much about infrastructure and poverty as about the bacterium itself.

AP Skill — Causation

The CED states that "diseases associated with poverty persisted." On the AP exam, explaining why they persisted is stronger than simply stating that they did. The environmental conditions — sanitation, density, climate, healthcare access — are the causal mechanism.

Causation — KC-6.1.III.A

Emergent Epidemics: The 1918 Influenza Pandemic, Ebola, and HIV/AIDS

While diseases of poverty persisted, entirely new diseases emerged as epidemics and threats to human populations. These were not ancient killers returning — they were new pathogens spreading along the dense population and travel networks that the modern world created.

The 1918 influenza pandemic killed tens of millions of people worldwide in roughly 18 months — more than World War I itself. The virus spread with devastating speed through crowded military training camps, troop transports, and the rail networks that moved soldiers across continents. Wartime censorship suppressed public health warnings in combatant nations, delaying responses. Cities that implemented early public health interventions — closing schools, banning gatherings, enforcing quarantines — experienced significantly lower death rates than those that delayed. The 1918 pandemic demonstrated a principle that would recur throughout the century: environmental density and population movement determine how fast an epidemic kills.

Ebola first appeared in Central Africa in 1976. For decades it caused isolated outbreaks in rural areas. But the 2014–2016 West African epidemic — concentrated in Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone — showed what happened when Ebola reached dense urban populations with limited healthcare infrastructure. The outbreak killed over 11,000 people and overwhelmed health systems. It also spurred international investment in Ebola vaccine development and emergency response capacity.

HIV/AIDS became one of the defining health crises of the late 20th century. First identified in the early 1980s, HIV spread globally through sexual contact, blood transfusion, and needle-sharing. Sub-Saharan Africa was devastated: in some countries, life expectancy fell by more than 20 years. Millions of children were orphaned. The social disruption was immense. But the crisis also spurred technological and medical advances — antiretroviral drugs transformed HIV from a death sentence into a manageable chronic condition, and global health funding expanded dramatically in response.

AP Skill — Contextualization

The CED states that emergent epidemics sometimes led to "social disruption" and that "these outbreaks spurred technological and medical advances." Both effects are testable. The AP exam may ask you to explain how a specific epidemic caused social disruption AND how it led to advances — holding both consequences in the same argument.

Causation — KC-6.1.III.A

Diseases of Longevity: Heart Disease and Alzheimer's Disease

The CED makes a quiet but important observation: "Some diseases occurred at higher incidence merely because of increased longevity." This is not about new pathogens or environmental failures. It is about a paradox: the same medical advances that allowed people to live longer also shifted the disease burden toward conditions that appear primarily in old age.

Heart disease became the leading cause of death in industrialized nations by the mid-20th century. It was not a new disease — but its prevalence as a mass killer was. Urbanization brought sedentary lifestyles, processed-food diets, and widespread smoking. People who would have died of infectious disease in childhood or middle age now lived long enough for cardiovascular disease to develop. By the late 20th century, heart disease was responsible for more deaths globally than any other single cause.

Alzheimer's disease and other forms of dementia emerged as major public health challenges as populations aged. A condition rarely diagnosed before the 20th century became one of the most common causes of death in wealthy nations — not because it was new, but because people now lived long enough to develop it. The cost of caring for aging populations with dementia strained healthcare systems and family structures across the industrialized world.

The diseases-of-longevity pattern completes the ENV picture: environmental conditions shaped disease at every stage. Poverty sustained malaria and cholera. Density and travel spread influenza and HIV. And the changed environments of industrial modernity — longer lives, different diets, sedentary work — produced a new category of disease that earlier generations had rarely experienced.

AP Skill — Causation

The word "merely" in the CED statement is doing real work. It means that increased longevity alone — without any new pathogen or environmental failure — was sufficient to raise the incidence of these diseases. That is a causal claim worth unpacking on the exam.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Topic 9.2 is about one idea: environmental factors shape disease outcomes. Poverty sustained malaria, TB, and cholera. Density and movement spread the 1918 flu, Ebola, and HIV/AIDS — and those outbreaks spurred medical advances. Longer lives shifted the burden to heart disease and Alzheimer's. The environment shaped the disease; the disease reshaped the population. That is the ENV thematic focus in three acts.
