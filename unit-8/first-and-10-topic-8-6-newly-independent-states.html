Causation — KC-6.2.III.A.i

New Lines, New States: How Colonial Withdrawal Created Countries

When European colonial powers withdrew from their empires — whether through negotiated agreements or military defeat — they left behind something that looked like states: territories with defined boundaries, capitals, administrative buildings, flags, and constitutions. In most cases, these boundaries had been drawn by the colonizers themselves, for administrative purposes, without reference to the ethnic, religious, linguistic, or cultural communities that lived inside them. The CED identifies three illustrative examples of states created through this process of boundary redrawing: Pakistan, Israel, and Cambodia.

Pakistan was created through the Partition of British India in 1947. The Indian Independence Act simultaneously created two dominions — India and Pakistan — dividing the subcontinent along religious lines. The task of drawing the boundary fell to Cyril Radcliffe, a British barrister who had never been to India and completed the work in six weeks. The Radcliffe Line divided Punjab in the west and Bengal in the east, cutting through communities, canals, railways, and market networks that had functioned for generations. Pakistan itself was divided into two non-contiguous territories — West Pakistan and East Pakistan (separated by 1,000 miles of Indian territory) — a geographical reality that would eventually produce a second partition when East Pakistan became Bangladesh in 1971.

Israel was created through the end of the British Mandate for Palestine. Britain had held Palestine under a League of Nations mandate since 1920, caught between competing promises: the 1917 Balfour Declaration had expressed British support for a Jewish homeland in Palestine, while Britain had also made wartime assurances to Arab leaders. As Jewish immigration increased dramatically in the 1930s–1940s — driven by the Holocaust — intercommunal violence between Jewish and Arab communities escalated. Britain turned the question over to the newly formed United Nations. The UN Partition Plan (Resolution 181, 1947) proposed dividing Mandatory Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem under international administration. The State of Israel declared independence on May 14, 1948 — the day the British Mandate formally ended.

Cambodia achieved independence from French Indochina in 1953, through the diplomatic maneuvering of King Norodom Sihanouk. The French Empire in Southeast Asia was already crumbling under Vietnamese military pressure; Cambodia's independence came through negotiation rather than armed struggle. Cambodia's boundaries reflected French colonial administrative divisions of mainland Southeast Asia — borders drawn by France in the 19th and early 20th centuries to define its colonial jurisdiction, not to reflect Cambodian political traditions or ethnic communities.

AP Thinking — Causation

The causal chain is: colonial rule → boundary drawing (by the colonizer) → colonial withdrawal → new state defined by those boundaries. The key AP insight is that the political change (colonial withdrawal) produced a territorial development (new state) — but the boundary that defined the new state had been drawn by someone else, for different purposes, at an earlier time. The new state inherited a political geography it did not create. The consequences of that inheritance define much of postcolonial history.

Causation — KC-6.2.III.A.ii

When the Lines Cut Through People: Conflict and Displacement

The CED is explicit: 'The redrawing of political boundaries in some cases led to conflict as well as population displacement and/or resettlements, including those related to the Partition of India and the creation of the state of Israel.' Both cases produced mass population displacement on a scale that shocked even the planners who set the process in motion.

When the Radcliffe Line was published on August 17, 1947 — two days after Indian independence was proclaimed — it triggered one of the largest forced migrations in history. Muslim families in India moved toward Pakistan; Hindu and Sikh families in Pakistan moved toward India. The routes were simultaneous and overlapping — columns of people heading in opposite directions, through territories where intercommunal violence was erupting as they traveled. An estimated 10–20 million people were displaced across the Punjab and Bengal partition lines. An estimated 200,000 to 2 million people died — in massacres, on trains, in refugee columns on foot. Trains arrived at their destinations carrying massacred passengers instead of live refugees. The violence was worst in Punjab, where organized armed bands of Sikhs, Muslims, and Hindus attacked columns moving in the opposite direction.

The new governments of India and Pakistan each inherited a refugee crisis that overwhelmed their administrative capacity within weeks of independence. The economic disruption was as severe as the human toll: factories lost their workers; farms lost their owners; cities lost the communities that had run their markets and workshops. The partition of Punjab and Bengal was not just a political division — it was the destruction of economic ecosystems that had been integrated for generations.

The 1948 Arab-Israeli War produced its own displacement crisis. The Arab League member states — Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, and others — attacked the newly declared State of Israel on May 15, 1948. In the course of the war, approximately 700,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were expelled from their homes — an event Palestinians call the Nakba (Arabic: 'catastrophe'). These refugees and their descendants — numbering several million today — remain displaced, the majority in refugee camps and communities in Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, the West Bank, and Gaza. The 1948 war also produced a reverse displacement: approximately 850,000 Jews from Arab countries emigrated to Israel between 1948 and 1972, driven by persecution that intensified after Israeli independence.

AP Thinking — Causation

Boundary decisions are demographic events. The Partition of India and the creation of Israel each produced mass displacement because the lines drawn at independence did not match the distribution of people on the ground — communities that had lived on both sides of the line were forced to move to the 'correct' side, or were expelled from the territory claimed by the new state. For AP, explain the mechanism: colonial boundary → independence → population movement → refugee crisis. The boundary is the cause; the displacement is the effect; the violence that accompanied it is the product of the mismatch between the line and the communities it divided.

Continuity and Change — KC-6.3.I.C & KC-6.2.III.B

Building Economies, Keeping Ties: Development and Migration After Independence

Political independence did not automatically produce economic independence. The economies of newly independent states had been structured by colonialism to export raw materials, supply the metropole's industries, and import finished goods. When independence came, governments faced a fundamental question: how to build an economy that would actually serve the people of the new state, not the former colonizer. The CED identifies a clear pattern: in newly independent states after World War II, governments took on a strong role in guiding economic life to promote development (KC-6.3.I.C).

Gamal Abdel Nasser in Egypt nationalized the Suez Canal in 1956 — bringing the canal's revenues under Egyptian state control and using them to fund the Aswan High Dam (built with Soviet technical assistance after the United States withdrew financing). Nasser's Arab socialism model nationalized banks, insurance companies, and major industries. The state became the primary engine of Egyptian economic development.

Indira Gandhi in India nationalized India's major banks in 1969 and pursued state-directed development through five-year plans. India's economy under Gandhi combined state-owned enterprises in heavy industry with private manufacturing under heavy regulation — a model sometimes called the License Raj because of the extensive permits required for private economic activity. Gandhi also launched garibi hatao ('eliminate poverty') as a political program, positioning the state as the agent of economic redistribution.

Julius Nyerere in Tanzania developed ujamaa — 'familyhood' — a distinctly African socialist development model. The 1967 Arusha Declaration committed Tanzania to nationalization of banks and industry and rural development through communal villages. Nyerere positioned Tanzania as explicitly non-aligned, rejecting both Cold War blocs, and sought a development path rooted in African communal values rather than either Western capitalism or Soviet-style communism.

Sirimavo Bandaranaike in Ceylon (Sri Lanka) continued her husband's economic program of nationalization and import substitution. Bandaranaike became the world's first elected female head of government in 1960 — governing through a constitutional system the British colonial administration had established, directing the economic life of a newly independent nation, and shaping its social policies toward Sinhala Buddhist nationalism.

Even as new states built their economies, the connections to former colonizers persisted through migration. South Asians moved to Britain — Commonwealth citizenship rights allowed Pakistanis, Indians, and Bangladeshis to settle in British industrial cities through the 1950s–1970s. Algerians moved to France — Algerian laborers had been recruited into French industry since the 1920s; after independence, the migration continued, with large Algerian communities in Paris, Lyon, and Marseille. Filipinos moved to the United States — the Philippines, a former American colony, sent nurses, domestic workers, and agricultural laborers to California and Hawaii in increasing numbers from the 1960s onward. In every case, migration maintained cultural and economic ties between former colony and former colonizer — remittances flowing home, cultural influence flowing in both directions, labor markets linked across thousands of miles.

AP Thinking — Continuity and Change

LO H asks for both economic changes AND continuities. What changed: who controls the economy. Newly independent governments nationalized key industries and directed economic development — replacing colonial firms with state-owned enterprises and ending the automatic preference for metropolitan interests. What continued: migration corridors and trade dependencies. Former colonial subjects moved to the metropole's cities, sending remittances home; trade patterns often still ran through the former colonizer. Economic independence was harder to achieve than political independence — the structures of the colonial economy persisted even after the colonial flag came down.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Colonial withdrawal produced new states — but those states were defined by boundaries drawn by someone else. Where those boundaries cut through communities, the result was conflict and mass displacement (Partition, Nakba). Where new states gained control of their economies, they typically chose state direction over free markets. And where migration corridors had been built under empire, they continued — keeping former colonies economically tied to former metropoles even after independence.
