First & 10 Reading

End of the Cold War

How U.S. military and technological pressure, a decade of failed war in Afghanistan, and the internal contradictions of Soviet-style communism combined to end a forty-year superpower rivalry — and why a system that had survived Stalin, Korea, and the Cuban Missile Crisis collapsed within six years of Gorbachev's first reform speech.

Causation — KC-6.2.IV.E (Strand 1)

Outspent and Outpaced: U.S. Military and Technological Development

KC-6.2.IV.E identifies three causes of the Cold War's end. The first is 'advances in U.S. military and technological development.' This is a causal claim — not just that the U.S. had a strong military, but that specific advances in American military technology created pressure the Soviet system could not sustain. Understanding this cause requires explaining a mechanism: what specifically did U.S. technological development do that pushed the Soviet system toward crisis?

The Reagan defense buildup of the 1980s was part of a deliberate strategy to escalate the arms competition in domains where the Soviet economy was structurally weakest. American defense spending rose sharply, and more importantly, it was directed toward microelectronics, precision systems, and computing — technologies where the Soviet command economy chronically lagged. Soviet factories could produce massive quantities of tanks and artillery; they could not keep pace with the American technological revolution in guidance systems, radar, and computers.

On March 23, 1983, Reagan announced the Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI) — a proposed system of space-based missile defenses that, if it worked, would render the Soviet nuclear arsenal obsolete. Critics called it 'Star Wars' and questioned its feasibility. But the Soviet response revealed the seriousness of the threat: if SDI worked, a U.S. first strike could destroy most Soviet missiles, and whatever survived could be shot down by the defense shield. The Soviet deterrent would be neutralized. Counter-SDI measures would require technological investments the Soviet economy could not bear — and Soviet military planners knew their economy could not match American computing and precision manufacturing.

The key mechanism: the U.S. military-technology push did not just require the Soviets to spend more — it required them to spend more in domains they could not compete in effectively. Every ruble spent trying to counter SDI was a ruble not spent on the civilian economy that Soviet citizens were demanding — and the defense burden was already consuming an estimated 15–20% of Soviet GDP (versus the 5–6% officially acknowledged). This structural tension — a technologically outpaced military competing against a country with deeper technological resources — set the stage for the economic and political crisis that followed.

AP Thinking — Causation

The mechanism matters for AP credit. "The U.S. had a strong military" is a fact. "U.S. technological development — specifically SDI and the shift toward microelectronics — created pressure the Soviet economy could not match because Soviet industrial capacity was strongest in heavy manufacturing and weakest in computing, so the arms race moved into terrain where the Soviets were structurally outmatched" is a causal explanation. LO J asks you to explain causes — which means explaining mechanisms, not just listing events.

Causation — KC-6.2.IV.E (Strand 2)

The Soviet Vietnam: Afghanistan, 1979–1989

The second causal strand is 'the Soviet Union's costly and ultimately failed invasion of Afghanistan.' The Soviet invasion began in December 1979 — not as an unprovoked conquest but as an intervention to stabilize a communist government in a country on the Soviet border. The Afghan communist government was collapsing under pressure from Islamic insurgents. Soviet leaders feared that a communist collapse on the Soviet border would send a catastrophic signal to other Soviet clients. What they intended as a quick stabilizing operation became a nine-year war.

The Mujahideen — decentralized Islamic resistance fighters operating in Afghanistan's mountains — proved impossible to defeat with the Soviet military's conventional tactics. The CIA's Operation Cyclone supplied the Mujahideen with weapons, training, and intelligence, including from 1986 the shoulder-fired Stinger anti-aircraft missile that began destroying Soviet helicopters. Soviet forces could hold the cities and major roads; they could not pacify the countryside. By the time of withdrawal, approximately 15,000 Soviet soldiers had been killed and more than 35,000 wounded. The financial cost — estimated at $2–3 billion rubles per year — added to the pressure on a defense budget that was already straining the civilian economy.

The war's domestic consequences extended beyond the budget. Soviet veterans returned disillusioned. Soviet citizens increasingly knew the war was not going well, even though official media did not report it. The gap between official propaganda and visible reality contributed to the erosion of public confidence in the system — exactly the dynamic that Gorbachev's glasnost would later expose and amplify.

Gorbachev ordered withdrawal planning in 1986; the last Soviet troops crossed back into the USSR on February 15, 1989. The withdrawal was both a military necessity and a geopolitical signal. For communist governments in Eastern Europe — and for Eastern European populations — it revealed something that would matter enormously in 1989: the Soviet Union was not omnipotent, and perhaps not willing to pay any price to maintain its positions. The Brezhnev Doctrine — the commitment to military intervention to defend communist governments — was quietly dying with the Afghan withdrawal.

AP Thinking — Causation

Two mechanisms, not one: Afghanistan damaged the Soviet system through (1) direct costs — personnel, money, morale, domestic disillusionment — and (2) signal effects — the withdrawal told Eastern European populations and governments that the Soviet Union would not pay any price to maintain communist rule, enabling the 1989 revolutions. For AP, the signal effect is often underexplained. The withdrawal in February 1989 preceded the Eastern European revolutions by months — its timing and meaning were not coincidental.

Causation — KC-6.2.IV.E (Strand 3)

The System Cracks: Public Discontent, Economic Weakness, and the Collapse

The third causal strand — 'public discontent and economic weakness in communist countries' — addresses the internal dimension of Soviet collapse. By the early 1980s, the Soviet economy faced chronic structural problems: stagnant productivity, endemic consumer goods shortages, and a widening technological gap with Western economies. The system that had produced rapid industrialization in the 1930s–1950s had run out of easy inputs — labor, land, natural resources — and could not develop the mechanisms of innovation and efficiency that market economies used to keep growing.

When Mikhail Gorbachev became General Secretary in March 1985, he inherited this stagnating system and believed it needed fundamental reform to survive. His two signature programs — glasnost ('openness,' a relaxation of censorship and information control) and perestroika ('restructuring,' economic and political reform to increase efficiency) — were designed to modernize Soviet communism, not end it. The unintended consequences of these reforms are central to understanding why the system collapsed.

Glasnost, once initiated, could not be controlled. Open criticism of the Soviet system did not stop at economic inefficiency. It extended to the Stalinist terror, to the history of Soviet nationalities policy, to the real costs of Afghanistan, to the cover-up of the Chernobyl nuclear disaster (April 1986). Nationalist movements in the Baltic states — Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania — found in glasnost the space to organize demands for independence that had been suppressed since the Soviet annexation in 1940. Perestroika, by disrupting existing production relationships before creating new ones, worsened shortages in the short term even as it aimed to improve the system long-term.

In Eastern Europe, Gorbachev's decision not to use military force to defend communist governments — the effective end of the Brezhnev Doctrine — opened the door that 1989 walked through. Poland's Solidarity movement won partially free elections in June. Hungary opened its border with Austria in May. East Germany faced a mass emigration crisis. On November 9, 1989, the Berlin Wall fell — triggered by a miscommunicated press conference. By year's end, communist governments across Eastern Europe had collapsed or been reformed. The Soviet republics began declaring independence. On December 25, 1991, Gorbachev resigned. The Soviet Union dissolved into fifteen states. The Cold War was over.

AP Thinking — Continuity and Change Over Time

The Soviet economic crisis was not new in 1985 — the structural problems had been building since at least the 1970s. What changed was Gorbachev's decision to respond with reform rather than repression. CCOT for LO J: the continuity was chronic economic weakness; the change was glasnost releasing the public discontent that weakness had been generating for decades. The 1989 revolutions and the 1991 dissolution were not sudden — they were the accumulated result of structural weakness intersecting with a reform response that released pressures the system could not absorb.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

KC-6.2.IV.E identifies three causes of the Cold War's end: (1) U.S. military and technological development — specifically SDI and the shift to microelectronics-based military competition — created pressure the Soviet economy was structurally unable to match; (2) the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan became a nine-year failure that drained resources, disillusioned the public, and signaled Soviet limits to Eastern Europe; (3) public discontent and economic weakness in communist countries — exposed and amplified by Gorbachev's glasnost and perestroika — produced the 1989 Eastern European revolutions and the 1991 Soviet dissolution. The AP skill is explaining how these three causes interacted and compounded each other, not just listing them.
