First & 10 Reading

Global Resistance to Established Power Structures

How groups and individuals challenged the wars and power structures of the 20th century — from Gandhi's satyagraha and King's direct action to Pinochet's military terror and Al-Qaeda's transnational attacks — and what distinguishes the three CED categories of reaction.

Causation Comparison

Before You Read

Track three categories as you read: (1) nonviolent challenges to power structures — what was the method and why did it work; (2) militarized-state responses that intensified conflict — how did state power make conflict worse rather than better; (3) movements that targeted civilians — what distinguished these from the other two categories and what political aims did they pursue. The AP skill is distinguishing these analytically, not just listing who belongs where.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to: explain how at least two of the three named individuals (Gandhi, MLK, Mandela) promoted nonviolence as a strategy for political change; explain how at least two CED examples of militarized-state response intensified conflict; and explain what Shining Path and Al-Qaeda were trying to achieve and why civilian targeting was central to their method.

Causation — KC-6.2.V.A

The Nonviolent Challenge: Gandhi, King, and Mandela

KC-6.2.V frames the topic: 'Although conflict dominated much of the 20th century, many individuals and groups—including states—opposed this trend. Some individuals and groups, however, intensified the conflicts.' KC-6.2.V.A then names three individuals who chose the first path — Mohandas Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., and Nelson Mandela — and identifies what distinguished their approach: the promotion of nonviolence as a method for bringing about political change.

Gandhi developed satyagraha — 'truth-force' — not in India but in South Africa between 1906 and 1915. Satyagraha was not passive acceptance of injustice; it was active noncooperation, disciplined and organized, designed to make authority ungovernable without using violence. Gandhi brought this method to India, most famously in the Salt March of 1930 — a 240-mile walk to the sea to make salt illegally, directly defying British colonial tax law. The strategy was built on a specific theory of political change: make the cost of maintaining colonial authority (arresting peaceful marchers, beating protesters in front of the world press) higher than the cost of political concession. The method depended on the opponent's sensitivity to both domestic and international opinion.

Martin Luther King Jr. explicitly built his strategy on Gandhi's method — studying satyagraha, visiting India in 1959, and applying disciplined nonviolence to the American civil rights struggle. The Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–1956) demonstrated that organized economic pressure combined with nonviolent discipline could break segregation. The Birmingham Campaign (1963) — where police turned fire hoses and dogs on peaceful demonstrators — produced images that forced the nation to confront what it was defending. In his Letter from Birmingham Jail, King explained that nonviolent direct action was designed to 'create such a crisis and foster such a tension' that authorities could no longer refuse to negotiate. Nonviolence, for King, was not passivity — it was the deliberate creation of a political crisis through moral witness.

Nelson Mandela and the ANC present a more complicated case. The ANC's Defiance Campaign of 1952 was explicitly Gandhian — noncooperation with apartheid's pass laws. But after the Sharpeville Massacre (March 21, 1960) — where South African police killed 69 protesters — the apartheid state banned the ANC and detained its leadership. Mandela concluded that strict nonviolence could not be maintained as the only strategy, and co-founded Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK) in 1961 — committed to sabotage of infrastructure while explicitly trying to avoid killing people. Mandela was convicted at the Rivonia Trial (1964) and served 27 years before his release in 1990. He negotiated the transition to democracy, won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1993, and became South Africa's first democratically elected President in 1994. The CED places Mandela in KC-6.2.V.A because his overall method and legacy center on political change through moral authority and negotiation — but the ANC's history shows that the line between nonviolent and armed resistance is sometimes contested within movements themselves.

AP Thinking — Causation

Nonviolence works through a specific causal mechanism: the deliberate acceptance of suffering in public forces the opponent to choose between conceding and escalating repression that becomes politically unsustainable. The conditions required are: (1) the opponent must have some sensitivity to public opinion; (2) the movement must maintain discipline; (3) the suffering must be visible to audiences the opponent cares about. Where these conditions were met — colonial India, the American South — the method produced political change. Where they were absent, the method faced severe limits.

Causation — KC-6.2.V.C

States That Answered with Force: Militarized Responses That Intensified Conflict

KC-6.2.V.C identifies a different type of reaction: militaries and militarized states that responded to conflict in ways that further intensified it. The four CED examples span continents and eras but share one dynamic — state power deployed in ways that deepened political divisions, killed civilians, and produced more conflict rather than less.

Chile under Augusto Pinochet (1973–1990): On September 11, 1973, a military coup overthrew the democratically elected socialist government of Salvador Allende. General Pinochet's junta immediately detained, tortured, and executed thousands of political opponents. An estimated 3,000 people were killed; tens of thousands more were detained and tortured. Political parties and trade unions were banned. The military's response to Allende's democratic socialism intensified political conflict into systematic state terror — not resolving the underlying political debate about Chile's economic future but suppressing it through violence.

Spain under Francisco Franco (1939–1975): Franco's Nationalist forces won the Spanish Civil War with military support from Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy. The postwar period saw mass executions of Republican opponents, mass imprisonment, and the suppression of regional identities — Basque and Catalan languages and cultures were banned. Decades of political repression followed the military victory, preventing reconciliation and deepening social divisions that only emerged after Franco's death in 1975. Spain under Franco illustrates how military victory in civil conflict can entrench division rather than resolve it.

Uganda under Idi Amin (1971–1979): Amin came to power in a military coup, overthrowing Uganda's elected government. His regime was defined by arbitrary violence against perceived enemies — ethnic purges, the mass expulsion of Uganda's South Asian community in 1972, and the systematic killing of political opponents and ethnic minorities. Estimated deaths range from 100,000 to 500,000. Amin's regime illustrates how military state power can generate and intensify conflict through unpredictable terror rather than containing or resolving it.

The buildup of the military–industrial complex and weapons trading represents a structural, not individual, form of conflict intensification. President Eisenhower coined 'military–industrial complex' in his 1961 farewell address — warning that interlocking military and industrial interests created institutional incentives for continued arms development and conflict. The global weapons trade distributed the material capacity for lethal conflict across the world: Cold War superpowers supplied weapons to proxy states and movements; arms industries in major powers depended on conflict for their markets. The result was a structural dynamic in which more weapons in more hands made more conflicts more lethal — intensifying conflict not through individual state decisions but through the global economy of weapons.

AP Thinking — Causation

The connecting logic across all four KC-6.2.V.C examples: each represents a reaction to conflict or perceived threat that made the underlying situation worse rather than better. Pinochet's coup intensified political conflict into terror. Franco's victory institutionalized repression. Amin's regime turned ethnic and political tension into mass killing. The military–industrial complex gave conflicts the material means to become catastrophic. For AP, the key verb is "intensified" — the state's reaction is not just force, but force that escalated rather than resolved.

Causation — KC-6.2.V.D

Violence Against Civilians: Shining Path and Al-Qaeda

KC-6.2.V.D identifies a third category: movements that used violence against civilians in pursuit of political aims. The CED examples are Shining Path and Al-Qaeda. The analytical task is to distinguish this category from state repression (KC-6.2.V.C) and from nonviolent resistance (KC-6.2.V.A): these were non-state movements that deliberately targeted civilian populations as an instrument of political strategy — not as an accidental by-product of conflict.

Shining Path (Sendero Luminoso) was a Maoist insurgency in Peru founded by philosophy professor Abimael Guzmán. It launched its armed campaign on May 17, 1980 — the same day Peru held its first democratic elections in seventeen years, deliberately framing its violence as opposition to liberal democracy. Shining Path committed massacres in rural indigenous communities it deemed insufficiently revolutionary, assassinated politicians and NGO workers, and bombed urban infrastructure. The Peruvian state's military response was itself brutal. The conflict killed an estimated 70,000 people; a Peruvian Truth and Reconciliation Commission found both sides responsible for grave human rights abuses. Guzmán was captured in 1992, after which the movement collapsed. Shining Path illustrates KC-6.2.V.D because civilian targeting — including killing peasants who were supposed to be the constituency for revolution — was central to its theory of how to create revolutionary conditions.

Al-Qaeda was a transnational Islamist network founded by Osama bin Laden in the context of the anti-Soviet Afghan war. Its stated political aims were the withdrawal of U.S. military forces from Muslim-majority countries and the overthrow of what it characterized as apostate regimes in the Arab world. Its primary method was spectacular attacks on civilian targets to achieve these aims — the 1998 U.S. Embassy bombings in East Africa, the USS Cole bombing (2000), and most consequentially the September 11, 2001 attacks, which killed approximately 3,000 people. Al-Qaeda's strategy was to provoke a disproportionate American military response that would generate Muslim opposition to U.S. presence in the region. For the AP, Al-Qaeda illustrates KC-6.2.V.D because civilian targeting in high-profile locations was the primary instrument of its political strategy — not state repression and not principled nonviolence.

AP Thinking — Comparison

The three CED categories are analytically distinct. KC-6.2.V.A: non-state actors using noncooperation, moral witness, and disciplined suffering to produce political pressure. KC-6.2.V.C: states using institutional military and police power in ways that intensify conflict. KC-6.2.V.D: non-state actors using deliberate attacks on civilian populations as an instrument of political strategy. The AP exam rewards students who can explain the distinction — not just list examples but explain what the method is, who is targeted, and why that method connects to the actor's political goal.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The 20th century produced three broad categories of reaction to existing power structures: groups and individuals who promoted nonviolence as a path to political change (Gandhi, King, Mandela — KC-6.2.V.A); militarized states whose responses to conflict intensified it rather than resolved it (Pinochet, Franco, Amin, the military–industrial complex — KC-6.2.V.C); and non-state movements that targeted civilians as an instrument of political strategy (Shining Path, Al-Qaeda — KC-6.2.V.D). The AP skill is explaining what distinguishes these categories — not what they share, but how and why each represents a different theory of how political change happens, or doesn't.
