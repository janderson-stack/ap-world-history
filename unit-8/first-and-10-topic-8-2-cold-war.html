Causation — KC-6.2.IV.C.ii

Two Worldviews Collide: The Ideological Roots of the Cold War

World War II ended with two powers standing where five had stood before. The United States and the Soviet Union had both been strengthened by the war — and both had watched Europe's old empires exhaust themselves. But the two surviving superpowers held fundamentally incompatible visions for the world they now dominated.

The United States promoted liberal capitalism: open markets, multiparty democracy, free elections, and individual rights. American leaders believed that economic freedom and political freedom were inseparable, and that both were best protected by a U.S.-led international order. By 1945, the United States held roughly half the world's industrial output, controlled nuclear technology, and had military forces positioned across Europe, the Pacific, and beyond. It had the power to build the world it wanted.

The Soviet Union promoted Marxism-Leninism: state control of the economy, collective ownership, one-party communist rule, and the historical argument that capitalism was a system of exploitation that would inevitably be replaced by communism. Soviet leaders believed they were leading the vanguard of global liberation — and that every country that turned communist was a victory for human progress. The USSR had suffered 27 million deaths in WWII but had industrialized rapidly and emerged with the world's largest land army. By 1949, it had nuclear weapons too.

The two powers moved immediately to expand their spheres of influence. The United States announced the Truman Doctrine in 1947 — committing to support any government resisting communist takeover — and backed it with $400 million for Greece and Turkey. The Marshall Plan (1948) committed $13 billion to rebuild Western Europe, explicitly to prevent communist parties from winning power in economically devastated democracies. The Soviet Union, meanwhile, consolidated control over Eastern Europe — Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, and East Germany — installing communist governments backed by the Red Army behind what Winston Churchill called the Iron Curtain.

AP Thinking — Causation

The Cold War did not happen because the U.S. and USSR disliked each other. It happened because WWII fundamentally shifted the global balance of power, leaving two rival superpowers with incompatible ideologies and the capacity to act on them globally. The cause of the Cold War was structural: the same war that destroyed European empires elevated two powers that could not coexist peacefully because they held fundamentally different beliefs about how societies should be organized.

Causation — Effects on International Relations

A Divided World: Alliances, Arms Race, and Proxy Wars

The ideological struggle between capitalism and communism did not stay in the realm of rhetoric. It reshaped the physical and military architecture of the post-war world. In 1949, the United States and eleven Western democracies formed the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) — a formal military alliance committing each member to treat an attack on one as an attack on all. When West Germany joined in 1955, the Soviet Union responded with the Warsaw Pact — a Soviet-led military alliance that brought the Eastern European satellite states under unified military command. The Cold War divide was now institutionalized.

The ideological struggle also became existential with the nuclear arms race. The United States had used atomic bombs in 1945. The Soviet Union tested its first atomic bomb in 1949 — years earlier than U.S. intelligence had predicted. Within a decade, both sides had developed hydrogen bombs hundreds of times more powerful. By the late 1950s, both possessed enough warheads to destroy civilization. This produced the doctrine of Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD): both sides understood that a nuclear first strike would trigger a response that would annihilate both. The Cold War stayed cold — but only barely.

The conflict turned violent through proxy conflicts — wars where the United States and Soviet Union backed opposing sides without directly fighting each other. The Korean War (1950–1953) began when Soviet-backed North Korea invaded U.S.-backed South Korea. Three years of brutal fighting killed over 3 million people, most of them Korean civilians. When the armistice was signed in 1953, the Korean peninsula was divided at almost exactly the same line where it had started — the 38th parallel. Nothing had changed on the map. Everything had changed in human cost.

The Vietnam War (1955–1975) followed a similar logic. Communist North Vietnam, supported by the Soviet Union and China, fought to unite the country under communist rule. South Vietnam, supported by the United States, resisted. Over two decades of escalating conflict killed over 2 million Vietnamese civilians and left the country devastated. The United States withdrew its forces in 1973. North Vietnam reunified the country under communist rule in 1975. The world's most powerful military could not win a proxy war fought in a poor country by a determined opponent who was willing to accept far greater losses.

AP Thinking — Causation (Effects)

NATO, the Warsaw Pact, the nuclear arms race, and proxy conflicts in Korea and Vietnam are all effects of the same underlying cause: the ideological power struggle between the capitalist United States and the communist Soviet Union. Strong AP causation analysis explains the connection, not just the events: each alliance, each proxy war, and each nuclear test was a consequence of two superpowers with incompatible ideologies competing for global influence.

Argumentation — KC-6.2.V.B

A Third Way: The Non-Aligned Movement

Not everyone accepted the Cold War's binary. In April 1955, representatives of 29 Asian and African nations gathered in Bandung, Indonesia, for a conference that had no superpower host and no superpower agenda. They came from countries that had recently won independence from European empires — or were still fighting for it. Their shared experience was not of capitalism versus communism. It was of colonialism: the experience of being governed, exploited, and dismissed by foreign powers for generations.

Sukarno of Indonesia, who convened the conference, argued that the Cold War was a problem created by the old world order — and that newly independent nations had no obligation to inherit it. His opening address at Bandung invoked the idea that the nations gathered there represented the majority of humanity, and that they had the right to define their own future. Non-alignment was not neutrality — it was an assertion of sovereignty. The Bandung Conference produced a statement of ten principles including respect for national sovereignty, non-aggression, and non-interference in other nations' internal affairs.

Kwame Nkrumah of Ghana — who had argued for African independence for decades and would lead the Gold Coast to become the first sub-Saharan African state to gain independence in 1957 — framed the issue in terms of economic dependency. Accepting U.S. or Soviet development aid meant accepting political conditions that would reproduce the dependency of colonial rule. True independence required economic independence from both superpowers, not merely the replacement of one foreign master with another.

India's Jawaharlal Nehru, one of the architects of what became the Non-Aligned Movement, argued that the Cold War bloc system reproduced the imperial pattern: great powers treating smaller nations as pieces in their own game. Non-alignment was an active ideology — a belief about how the world should work, rooted in the conviction that newly independent nations had both the right and the responsibility to chart their own course. The Non-Aligned Movement was, in the terms of the AP CED, groups and individuals promoting alternatives to the existing economic, political, and social orders.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

An AP argumentation claim about the Non-Aligned Movement needs to do more than describe it. It needs to explain what the Non-Aligned Movement reveals about the Cold War era — and connect it to broader patterns. The strongest argument treats the Non-Aligned Movement as evidence that the Cold War's binary framing was always contested: newly independent nations did not simply choose between capitalism and communism; many explicitly rejected both as forms of foreign domination. Sukarno and Nkrumah are not just names to drop — they are evidence of a broader historical pattern.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The Cold War emerged from WWII's fundamental shift in the global balance of power: two rival superpowers, with incompatible ideologies, competed for global influence. The effects reshaped international relations — NATO and the Warsaw Pact institutionalized the divide, the nuclear arms race made the conflict existential, and proxy wars in Korea and Vietnam made it violent. But the Cold War's binary was always contested: the Non-Aligned Movement, launched at the 1955 Bandung Conference by leaders including Sukarno and Nkrumah, asserted that newly independent nations had the right to define their own beliefs about the world order — not simply choose between two versions of superpower control.
