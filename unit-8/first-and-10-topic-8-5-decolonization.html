Causation — KC-6.2.II.A

The Leaders and the Parties: Four Paths to the Same Demand

Independence movements across Asia and Africa were not spontaneous — they were built by organizations, parties, and leaders who mobilized populations and developed strategies for challenging colonial power. The CED identifies four illustrative leaders and parties, each representing a distinct approach to seeking autonomy or independence from imperial rule.

The Indian National Congress (INC), founded in 1885, built India's independence movement over six decades. Under Mohandas Gandhi, the INC developed satyagraha — nonviolent civil disobedience — as its core strategy. The Salt March of 1930, in which Gandhi led thousands in a 240-mile walk to collect salt illegally, was a mass act of defiance against British tax law that drew global attention and made British repression politically costly at home. The INC also built cross-caste coalitions and mobilized rural India in ways that earlier elite-dominated nationalist organizations had not. Jawaharlal Nehru, Gandhi's political partner and successor, directed the INC's international diplomacy and would become India's first Prime Minister after the Indian Independence Act of 1947.

Ho Chi Minh founded the Viet Minh in 1941, combining Vietnamese nationalism with communist ideology. On September 2, 1945 — pointedly choosing the same day Japan formally surrendered — he declared Vietnamese independence, quoting the American Declaration of Independence and the French Declaration of the Rights of Man. France refused to accept the declaration and fought the First Indochina War (1946–1954). The Viet Minh's defeat of France at Dien Bien Phu (1954) forced French withdrawal. Ho Chi Minh's strategy combined mass rural organization, armed resistance, and international political framing — demonstrating that the colonial power's refusal to negotiate forces an armed path.

Kwame Nkrumah returned to the Gold Coast in 1947 after a decade studying in the United States and attending the 1945 Pan-African Congress in Manchester. He split from the conservative UGCC to found the Convention People's Party (CPP) with the slogan "Self-Government Now." Nkrumah's strategy was mass mobilization: the CPP organized workers, market women, youth, and veterans — constituencies the UGCC's elite leadership had ignored. His Positive Action campaign (January 1950) launched nonviolent strikes and boycotts; he was arrested and imprisoned. But the CPP won the 1951 elections while Nkrumah was in prison, and the British released him to govern. Ghana became the first sub-Saharan African nation to achieve independence in 1957 — and Nkrumah became the symbol of African decolonization globally.

Gamal Abdel Nasser led a military coup in Egypt in 1952, overthrowing the British-backed monarchy. Egypt had been nominally independent since 1922 but remained economically controlled by Britain and France through the Suez Canal Company. Nasser's nationalization of the Suez Canal in 1956 transformed the question of sovereignty from political to economic — he was asserting that Egypt would control its own resources. Britain and France responded with military force, but the United States and Soviet Union both pressured them to withdraw, revealing that European imperial power in the postwar world was constrained by Cold War superpower politics.

AP Thinking — Comparison

All four CED examples involve nationalist leaders seeking independence — but their strategies differ significantly. The INC and CPP used nonviolent mass mobilization; Ho Chi Minh used armed resistance; Nasser used state power and economic nationalization. AP LO F asks you to compare processes — so the comparison question is not just who succeeded, but why each chose the strategy they did, and what conditions made each strategy work (or fail to work) in its specific context.

Comparison — KC-6.2.II.B

Two Roads Out: Negotiated Independence vs. Armed Struggle

The CED's most important comparison for Topic 8.5 is between colonies that negotiated independence and those that achieved it through armed struggle. Both groups produced independence — but through dramatically different processes, at dramatically different costs.

Negotiated independence was the path taken where the colonial power calculated that the political, military, and economic cost of holding the colony outweighed the benefit. India achieved independence through the Indian Independence Act of 1947 — the result of decades of INC mass mobilization, the British Labour government's post-war willingness to negotiate exit, and the recognition that Britain could no longer financially sustain empire after World War II. The Gold Coast followed in 1957 after Nkrumah's CPP demonstrated electoral legitimacy: Britain could not govern the Gold Coast without the CPP's participation. French West Africa negotiated a series of independence agreements in 1960 — France concluded after Dien Bien Phu that fighting to hold all its colonies was unsustainable.

Independence through armed struggle was the path taken where the colonial power refused to negotiate — typically because the stakes were too high politically, economically, or in terms of settler populations. Algeria fought France from 1954 to 1962. France did not treat Algeria as a colony but as a department of France itself — constitutionally part of the metropolitan state, home to over one million European settlers (pied-noirs). Withdrawal required a French constitutional crisis; it brought down the Fourth Republic and returned de Gaulle to power. Over a million Algerians died. Angola fought Portuguese colonial rule until 1975 — Portugal, under the Salazar dictatorship, had no democratic mechanism for colonial withdrawal, and its empire was its economic claim to great-power status. Vietnam fought French colonialism (1946–1954) and then American-backed South Vietnamese forces (1955–1975) across three decades of continuous war.

What conditions pushed a colony one road or the other? Four factors matter most: (1) Settler presence — where large European settler populations had political and economic stakes in maintaining colonial control (Algeria, Angola), withdrawal was politically impossible without crisis; (2) Metropole politics — Labour Britain under Attlee was more willing to negotiate colonial exit than the French Fourth Republic or the Salazar dictatorship in Portugal; (3) Leadership strategy — Gandhi and Nkrumah built mass nonviolent movements that made repression politically costly; Ho Chi Minh chose armed resistance because France refused to recognize Vietnam's independence; (4) Cold War stakes — movements branded as communist faced different responses from Western colonial powers.

AP Thinking — Comparison

For AP comparison, you need both a similarity AND a difference, plus a factor that explains the difference. Similarity: both negotiated and armed-struggle paths required organized mass action with identifiable leaders and parties. Difference: negotiated paths produced independence without prolonged violence when the metropole calculated withdrawal was less costly than continued occupation. Factor explaining the difference: settler presence — colonial territories with large European settler populations (Algeria, Angola) required crisis-level political disruption to achieve independence, while colonies without major settler populations (India, Gold Coast) could be released when political conditions changed at home.

Causation — KC-6.2.I.C

Boundaries Under Challenge: Religion, Region, and Ethnic Identity

European colonial empires drew borders without regard for ethnic, linguistic, religious, or cultural communities. When the empires withdrew, these inherited boundaries became some of the most explosive fault lines in the postcolonial world. Three CED cases illustrate how regional, religious, and ethnic movements challenged both colonial rule and the borders it left behind.

The Muslim League, founded in 1906, arose from Muslim concerns about political marginalization in a Hindu-majority India once the British withdrew. As the INC under Gandhi and Nehru pushed for a unified independent India, League leader Muhammad Ali Jinnah argued that Muslim-majority regions needed a separate state — Pakistan — to protect Muslim political and religious rights. Partition in 1947 simultaneously created independent India and Pakistan. Approximately 10–20 million people were displaced in one of the largest forced migrations in history; an estimated 200,000 to 2 million people were killed in intercommunal violence along the partition lines. The colonial boundary — drawn by British administrators in six weeks — divided communities, cut irrigation systems, and separated villages from the cities they depended on.

The Québécois separatist movement in Canada represents a different kind of inherited-boundary challenge: the claim of a French-speaking minority within an English-dominant state whose structure was shaped by British colonial conquest (1760). The Parti Québécois (founded 1968) pursued independence through democratic means — provincial elections and referenda. Quebec held independence referenda in 1980 (rejected 60–40) and 1995 (rejected by just 50.6% to 49.4%). The movement illustrates that "inherited imperial boundaries" challenged political legitimacy even within long-established Western democracies, not just in Asia or Africa.

The Biafra secessionist movement demonstrates the violence possible when new post-independence states refused to accept challenges to colonial-era boundaries. Nigeria, independent from Britain since 1960, was a federation of three major ethnic groups — Hausa-Fulani (north), Yoruba (southwest), and Igbo (southeast) — whose boundaries had been drawn by British administrators for administrative convenience. After a 1966 military coup and anti-Igbo pogroms, the Igbo-majority southeast declared independence as the Republic of Biafra in 1967. The Biafran War (1967–1970) killed an estimated 1–3 million people, many from famine produced by the Nigerian government's food blockade. Nigeria's colonial boundaries were preserved — but the cost of preserving them revealed how violently artificial those boundaries had been.

AP Thinking — Causation

The colonial boundary is the cause; the independence movement or secessionist conflict is the effect — but it takes time, and the causal chain runs through independence itself. Explain the mechanism: European powers drew boundaries based on administrative convenience, exploiting ethnic divisions and drawing lines through communities. When those powers withdrew, new states inherited boundaries that did not match the political identities of the people inside them. Movements like the Muslim League, Québécois separatism, and Biafra were not random conflicts — they were predictable consequences of how empire was built and how it ended.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Three things to carry forward from this reading. First, nationalist leaders used different strategies — nonviolent mass mobilization (Gandhi, Nkrumah), armed resistance (Ho Chi Minh), state power and economic nationalism (Nasser) — each calibrated to its specific colonial context. Second, the key comparison for AP LO F: negotiated independence happened where the colonial power calculated withdrawal was less costly than occupation; armed struggle happened where the stakes were too high to negotiate — especially settler presence, metropole politics, and Cold War framing. Third, inherited imperial boundaries were the fault lines of postcolonial conflict: Partition, Québec, Biafra — three very different cases, one structural cause: colonizers drew borders for themselves, and the people who lived inside them were left with those borders when empire ended.
