Spread of Communism After 1900

How internal tension and Japanese aggression produced communist revolution in China, how the Great Leap Forward turned state economic control into catastrophe, and how redistribution movements in Vietnam, Ethiopia, India, and Iran took strikingly different paths to the same goal.

Causation — KC-6.2.I.i / LO D

How China Turned Communist: Two Causes

The Chinese Communist Party (CCP) was founded in 1921, in a country still reeling from the collapse of the Qing dynasty (1912). Without a stable imperial government, warlords controlled regional territories; the Kuomintang (KMT) — China's Nationalist Party — struggled to unify the country under Chiang Kai-shek. Peasants made up the vast majority of the Chinese population, and most lived as tenant farmers under landlords who controlled the land. The promise of land redistribution made the CCP's message compelling in the countryside.

The first cause of communist revolution was internal tension: political fragmentation, warlordism, the KMT's failure to build stable governance, and the deep inequality between landlords and a landless peasantry created the conditions for revolutionary organizing. But internal tension alone had not produced a communist victory in the 1920s or 1930s — the KMT still held the major cities and most of the military strength.

The second cause was Japanese aggression. Japan seized Manchuria in 1931 and launched a full-scale invasion of China in 1937. The KMT bore primary responsibility for resisting Japan in direct military confrontation — and suffered catastrophic losses in men, equipment, and political legitimacy. Meanwhile, the CCP operated from the rural countryside and the communist stronghold at Yan'an, building popular networks, promising land reform to peasants, and framing itself as the authentic resistance movement. The Long March (1934–1935) — the CCP's grueling 6,000-mile retreat from KMT encirclement — became the founding myth of Chinese communism, and Mao Zedong consolidated leadership during it.

After Japan's defeat in 1945, civil war between the KMT and CCP resumed immediately. The KMT — exhausted, corrupt, and having lost mass popular support — collapsed rapidly. On October 1, 1949, Mao Zedong proclaimed the People's Republic of China from Tiananmen Gate. The KMT retreated to Taiwan. The world's most populous country had become communist.

AP Thinking — Causation

The CED identifies two causes of China's communist revolution: internal tension (political fragmentation, warlordism, KMT failure, peasant land hunger) and Japanese aggression (which devastated the KMT while the CCP built rural support networks). AP causation requires naming both causes and explaining how each contributed — not just listing one. Both were necessary; neither alone was sufficient.

Causation — KC-6.3.I.A.ii / LO D

The Great Leap Forward: State Control and Its Consequences

The Great Leap Forward (1958–1962) was Mao's attempt to transform China from an agrarian society into a socialist industrial power within a single generation — through the state's total control of the national economy. It represents the CED key concept directly: communist government control of the national economy, repressive policies, and negative repercussions for the population.

Collectivization reorganized China's villages into massive people's communes — state-directed collective units that replaced family farming. Communes were assigned production quotas for both grain and steel. The signature policy was the backyard steel furnace campaign: every commune was required to produce steel using small clay kilns. Families melted down metal tools, cooking pots, and door hinges to meet the quota. The steel produced was brittle and largely unusable. Meanwhile, the loss of farm tools contributed directly to the agricultural collapse that followed.

The mechanism of catastrophe was production reporting. Local officials competed to report record harvests — inflating figures by factors of two, three, or more — to demonstrate loyalty and avoid being labeled "rightists." After Defense Minister Peng Dehuai was purged in 1959 for privately criticizing the Leap, no official dared report honest numbers. The state then extracted grain based on the inflated reports, leaving villages without food — without even seed grain for the next planting.

The result was the Great Chinese Famine (1959–1961) — an estimated 15–55 million deaths, the deadliest famine in recorded history. The famine was not caused by drought. It was caused by state economic control: production quotas, collectivization, inflated reporting, and forced grain procurement all operating simultaneously. This is the CED's "negative repercussions for the population" operating at catastrophic scale.

AP Thinking — Causation

The Great Leap Forward is a causation argument inside a causation argument: state control of the economy caused repressive policies (forced collectivization, quota pressure, purges for honest reporting) which caused the famine. The causal chain matters for AP — don't just say the famine happened; explain the mechanism by which state economic control produced it.

Causation / Comparison — KC-6.2.II.D.i / LO E

Four Paths to Redistribution: Vietnam, Ethiopia, India, and Iran

While China was the most dramatic case, the drive to redistribute land and resources shaped politics across Africa, Asia, and Latin America. The CED identifies four illustrative examples — and what makes them valuable for AP work is the range of paths they represent.

Communist Revolution for Vietnamese independence: Ho Chi Minh's Viet Minh combined anti-colonial nationalism with communist ideology, mobilizing Vietnamese peasants with promises of land redistribution from landlords to the rural poor. The First Indochina War (1946–1954) against France ended with the defeat of French forces at Dien Bien Phu and the division of Vietnam at the 17th parallel. Cause: colonial occupation + rural land inequality. Effect: independence, partition, land redistribution to peasants, and a divided Vietnam that became a Cold War battleground.

Mengistu Haile Mariam in Ethiopia: The Derg — a Marxist military junta — overthrew Emperor Haile Selassie in 1974, then nationalized land, industries, and businesses under Mengistu. Land reform broke up large aristocratic and religious estates. But the government's Red Terror (1977–1978), a brutal suppression of political opponents, killed tens of thousands, and civil war combined with collectivization produced famine. Cause: imperial inequality + military coup. Effect: redistribution alongside brutal political repression and civil war.

Land reform in Kerala and other states within India: The Communist Party of India won state elections in Kerala in 1957 — the first democratically elected communist government in the world — and passed sweeping land reform legislation, redistributing holdings from landlords to tenant farmers. Other Indian states followed with similar reforms in the 1950s–1970s. Cause: extreme landlord-tenant inequality + communist electoral organizing. Effect: significant redistribution without revolution or civil war, improving conditions for tenant farmers and landless laborers through democratic legislative means.

White Revolution in Iran: Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi launched the White Revolution in 1963 — a top-down modernization and land redistribution program designed partly to preempt communist rural organizing and satisfy American pressure for reform. The Shah redistributed land from large religious and aristocratic landholdings to rural peasants, disrupting traditional power structures. Cause: fear of communist organizing + U.S. pressure for modernization. Effect: redistribution that displaced traditional elites and accelerated urbanization, contributing to the social tensions that produced the Iranian Revolution of 1979.

AP Thinking — Comparison

These four cases cover revolutionary (Vietnam), military-junta (Ethiopia), democratic-electoral (Kerala/India), and monarchical top-down (Iran) paths to redistribution. The causes differ; so do the effects. AP LO E asks you to explain causes AND effects. Use the cases comparatively: identify where causes overlapped (colonial inequality, rural land hunger) and where effects diverged (independence vs. civil war vs. democratic reform vs. revolution against the reformer).

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Two learning objectives, one topic. LO D: China turned communist because of internal tension (warlordism, KMT failure, land inequality) AND Japanese aggression — both causes are required. The Great Leap Forward shows what communist state control of the economy looks like at catastrophic scale: collectivization, backyard steel furnaces, inflated production reporting, grain extraction based on fake numbers, and a famine that killed tens of millions. LO E: redistribution movements in Vietnam (revolutionary), Ethiopia (military junta), Kerala/India (democratic election), and Iran (monarchical decree) show four different causes and four different effects — use the comparison to show the range, not just to list the cases.
