First & 10 Reading

When Empires Fall

In 1900, the West dominated the global political order, and three enormous land-based empires — the Ottoman, the Russian, and the Qing — still sprawled across Eurasia. By 1923, all three were gone, replaced by new states. In Mexico, a revolution that began with a rigged election rewrote the political and social order itself. None of these collapses had a single cause: in every case, internal weakness and external pressure reinforced each other until the old order broke.

Causation Comparison Argumentation

Before You Read

Sort every cause you meet into one of two bins: internal factors (pressures from inside the state — ethnic tension, misrule, peasant land hunger, failed reform) and external factors (pressures from outside — foreign imperialism, military defeat, world war). The AP exam will ask you to explain how the two kinds of factors worked together, not just to list them.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain the internal and external factors behind the Ottoman, Russian, and Qing collapses, trace how political crisis in Russia led to communist revolution in 1917, and explain how the Mexican Revolution challenged the existing political and social order.

Contextualization

The World in 1900 — Western Dominance, Imperial Strain

At the beginning of the 20th century, the West dominated the global political order. Britain ruled a quarter of the world's land and people; France, Germany, and the United States commanded industrial economies, modern navies, and colonial empires. Power in 1900 meant steel production, railroads, battleships, and capital — and the West had nearly all of it.

Stretched across Eurasia between these industrial powers lay three older land-based empires: the Ottoman Empire, the Russian Empire, and Qing China. Each was enormous, multiethnic, and ancient — and each was struggling. They had fallen behind in industrialization. Their governments rested on autocratic rulers and weak institutions. Their peasant majorities were poor and land-hungry. Their subject nationalities increasingly wanted out. Europeans called the Ottoman Empire "the sick man of Europe," but the diagnosis fit all three.

The pressures these empires faced came in two kinds. Internal factors grew from inside: ethnic and nationalist tension, misrule, poverty, failed reform. External factors battered them from outside: foreign imperialism, military defeat, and — after 1914 — the strain of world war. No single factor brought any of them down. It was always the combination.

AP Thinking — Contextualization

Topic 7.1 opens Unit 7 with the world as it stood in 1900: Western dominance at its height, and the old land-based empires under strain. Every event in this topic — and most of Unit 7 — makes sense only against this backdrop. When you contextualize an essay on 20th-century conflict, start here: a Western-dominated order that the century would systematically dismantle.

Key Concept

The Ottoman and Qing Collapses — Two Empires, One Pattern

The Ottoman Empire had ruled for six centuries, but by 1900 nationalist movements in the Balkans had already stripped away much of its European territory, and ethnic tensions ran through what remained. In 1908 a reform movement of young officers and officials — the Young Turks — forced the restoration of constitutional government, trying to save the empire by modernizing it. World War I destroyed it instead: the Ottomans joined the losing side, and defeat in 1918 brought occupation and partition of the empire's Arab lands. Out of the wreckage, Mustafa Kemal Atatürk led a national resistance movement that expelled occupying forces and proclaimed the Republic of Turkey in 1923 — a secularizing nation-state where a dynastic empire had stood.

Qing China followed the same pattern with different actors. External pressure came first: a century of unequal treaties, foreign spheres of influence, and military humiliation drained Qing sovereignty. Internal anger answered it — the Boxer Rebellion (1899–1901) rose to expel foreign influence, and its defeat by an eight-nation force brought a crushing indemnity and even deeper foreign control. The dynasty's legitimacy collapsed. In 1911 the Xinhai Revolution spread from a mutiny to a national movement, and in 1912 the boy emperor Puyi abdicated — ending not just the Qing dynasty but two millennia of imperial rule in China, and opening a contested republican era shaped by Sun Yat-sen and his successors.

AP Thinking — Comparison

The Ottoman and Qing collapses are a ready-made comparison pair. Similarity: both fell to a combination of internal factors (ethnic tension, failed reform, lost legitimacy) and external factors (foreign imperialism, military defeat). Difference: the trigger — the Ottoman Empire was destroyed by defeat in a world war it joined, while the Qing fell to an internal revolution after decades of foreign encroachment. A strong comparison names the shared pattern AND explains what the different triggers reveal.

Key Concept

Russia — From Political Crisis to Communist Revolution

Russia's collapse came in stages, and the AP exam cares about the sequence. The first warning was the Revolution of 1905: humiliating defeat by Japan and the massacre of peaceful petitioners on "Bloody Sunday" set off strikes and mutinies across the empire. Tsar Nicholas II survived by conceding a parliament — the Duma — but kept real power autocratic, leaving every underlying problem unsolved.

World War I broke what 1905 had cracked. Russia's army suffered catastrophic casualties; its cities ran short of food and fuel; its government cycled through ministers while the tsar took personal command at the front and absorbed the blame for every defeat. In the February Revolution of 1917, bread protests and strikes in Petrograd swelled until the soldiers sent to crush them joined them — and Nicholas II abdicated, ending three centuries of Romanov rule.

Power passed to a Provisional Government of liberal politicians — and it failed. It kept Russia in the war the population hated, delayed land reform the peasants demanded, and shared authority uneasily with the workers' and soldiers' councils called soviets. Into that vacuum stepped Lenin and the Bolsheviks with a promise sharp enough to march under: "Peace, Land, and Bread." In the October Revolution, Bolshevik forces seized the levers of power in Petrograd and overthrew the Provisional Government. Political crisis had led, exactly as the CED puts it, to communist revolution.

AP Thinking — Causation

Russia 1917 is the model causation chain in this topic — keep the links in order: 1905 (warning unheeded) → World War I (external catastrophe) → February 1917 (tsar falls) → Provisional Government fails (war continues, no land reform) → October 1917 (Bolsheviks seize power). Notice how external and internal factors alternate and reinforce: the war did not create Russia's weaknesses, it exposed and accelerated them.

4

Key Concept

Mexico — Revolution Against the Existing Order

The challenge to the old order was not confined to Eurasia. In Mexico, Porfirio Díaz had ruled for over three decades — an era of railroads, foreign investment, and growth whose benefits flowed to a narrow elite while most rural Mexicans lost land to great estates. When Díaz rigged yet another election in 1910, the resulting political crisis exploded into the Mexican Revolution (1910–1920), a decade of civil war that drew in every class of Mexican society.

Two leaders show what made the revolution social as well as political. In the south, Emiliano Zapata fought under the banner "Tierra y Libertad" — land and liberty — demanding the return of village lands swallowed by haciendas. In the north, Pancho Villa led popular armies of cowboys, miners, and the dispossessed. Their pressure pushed the revolution beyond replacing one president with another and toward remaking society.

The revolution's landmark achievement was the Constitution of 1917 — one of the most radical in the world at the time. It authorized land reform and the breakup of great estates, declared national ownership of subsoil resources (a direct challenge to foreign investors), restricted the power of the Catholic Church, and guaranteed labor rights few industrial democracies matched. This is KC-6.2.II.D in action: a state challenging the existing political and social order, born from political crisis.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Were these collapses caused more by internal or external factors? A strong claim takes a position and defends it with specifics. For Russia you might argue external (no World War I, no 1917) — but 1905 shows the cracks predated the war. For Mexico the case is mostly internal (Díaz's political crisis), with foreign economic influence as a secondary pressure. The best arguments acknowledge the interaction instead of pretending one kind of factor acted alone.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

In 1900 the West dominated the global political order, but the old land-based empires were already failing. The Ottoman Empire — strained by ethnic tensions and Young Turk reform — was destroyed by defeat in World War I and replaced by Atatürk's Republic of Turkey (1923). The Qing — hollowed out by foreign imperialism and the failed Boxer Rebellion — fell to the Xinhai Revolution (1911), ending two millennia of imperial rule in China. Russia's political crisis ran from the Revolution of 1905 through the February Revolution, the Provisional Government's failure, and the October Revolution of 1917, when Lenin and the Bolsheviks turned imperial collapse into communist revolution. And in Mexico, the political crisis of Díaz's rigged rule produced the Mexican Revolution and the radical Constitution of 1917 — proof that states around the world were challenging the existing political and social order. In every case the cause was the same combination: internal and external factors, reinforcing each other until the old order broke.
