Topic 7.2 — Causes of World War I  |  AP World History: Modern

First & 10 Reading

The Powder Keg

On 28 June 1914, a nineteen-year-old nationalist shot two people in Sarajevo. Five weeks later, every great power in Europe was at war, and within years the fighting reached Africa, Asia, the Middle East, and the world's oceans. The assassination explains almost nothing by itself. What turned a regional crisis into a global war was everything already in place around it: imperialist competition for resources, territorial conflicts in the Balkans, a flawed alliance system, and intense nationalism — the four causes this topic asks you to weigh.

Causation Contextualization Argumentation

Before You Read

This is a layered causation topic. Track three layers as you read: the powder (long-term causes — imperial rivalry and nationalism), the fuse (regional conflicts in the Balkans and the alliance system), and the spark (the assassination and the July Crisis). The AP exam will ask you which layer mattered most — so collect evidence for each as you go.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain how imperialist expansion and competition for resources raised tensions among European powers, trace how Balkan conflicts and the flawed alliance system escalated a regional crisis into a global war, and explain how intense nationalism contributed to the outbreak of World War I.

Contextualization

Europe, 1914 — A Continent Primed to Explode

Start with the question this whole topic exists to answer: why did one assassination become a world war? Political murders were not rare in this era — presidents, kings, and an empress had been assassinated in the decades before 1914 without triggering general war. Something about Europe in 1914 turned this one into catastrophe.

Part of the answer comes straight from Topic 7.1. The Ottoman Empire you just watched collapse had ruled southeastern Europe for centuries; its long retreat left a power vacuum in the Balkans. New states — Serbia, Bulgaria, Romania, Greece — scrambled to fill it, while two great powers, Austria-Hungary and Russia, maneuvered behind them. The same imperial decline that ended Topic 7.1 loads the gun in Topic 7.2.

Around that vacuum stood a Europe wound tight by forty years of pressure: empires competing for colonies and resources on every continent, armies and navies swollen by militarism, two rival alliance blocs bound by secret promises, and publics taught by politicians and newspapers that national honor was worth a war. The powder was stacked long before anyone struck the spark.

AP Thinking — Contextualization

Strong AP writing situates 1914 in the longer story. The Balkan crisis of 1914 only makes sense against the Ottoman collapse (Topic 7.1), the imperial rivalries of Unit 6, and the nationalism unleashed by the revolutions of Unit 5. When you contextualize World War I, reach backward: the war's causes were decades in the making.

2

Key Concept

Imperialist Expansion and Competition for Resources

The first cause the CED names is imperialist expansion and competition for resources. In the Scramble for Africa, European powers seized nearly the entire continent in a generation; at the Berlin Conference (1884–1885) they divided it on paper — without a single African representative in the room. Imperial competition became the normal mode of great-power politics: raw materials, markets, and strategic territory were the scoreboard, and a rival's gain read as your loss.

That competition repeatedly carried the powers to the edge of war. Germany twice challenged France's position in Morocco — in 1905 and again in 1911, when a German gunboat appeared off Agadir — and both crises ended with Europe's alliance blocs glaring at each other over a colonial dispute. Britain and Germany poured wealth into a naval arms race, building rival fleets of dreadnought battleships. Imperialism taught two dangerous lessons: that great-power status required expansion, and that the next confrontation was always coming.

AP Thinking — Causation

Imperial competition is a long-term cause — powder, not spark. On the exam, connect it to the outbreak of war through a mechanism: colonial crises (Morocco 1905, 1911) hardened the alliance blocs, the naval race militarized Anglo-German rivalry, and decades of competition normalized the idea that great powers settle scores by force.

3

Key Concept

The Balkans, the Spark, and the Flawed Alliance System

Europeans called the Balkans the 'powder keg of Europe', and the label was earned. As Ottoman power receded, Serb, Bulgarian, and Romanian nationalism rose to claim the space, and the Balkan Wars (1912–1913) redrew the regional map twice in two years. Serbia emerged enlarged and emboldened; Austria-Hungary — a multiethnic empire with millions of South Slav subjects — watched Serbian ambition as an existential threat; Russia stood behind Serbia as patron and protector.

The spark came on 28 June 1914, when Gavrilo Princip, a Bosnian Serb nationalist, assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, in Sarajevo. Vienna blamed Serbia, secured Germany's unconditional backing — the famous 'blank check' — issued an ultimatum drafted to be refused, and on 28 July declared war on Serbia. On its own, this was a third Balkan crisis in three years.

The flawed alliance system made sure it did not stay regional. Europe stood divided into the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain), bound by secret commitments and watching each other with mutual suspicion. Russia mobilized to back Serbia; Germany declared war on Russia, then on Russia's ally France; Germany's invasion route ran through neutral Belgium, which brought Britain in. In one week of August 1914, a chain reaction of alliance obligations turned a regional conflict into a global conflict — exactly the escalation the CED describes.

AP Thinking — Causation

Keep the escalation chain in order — it is the skeleton of any 7.2 essay: assassination (28 June) → ultimatum and blank check (July) → Austria-Hungary declares war on Serbia (28 July) → Russian mobilization → German declarations on Russia and France → invasion of Belgium → Britain enters (4 August). Each link is an alliance commitment doing exactly what it promised — that is what made the system 'flawed': it was designed to deter a war and instead guaranteed its spread.

4

Key Concept

Intense Nationalism — and the Problem of Weighing Causes

Running through every other cause was intense nationalism. In the great powers, national pride fueled militarism: Germany demanded its 'place in the sun,' France nursed the loss of Alsace-Lorraine, and mass-circulation newspapers demonized rival nations until compromise looked like cowardice. In the Balkans, nationalism took the form of self-determination movements: Princip killed the Archduke in the name of South Slavs who wanted out of Austria-Hungary. Nationalism was the accelerant — it made imperial rivalry prouder, regional conflict fiercer, and every alliance obligation feel like a matter of national honor.

So which cause mattered most? Historians have argued for a century, and the AP exam invites you into the argument. Was the alliance system the decisive escalator — the machine that turned one murder into general war? Or was the machine only dangerous because nationalism and imperial rivalry had loaded it? Notice the structure of the question: it is the same powder versus spark distinction you practiced in Topic 7.1 with internal and external factors, and it returns in Topic 7.9, the causation capstone of this unit.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

A strong relative-significance argument does three things: takes a clear position ("the flawed alliance system was the most significant cause"), supports it with specific evidence (the blank check, the mobilization chain of August 1914), and addresses the alternative (the assassination was a spark that the alliance system — not nationalism alone — converted into global war). Ranking causes without defending the ranking is description, not argument.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

World War I had four braided causes, and the CED names them all in one sentence. Imperialist expansion and competition for resources — the Scramble for Africa, the Berlin Conference, the Moroccan crises, the naval race — made rivalry the normal state of great-power politics. Territorial and regional conflicts concentrated that rivalry in the Balkans, the powder keg left behind by Ottoman decline, where the Balkan Wars (1912–1913) and the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (28 June 1914) supplied the spark. The flawed alliance system — Triple Alliance vs. Triple Entente, blank check, mobilization chain — escalated Austria-Hungary's war on Serbia into a general European war within a week. And intense nationalism accelerated everything: great-power pride, militarism, demonized rivals, and Balkan self-determination movements. One assassination became a global conflict because all four causes were already in place — your job is to explain how they combined, and to argue which mattered most.
