Topic 7.7 — Conducting World War II  |  AP World History: Modern

First & 10 Reading

Mobilizing Everything

World War II was a total war, and every great power mobilized the same things: propaganda, art, media, intensified nationalism, and the full weight of the economy. But the College Board asks a comparative question — not just THAT governments mobilized, but HOW their methods were alike and different. Two democracies, Britain and the United States, persuaded free publics; two totalitarian states, Germany and the USSR, controlled them. And new technology — the atomic bomb, fire-bombing — drove casualties to heights even World War I never reached.

Comparison Causation Continuity & Change

Before You Read

Hold one similarity and one difference. The similarity: all four states mobilized propaganda, art, media, nationalism, and resources for total war. The difference: totalitarian states used ideology to repress basic freedoms and dominate daily life; democracies did not. For each leader — Churchill, Roosevelt, Hitler, Stalin — ask: what method, and what limit?

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain how governments used propaganda, media, and nationalism to mobilize home and colonial populations, compare democratic and totalitarian mobilization, and explain how the atomic bomb, fire-bombing, and total war drove wartime casualties higher.

Contextualization

Total War, Round Two

You already know the total-war playbook. In Topic 7.3 you saw whole societies mobilized for World War I — propaganda, rationing, economies turned to the front. In Topic 7.6 you saw how the second war began. Now watch the playbook run again, on a larger and deadlier scale.

The Key Concept opens flatly: World War II was a total war. Governments used a variety of strategies — political propaganda, art, media, and intensified forms of nationalism — to mobilize populations both in the home countries and the colonies or former colonies for the purpose of waging war. That parenthetical matters: empires drew troops and resources from across the globe, not just from home.

But the Learning Objective is comparative: explain the similarities and differences in how governments conducted the war. The CED supplies the comparison set itself — two Western democracies (Britain under Churchill, the U.S. under Roosevelt) and two totalitarian states (Germany under Hitler, the USSR under Stalin). Same war, four mobilizations.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Build a two-column table in your head. SHARED METHODS go in the middle: propaganda, art, media, intensified nationalism, full resource mobilization — every state used them. The DIFFERENCE goes in the margins: totalitarian states used ideology (fascism, communism) to repress basic freedoms and dominate daily life; democracies mobilized free publics by persuasion. A strong comparison names a precise shared method AND a precise difference — not just "they were similar and different."

2

Key Concept

The Democracies: Churchill and Roosevelt

Great Britain under Winston Churchill mobilized through words first. His wartime speeches — \"We shall fight on the beaches\" — turned national peril into national resilience, while film and radio carried the war effort into every home through the Blitz. Material mobilization matched the rhetoric: rationing and war-production measures redirected food, fuel, and factories to military needs.

The United States under Franklin Roosevelt became the \"arsenal of democracy.\" The Lend-Lease Act (1941) supplied Allied nations before America even entered the fighting; Fireside Chats built public support over the radio; war bonds and volunteer drives pulled citizens into the effort; and the War Production Board converted the civilian economy to wartime output — car factories building tanks almost overnight.

Note the common thread: both democracies mobilized totally in their demands on the economy, but did so by persuading a free public — with elections, a free press, and independent unions intact. Total mobilization did not require silencing the people.

AP Thinking — Continuity & Change

Connect back to Topic 7.3. The total-war methods of WWI — propaganda, rationing, economic mobilization — CONTINUE here, now amplified by radio and film. What is the change? Reach and intensity: mass media let governments speak directly into every living room, and the scale of production dwarfed 1914–1918. Use the continuity as evidence and name the change precisely.

3

Key Concept

The Totalitarian States: Hitler and Stalin

Germany under Adolf Hitler mobilized through total control of information. The Ministry of Propaganda under Joseph Goebbels controlled all media — press, radio, film — and framed the war as national survival and expansion. Mobilization here was coercive: forced labor and conscription fed the war machine, and dissent was not permitted. This was the militarization of society at the cost of individual freedoms.

The USSR under Joseph Stalin framed the conflict as the Great Patriotic War — a battle against fascism that summoned deep national feeling. Propaganda for unity rallied a population to staggering sacrifice, while political repression of dissent crushed any wavering. Collectivized agriculture and prioritized industrial production bent the entire command economy to military needs.

Here is the Key Concept's crucial second sentence in action: both states used ideologies — fascism and communism — to mobilize all of the state's resources, and, as totalitarian states, to repress basic freedoms and dominate many aspects of daily life. That is the line the democracies did not cross.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Name the difference precisely. It is not that dictatorships mobilized MORE — the democracies mobilized totally too. The difference is what mobilization was allowed to COST the population: in Germany and the USSR, ideology justified repressing basic freedoms and dominating daily life; in Britain and the U.S., mobilization ran through consent. Same toolkit, different limits — that single phrase answers the Learning Objective.

4

Key Concept

New Technology, New Casualties

Total war reached new extremes through technology. The second Key Concept names them: new military technology and new tactics, including the atomic bomb, fire-bombing, and the waging of \"total war\", led to increased levels of wartime casualties.

Fire-bombing raids on cities like Dresden and Tokyo, and the atomic bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, put civilians squarely inside the target zone. The line between soldier and civilian — already blurred in WWI — collapsed, and entire cities became the battlefield. The result was casualty levels beyond even World War I.

Now assemble the comparison the Learning Objective wants: all four powers mobilized propaganda, media, nationalism, and resources (the similarity), but only the totalitarian states repressed basic freedoms and dominated daily life (the difference) — and new technology drove the whole war's human cost higher than ever. Carry the thread to Topic 7.8: when total state power over populations reaches its extreme, the result is mass atrocity — and into the 7.9 causation capstone.

AP Thinking — Causation

Trace the technology trajectory. Topic 7.3 gave you the machine gun and poison gas escalating WWI casualties; here the atomic bomb and fire-bombing escalate them again. Each war's technology made the next deadlier, and "total war" itself — erasing the soldier/civilian line — is a cause of rising casualties, not just a description. That escalation is exactly the kind of long-arc causation the 7.9 capstone rewards.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

World War II was a total war. Every great power used propaganda, art, media, and intensified nationalism to mobilize populations — both at home and in the colonies or former colonies — and turned its whole economy to the front. Two Western democracies mobilized through persuasion: Britain under Churchill (speeches, film, radio, rationing) and the United States under Roosevelt (Lend-Lease, Fireside Chats, war bonds, the War Production Board). Two totalitarian states mobilized through control: Germany under Hitler (Goebbels's Ministry of Propaganda, forced labor) and the USSR under Stalin (the Great Patriotic War, political repression) — using fascism and communism to repress basic freedoms and dominate daily life. Same methods, different limits. And new technology — the atomic bomb and fire-bombing — plus the waging of total war put civilians in the target zone and drove wartime casualties beyond even World War I.
