First & 10 Reading

Extremism in Power

When extremist groups seize unchecked state authority, the result can be the attempted destruction of entire populations. The Key Concept names the pattern: the rise of extremist groups in power led to genocide, ethnic violence, and atrocities across the twentieth century — from the Holocaust during World War II to the Armenian Genocide, the Holodomor, Cambodia, and Rwanda. This reading traces the causes and consequences that the CED identifies, and the structure that recurs across every case.

Contextualization

From Total War to Mass Atrocity

In Topic 7.7 you studied total war — governments mobilizing propaganda, media, nationalism, and their entire economies for conflict. You saw totalitarian states use ideology to repress basic freedoms and dominate daily life. Now follow that power to its most extreme result.

The Key Concept states the claim directly: the rise of extremist groups in power led to the attempted destruction of specific populations, notably the Nazi killing of the Jews in the Holocaust during World War II, and to other atrocities, acts of genocide, or ethnic violence. That is one sentence — read it carefully. It names a cause (extremism in power), a mechanism (attempted destruction), a specific example (the Holocaust), and a category (atrocities, genocide, ethnic violence).

The Learning Objective asks you to explain the various causes and consequences of mass atrocities in the period from 1900 to the present. Notice the plural: causes (not just one) and consequences (not just the killing itself). The CED tests your ability to trace the causal chain from ideology to atrocity and from atrocity to aftermath.

Ground this in the thematic focus: Social Interactions and Organization (SIO) — how societies group their members and the norms that govern interactions between groups. Mass atrocities begin when a regime redefines who belongs to the society — and targets those it excludes.

AP Thinking — Causation

The KC gives you the cause: the rise of extremist groups in power. Your job is to explain how that cause led to the effect. Strong causation does not just say "X happened, then Y happened" — it explains the mechanism. For each case, trace the chain: extremist ideology → state power → dehumanization of a group → attempted destruction. That mechanism is what connects cause to consequence.

2

Key Concept

Extremism in Power: The Holocaust

The KC names the Holocaust directly: the Nazi killing of the Jews in the Holocaust during World War II. The Nazi regime's ideology of racial supremacy and antisemitism drove the systematic dehumanization of Jews through propaganda, legal exclusion, and escalating persecution.

The result was the systematic murder of approximately six million Jews, along with millions of Roma, disabled persons, political prisoners, and others. The Holocaust stands as the KC's central example of what happens when an extremist group holds unchecked state authority and defines a population as an enemy to be destroyed.

But the KC does not stop with the Holocaust — it says "and to other atrocities, acts of genocide, or ethnic violence." The CED's illustrative examples name four more cases. In each one, the same structure appears: an extremist ideology in power, the dehumanization of a defined group, crisis conditions that create opportunity, and unchecked state authority that removes restraints.

AP Thinking — Comparison

The CED names five cases. A strong comparison identifies what they share (the structure: extremist ideology + state power + dehumanization + crisis) and what differs (the specific ideology, the specific group targeted, the specific conditions). Comparing two cases sharpens the causal argument: if the same structure produces atrocity in different places and times, the structure itself is the cause.

3

Key Concept

Four Cases, One Pattern

Armenians in the Ottoman Empire during and after World War I. The Young Turk government launched a campaign of mass deportation and extermination, driven by fear of disloyalty during wartime and the desire to maintain territorial control. An estimated 1.5 million Armenians were killed. Connect to Topic 7.1: the Ottoman Empire's decline and nationalist pressures created the crisis conditions.

Ukraine in the Soviet Union in the 1920s and 1930s. The Holodomor was a man-made famine produced by forced collectivization and grain requisitioning under Stalin's regime, aimed at suppressing Ukrainian nationalism and consolidating state control. Millions perished. Connect to Topic 7.4: the Five-Year Plans and collectivized agriculture were the mechanism the state weaponized.

Cambodia during the late 1970s. The Khmer Rouge under Pol Pot attempted a radical agrarian communist transformation — emptying cities, abolishing institutions, targeting intellectuals, professionals, and ethnic minorities. An estimated 1.7 million people died through execution, forced labor, and starvation.

Tutsi in Rwanda in the 1990s. The Hutu majority government targeted the Tutsi population in a genocide that killed an estimated 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus in roughly 100 days. Colonial policies had entrenched ethnic divisions by favoring the Tutsi minority; post-independence governments exploited rather than healed those divisions. The international community's failure to intervene allowed the killing to continue at extraordinary speed.

AP Thinking — Causation

Across all five cases, trace the structure: extremist ideology in power, dehumanization of a defined group, crisis conditions, and unchecked state authority. That recurring structure is the causal argument the CED tests. When you write about mass atrocities, name the specific ideology, the specific conditions, and the specific group — then explain how the structure produced the outcome.

4

Key Concept

Consequences — From Trauma to Law

Consequences reach beyond the killing itself. Cultural trauma carried by survivors and descendants shapes communities for generations — identities disrupted, histories interrupted, intergenerational pain that persists long after the violence ends.

International legal frameworks were built in direct response. The Genocide Convention (1948) — adopted after the Holocaust — defined genocide in international law for the first time. The International Criminal Court (ICC) was established to prosecute individuals for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes. These institutions exist because earlier atrocities revealed the absence of any legal framework to prevent or punish them.

Political change and reconciliation followed in specific cases: post-genocide Rwanda undertook community-based justice (gacaca courts) and national reconciliation; the Nuremberg and Tokyo tribunals established that individuals — not just states — bear criminal responsibility for atrocities.

But the repeated occurrence of genocide after the Convention's adoption — Cambodia in the 1970s, Rwanda in 1994 — reveals a hard truth: naming and outlawing genocide did not prevent it. The gap between international law and international action is itself a consequence worth analyzing.

AP Thinking — Continuity & Change

The international response to mass atrocities changed: from no legal framework before the Holocaust, to the Genocide Convention (1948), to the ICC. That is the change. The continuity is grimmer: mass atrocities continued even after the Convention was adopted. A strong Continuity & Change answer names both — what changed (legal and institutional frameworks) and what did not (the recurrence of genocide) — and explains what that pattern reveals.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The Key Concept names the cause: the rise of extremist groups in power led to the attempted destruction of specific populations — notably the Holocaust, but also the Armenian Genocide, the Holodomor, Cambodia, and Rwanda. Across all five cases, the same structure recurs: extremist ideology in power, dehumanization of a defined group, crisis conditions, and unchecked state authority. The consequences include cultural trauma, international legal frameworks (the Genocide Convention, the ICC), political change and reconciliation, and global awareness — alongside the harsh continuity that naming genocide did not prevent it.
