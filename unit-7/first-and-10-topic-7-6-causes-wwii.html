Topic 7.6 — Causes of World War II  |  AP World History: Modern

First & 10 Reading

Four Roads to One War

The unresolved tensions you studied in Topic 7.5 did not stay unresolved — they exploded into a second total war. The College Board names four causes of World War II in a single sentence: a failed peace, a global economic collapse, continued imperialist ambition, and — its own word — "especially" the rise of fascist and totalitarian regimes. This reading walks each road, then asks the question the framework dares you to answer: which cause mattered most?

Before You Read

The Key Concept is one long sentence listing four causes. Number them as you read: (1) the unsustainable peace, (2) the Great Depression, (3) continued imperialist aspirations, (4) and especially the rise of fascist and totalitarian regimes. Watch the word "especially" — the CED is ranking its own causes, and the topic asks you to evaluate that ranking, not just repeat it.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain how the unsustainable peace after World War I, the Great Depression, and continued imperialist aspirations escalated tensions in the 1930s, and how the rise of fascist and totalitarian regimes — especially Nazi Germany under Hitler — turned those tensions into aggressive militarism and war.

Contextualization

The Twenty Years' Crisis

In Topic 7.5 you left the interwar world full of unresolved tensions — arbitrary mandate borders, conquered peoples under puppet states, colonized populations demanding self-determination, and a Germany nursing the wounds of a peace it never accepted. This topic is what happens when those tensions resolve violently. The two decades between the wars were less a peace than a pause.

The Key Concept compresses the whole story into one sentence, and it pays to read that sentence as a four-part list: World War II was caused by the unsustainable peace settlement after World War I, the global economic crisis engendered by the Great Depression, continued imperialist aspirations, and — note the word — especially the rise to power of fascist and totalitarian regimes that produced the aggressive militarism of Nazi Germany under Adolf Hitler.

That little word "especially" is the framework doing your historical thinking out loud. The CED is not just listing causes — it is ranking them, naming the rise of the regimes as the most significant. Your job in this topic is to test that ranking: build the case for it, build the case against it, and decide what you actually think.

AP Thinking — Causation

Distinguish two kinds of cause as you read. BACKGROUND CONDITIONS (the unsustainable peace, the Depression) created grievance and desperation but did not themselves start a war. The PROXIMATE cause (the rise of fascist regimes and their aggressive militarism) is the actor that converted those conditions into invasions. The CED's "especially" is a claim that the trigger outweighed the conditions — exactly the kind of weighting the 7.9 capstone will ask you to argue.

2

Key Concept

Two Conditions: A Failed Peace and a Broken Economy

Cause one is the unsustainable peace settlement. The Treaty of Versailles (1919) saddled Germany with crushing reparations, stripped away territory, and — through the war-guilt clause — forced it to accept sole blame for the war. The result was a deep well of humiliation, resentment, and economic burden: a widespread German conviction that the peace was a betrayal to be overturned, not a settlement to be honored. That bitterness became fertile ground for radical political movements promising to tear the treaty up.

Cause two is the global economic crisis engendered by the Great Depression — the collapse you studied in Topic 7.4, now seen as a cause of war. Mass unemployment and desperation discredited democratic governments that could not fix the crisis, and made the extremists' promises of order, revival, and national renewal suddenly attractive to people with nothing left to lose. The Depression did not start the war by itself, but it weakened the democracies and strengthened the radicals.

AP Thinking — Continuity & Change

Notice how Topics 7.4 and 7.5 fold into 7.6. The Great Depression (7.4) is no longer just an economic episode — here it is a CAUSE of the next war. The unsustainable peace (7.5's "peace that settled nothing") is no longer just an unresolved tension — here it is the first cause on the CED's list. Topic 7.6 reframes earlier content as causation. Use those callbacks as evidence, not just review.

3

Key Concept

Especially: The Rise of the Regimes

Cause three is the one the CED calls especially decisive: the rise to power of fascist and totalitarian regimes. In Germany, Hitler and the Nazi Party capitalized on the nationalist resentment Versailles had bred and on the desire for revenge, promising to restore Germany's glory, rebuild its military, and reclaim lost territories.

Those were not just slogans. They became aggressive expansionist policy: rearmament in open defiance of Versailles, the remilitarization of the Rhineland, and the annexation of neighboring lands. This is where the first two causes converge — Versailles supplied the grievance, the Depression supplied the desperation, and the Nazi regime supplied the militarized will to act on both.

Cause four — continued imperialist aspirations — shows the same pattern was not Germany's alone. The 1930s saw aggressive militarism across three regions: German expansion in Europe, Italian ambitions under Mussolini in the Mediterranean and Africa, and Japanese expansion in Asia — the Manchukuo of Topic 7.5 the leading edge. Three expansionist powers, one militarized appetite for empire.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Take "especially" as a thesis to test. The case FOR: without Hitler's regime, the grievances of Versailles and the pain of the Depression need not have produced a world war — many countries suffered both without invading their neighbors. The case AGAINST: without the failed peace and the Depression, Hitler never rises at all; the conditions made the trigger possible. A strong answer names a criterion for "most significant" and defends a position — it does not simply list four causes and stop.

4

Key Concept

The Multiplier: A League That Could Not Act

The four causes had an enabler that the Key Concept does not list but every account includes: the League of Nations' inability to respond effectively to aggression. When Japan seized Manchuria and Italy invaded Ethiopia, the League issued condemnations but imposed no real consequences — and the expansionist powers were watching.

Aggression that went unpunished emboldened the aggressors. Diplomacy curdled into appeasement — most infamously at Munich in 1938, where handing Hitler the Sudetenland only fed his appetite. Each unanswered move taught the same lesson: there was no price to pay for aggression, and the road from appeasement ran straight to 1939.

Now assemble the answer the Learning Objective wants: World War II had four causes the CED names — a failed peace, a global depression, imperial ambition, and especially the rise of fascist and totalitarian regimes — multiplied by a collective-security system too weak to stop any of them. Carry the weighing question forward: it is direct rehearsal for the 7.9 causation capstone, and a parallel to the cause-weighing you did back in Topic 7.2.

AP Thinking — Causation

Separate listed causes from enabling conditions. The CED's four causes are the engine; the League's failure and appeasement are the missing brakes. A sophisticated causation answer can use the League's weakness to explain HOW the four causes turned into an actual war — while still recognizing it is not one of the four causes the Key Concept names. Precision about what the framework says matters on the exam.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The College Board names four causes of World War II in one sentence: the unsustainable peace settlement after World War I (the Treaty of Versailles' reparations, territorial losses, and war-guilt clause, which bred humiliation and radical movements), the global economic crisis engendered by the Great Depression (which discredited democracies and made extremism attractive), continued imperialist aspirations (aggressive militarism by Germany, Italy, and Japan), and especially the rise to power of fascist and totalitarian regimes that produced the aggressive militarism of Nazi Germany under Adolf Hitler. A powerless League of Nations and the policy of appeasement let unpunished aggression run to war in 1939. The CED's word "especially" ranks the rise of the regimes above the rest — and the topic asks you to evaluate that weighting, not just memorize the list.
