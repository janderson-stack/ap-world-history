First & 10 Reading

Weighing What Mattered Most

Historians don't just list causes — they rank them. Topic 7.9 is the Unit 7 causation capstone, which means everything you studied in Topics 7.1 through 7.7 becomes evidence for a single analytical task: argue which causes of global conflict were most significant, and why. The skill is relative significance. The evidence is the whole unit. The argument is yours to build.

Causation Argumentation Evaluation

Before You Read

Keep two vocabulary terms in view: underlying causes — long-term structural factors that created conditions for conflict (imperial competition, nationalist movements, economic crisis) — versus immediate triggers — specific events that set conflict in motion (the Sarajevo assassination, the German invasion of Poland). Both matter, but most historians argue underlying causes carry more analytical weight in a long-arc argument.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain the distinction between underlying causes and immediate triggers, identify at least three major causal categories from Unit 7, explain how causes braid together rather than operate independently, and build a defensible claim that ranks causes by relative significance using weighing language.

1

Contextualization

The Capstone Question: Not What, But Which

You have spent the last seven topics answering the question what caused global conflict? The CED gave you the answer across thirteen Key Concepts: imperial competition, intense nationalism, a flawed alliance system, empire collapse, the Great Depression, fascist and totalitarian regimes, and new military technology. You can list them. Now comes the harder question: which causes mattered most?

This is the AP skill called relative significance — and it is what separates a descriptive list from a historical argument. Relative significance does not ask you to ignore some causes; it asks you to rank them and explain the ranking. Why did one cause do more work than another? What would have had to change for the conflict not to happen? What made some conditions necessary versus merely contributing?

The CED modeled this skill once, quietly, inside the WWII Key Concept. In Topic 7.6 you read: "the causes of World War II included the unsustainable peace settlement after World War I, the global economic crisis engendered by the Great Depression, continued imperialist aspirations, and especially the rise to power of fascist and totalitarian regimes." That word — especially — is a significance claim. It ranks one cause above the others. Your capstone task is to build your own, defend it with evidence, and use weighing language that makes the argument clear.

AP Thinking — Causation

The CED's "especially" is a model you can borrow, argue with, or beat. Notice what it does: it names the other causes, acknowledges them, and then ranks one above them. A weak answer lists all causes equally. A strong argument says "X was more significant than Y because..." and names a criterion — necessary condition, proximate cause, most explanatory power, widest impact. Name your criterion and defend it.

2

Key Concept

Underlying Causes vs. Immediate Triggers

The most important analytical move in a causation argument is sorting causes by type. Underlying causes are long-term structural factors that built pressure over years or decades — they created conditions in which conflict became possible, even likely. Immediate triggers are specific events that set conflict in motion — they lit the fuse, but they did not make the powder keg.

For World War I: the underlying causes were imperial competition and rivalry, intense nationalism across Europe, the hair-trigger alliance system (Triple Alliance vs. Triple Entente), and the territorial decline of older empires. These were all in place by 1914. The immediate trigger was the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand on June 28, 1914 — one gunshot, fired by a nineteen-year-old who had already failed once that morning and thought he had failed again.

For World War II: the underlying causes were the unsustainable peace settlement at Versailles, the Great Depression, and continued imperialist competition. The more proximate cause — the one the CED marks with "especially" — was the rise of fascist and totalitarian regimes: Nazi Germany under Hitler, which actively converted those underlying conditions into aggressive militarism, rearmament, and territorial expansion. Germany's invasion of Poland on September 1, 1939 was the trigger, but the regime was the mechanism that made invasion possible.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Ask yourself: if you removed this cause, would the conflict still have happened? The alliance system in 1914 was so rigid that a local Austro-Serbian conflict dragged in six great powers in five weeks — remove the alliance system and the assassination stays local. Remove the assassination and the war probably still happens, just from a different spark. That test — "would the conflict happen without this cause?" — is one way to rank relative significance.

3

Key Concept

Braided Causes: When Factors Work Together

The most common mistake in a causation argument is treating causes as if they operated independently, one after another. In history, causes braid — they reinforce each other, enable each other, and interact in ways that make the whole more explosive than any single strand.

Consider the WWII causal chain. The unsustainable peace at Versailles (Topic 7.5) imposed reparations and humiliation on Germany, creating economic crisis and political resentment. The Great Depression (Topic 7.4) then deepened that economic crisis, discrediting democratic governments and making radical political movements suddenly credible to millions of desperate, angry voters. Into that environment rose the Nazi regime (Topic 7.6), which transformed economic desperation and nationalist resentment into organized, militarized aggression. The regime then pursued imperialist expansion in the name of German destiny — seizing the Rhineland, Austria, the Sudetenland, and finally Poland.

This is not four separate causes — it is one braid: Versailles → Depression → fascist regime → aggressive militarism → war. A strong significance argument doesn't just identify the braid; it argues which strand carried the most weight. Was it the conditions (Versailles, the Depression) that made everything else possible? Or the regime that converted conditions into action? That question — which strand mattered most — is exactly what relative significance asks.

AP Thinking — Causation

Distinguish contributing factors from necessary conditions. A contributing factor made conflict more likely but was not indispensable. A necessary condition was one without which the conflict would not have occurred — or would have been fundamentally different. Necessary conditions carry more argumentative weight than contributing factors. If you can identify a necessary condition in the causal chain, argue for it — that is your significance claim.

4

Key Concept

Building the Argument: Weighing Language

A significance argument requires precision language — terms that signal you are ranking, not just listing. The AP exam rewards students who use weighing language explicitly, because it shows the reader that you understand you are making a judgment, not just describing events.

Here are the moves available to you. "Most significant because" — names your top-ranked cause and your criterion for ranking it. "More significant than" — makes an explicit comparison between two causes, which is stronger than ranking in isolation. "Necessary condition" — identifies a cause without which the conflict could not have occurred in the form it took. "Underlying cause" versus "immediate trigger" — sorts causes by proximity to the event. "Enabled by" — traces how one cause made another cause possible.

What to avoid: "equally important" — that is not a ranking. "All the causes combined" — that is not a significance claim. "There are many causes" — that is a description, not an argument. You are allowed to disagree with the CED's "especially" — but you have to disagree with specific evidence, not just a different list. A historian who argues against the consensus has to argue harder, not differently.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Write the claim before you write the evidence. Try this template: "[Cause X] was the most significant cause of global conflict in the period 1900–1945 because [criterion — it was a necessary condition / it enabled all other causes / it was the proximate mechanism that converted conditions into action]. Without [Cause X], [Cause Y] and [Cause Z] would not have produced the same outcome." Then fill in the evidence. A claim that strong is worth defending with three topics of specific evidence from Unit 7.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Topic 7.9 asks you to move from listing causes to ranking them — the skill of relative significance. Causes come in two types: underlying causes (long-term structural factors like imperial competition, nationalism, economic crisis, and empire collapse) and immediate triggers (the events that lit the fuse). Causes braid together — Versailles → Depression → fascist regimes → aggressive militarism is a chain, not a list of independent factors. A strong argument picks the most significant strand and defends the ranking using weighing language: "most significant because," "necessary condition," "more significant than," "enabled by." The CED modeled this once with the word "especially" in the WWII Key Concept. Your capstone task is to build your own defensible claim, support it with evidence from at least three Unit 7 topics, and explain why your ranked cause carried more weight than the others.
