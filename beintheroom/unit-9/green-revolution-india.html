The Wheat Decision

New Delhi, 1966. Two consecutive failed monsoons have left India dependent on emergency American grain shipments to avoid famine. Food Minister C. Subramaniam must decide whether to approve the mass import of roughly 18,000 tons of Mexican high-yield dwarf wheat seed — developed by Norman Borlaug and championed in India by agricultural scientist M.S. Swaminathan — and the fertilizer, irrigation, and pricing reforms required to make it work. You are an advisor in the Ministry of Food and Agriculture, briefing the minister before he faces the cabinet. Can you see the benefits of this technology? Can you see the costs?

Historical Situation

India in 1966 is a nation on the edge. Two consecutive monsoon failures have devastated harvests. The government depends on American PL-480 food aid — "ship-to-mouth" shipments that arrive just in time to prevent starvation but come with political conditions that humiliate a proud, newly independent democracy. President Lyndon Johnson has used the food shipments as leverage, sometimes delaying authorization to pressure India on foreign policy issues including the Vietnam War.

The alternative is a technological gamble. Norman Borlaug's high-yield dwarf wheat varieties have produced extraordinary results in Mexico, but they have never been tested at scale in India. The seeds require chemical fertilizers, controlled irrigation, and modern pricing systems that India's agriculture does not yet have. Adopting them means transforming Indian farming — not just planting different seeds.

Food Minister C. Subramaniam and agricultural scientist M.S. Swaminathan believe India can grow its way out of famine dependence. But the cabinet is divided. The costs are enormous, the risks are real, and the consequences — intended and unintended — will reshape Indian agriculture for generations.

Step 2 — Decision 1

Adopt at Scale, Pilot Regionally, or Refuse the Imported Seed?

The ministry has three paths. Each has a real defender's case — and each carries consequences that extend far beyond the immediate harvest.

Adopt at Scale

Import the full 18,000 tons of Mexican dwarf wheat seed. Commit to the fertilizer, irrigation, and pricing reforms required to make it work. Accept the risk that the seeds may fail in Indian conditions — but act now, because famine will not wait for pilot programs.

Pilot Regionally

Import a smaller quantity and test the new varieties in Punjab and Haryana — regions with existing irrigation. Use the results to decide whether to scale up. This is slower but reduces the risk of catastrophic failure if the seeds do not perform as expected.

Refuse the Imported Seed

Reject dependency on foreign seed varieties. Invest instead in improving India's indigenous crop varieties and traditional farming methods. Accept continued PL-480 dependence in the short term to preserve agricultural sovereignty in the long term.

Step 2 — Decision 2

Who Gets the Seed and Fertilizer First?

Resources are limited. The new seeds require fertilizer and irrigation that India cannot provide everywhere at once. The question is not just what to adopt — but who benefits first, and who waits.

Punjab's Large Irrigated Farms

Concentrate seed and fertilizer in Punjab and Haryana — regions with existing irrigation infrastructure and large commercial farms. This produces the fastest yield gains and the quickest famine relief, but concentrates benefits among farmers who are already better off.

Broader Distribution Including Smallholders

Distribute seed and fertilizer more widely, including to smallholder farmers in drier regions. This is slower, riskier, and more expensive — but it avoids concentrating all gains among large commercial farms and reduces the risk of deepening rural inequality.

Cooperative Distribution Model

Channel seed and fertilizer through farmer cooperatives. Cooperatives can pool resources, share irrigation access, and negotiate prices collectively. But building cooperative infrastructure takes time India may not have.

Step 2 — Decision 3

Accept the Dependence?

High-yield varieties require chemical fertilizers, controlled irrigation, and repeated seed purchase. The technology that promises to end food dependence on America creates a new dependence — on inputs India does not yet produce. Name the long-term tradeoff before recommending.

Accept — Feed People Now

The immediate crisis outweighs long-term concerns. Accept the dependence on fertilizer and seed imports. Build domestic production capacity later, after the famine threat is gone.

Accept with Mitigation

Adopt the new seeds, but simultaneously invest in domestic fertilizer production, seed-breeding programs, and soil conservation. Accept short-term dependence while building long-term self-sufficiency.

Conditional Acceptance

Adopt only if the government commits to a 10-year plan for domestic input production. Set specific milestones for reducing import dependence. If the milestones are not met, reconsider the program.

Reject the Dependence

The history of colonialism teaches that dependence on foreign inputs leads to foreign control. India must develop its own agricultural solutions, even if they take longer and produce smaller yields in the short term.
