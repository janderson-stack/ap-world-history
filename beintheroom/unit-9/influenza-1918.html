The Closing Order

A mid-sized U.S. city, autumn 1918. Cases of a severe respiratory illness are appearing in the city's hospitals. Nearby army training camps are overcrowded, and troops pass through the city by rail daily. A major Liberty Loan war-bond parade — expected to draw tens of thousands into dense street crowds — is scheduled for this week. You are an advisor to the city's Health Commissioner. The commissioner must decide: issue emergency closure orders now, or wait for more confirmed cases? Cancel the parade, or let patriotic duty prevail? And when the closures finally come, how long do you hold?

Historical Situation

In the autumn of 1918, a devastating strain of influenza is sweeping across the world. The war in Europe has concentrated millions of soldiers in crowded training camps, troop ships, and trenches — ideal conditions for respiratory disease transmission. The virus has already torn through military installations, and cases are now appearing in civilian populations across the United States.

Your city sits along a major rail corridor. Army camps nearby house thousands of soldiers in close quarters. Troops move through the city's train station daily. The first civilian cases arrived in early autumn, and hospital admissions are climbing. The city's physicians are reporting a pattern unlike ordinary influenza: rapid onset, severe pneumonia, high mortality among young adults.

The Health Commissioner faces enormous pressure from multiple directions. The war effort demands public morale and economic activity. A Liberty Loan parade — a massive public event to sell war bonds — is scheduled. Business owners fear the economic damage of closures. But the evidence from other cities suggests that density and public gatherings accelerate transmission dramatically. Cities that acted early — closing schools, banning gatherings, enforcing quarantines — appear to have experienced significantly lower per-capita mortality than those that delayed. The question is whether your city will act before the curve climbs, or after.

Note: Specific mortality figures referenced in this scenario are drawn from the documented historical contrast between cities that acted early and those that delayed during the 1918 pandemic. Exact numbers should be verified against primary sources before use in formal writing.

Step 2 — Decision 1

Issue Closure Orders Now, or Wait for Confirmation?

Cases are appearing. Nearby army camps are crowded and troops move through the city by rail. The pattern from other cities suggests early action saves lives — but acting now means economic disruption, public anger, and the political risk of overreacting if the outbreak proves mild.

Close Now — Act Before the Curve

Issue immediate closure orders: close schools, theaters, churches, and all indoor public gatherings. Ban large outdoor assemblies. The evidence from other cities shows that early intervention reduces per-capita mortality. Acting on density and crowding before the curve climbs is the strongest tool available.

Wait for More Confirmed Cases

The current case count may not justify the economic and social disruption of city-wide closures. Wait until the hospitals report a clear surge before acting. This avoids panic and preserves public trust — but risks allowing transmission to accelerate before controls are in place.

Targeted Measures Only

Issue advisories and close the most crowded venues — military barracks, hospitals, and schools — but keep businesses, churches, and public life open. Balance the public health risk against the economic and social cost of full closure.

Step 2 — Decision 2

The Liberty Loan Parade

A major war-bond parade expecting tens of thousands of people in dense street crowds is scheduled for this week. The parade is a patriotic event — it funds the war effort, demonstrates public morale, and carries the weight of national duty. Canceling it risks accusations of disloyalty and undermining the war effort. But dense crowds during a respiratory pandemic create ideal conditions for mass transmission.

Cancel the Parade

Dense crowds in a respiratory pandemic are a transmission accelerator. The science is clear: tens of thousands packed into city streets will spread the virus to thousands of households within days. Cancel the parade and redirect war-bond sales to other channels. Public health outweighs public morale.

Allow the Parade

The war effort cannot pause for an illness that may prove no worse than ordinary influenza. Canceling the parade signals weakness to the enemy and disloyalty at home. Millions of soldiers are fighting overseas — the home front must hold. Advise attendees to cover their coughs, but let the parade proceed.

Modify — Reduce Scale and Duration

Shorten the parade route, limit the number of marching units, discourage spectators from lingering, and distribute public health guidance. This preserves the patriotic event while reducing (but not eliminating) the density risk.

Step 2 — Decision 3

When to Lift Restrictions

After weeks of closures, case counts are declining. Economic and social pressure to reopen is intense. Businesses are losing revenue. Families are isolated. Churches and schools have been closed for weeks. The city's political leadership is demanding a reopening date. But public health officers warn that lifting restrictions too early — while the virus is still circulating — risks a second wave as people re-crowd into the spaces that were closed.

Lift Now — Relieve the Strain

Cases are declining. The worst appears to be over. Reopen schools, churches, and businesses immediately. The economic and social cost of continued closure now outweighs the public health benefit.

Hold Longer — Guard Against a Second Wave

Declining cases do not mean the virus is gone. Premature reopening will re-crowd the exact spaces that closures were designed to empty. Hold restrictions for at least two additional weeks beyond the peak decline to ensure the virus has fully receded.

Phased Reopening

Reopen outdoor spaces and low-density businesses first. Keep schools, theaters, and churches closed for another week. Monitor case counts daily. If numbers spike, re-close immediately.

Conditional — Tie Reopening to Data

Announce specific thresholds: reopen when hospital admissions fall below a stated number for a stated number of consecutive days. Remove the decision from political pressure by anchoring it to measurable data.
