After Sharpeville

Johannesburg, South Africa  ·  June 1961

It has been fifteen months since March 21, 1960, when South African police opened fire on a crowd of several thousand Black protesters near the Sharpeville police station, killing 69 people and wounding more than 180. The apartheid government declared a state of emergency. The African National Congress was banned under the Unlawful Organizations Act on April 8, 1960. Its leadership has been arrested, harassed, or driven underground. Chief Albert Luthuli, the ANC's president — who has led the organization on a strictly nonviolent path since 1952 — was arrested and then released, his movements restricted by banning orders. Nelson Mandela and others have been meeting in secret.

You are a strategist in the banned ANC's underground leadership circle. The movement is debating a question that cannot be deferred any longer: the apartheid state has answered the movement's fifteen years of nonviolent resistance with a massacre, a banning, and mass arrests. Is strict nonviolence still the right path — or must the movement adapt? And if it adapts, what does that mean?

You are reasoning from June 1961. You do not know how this ends. You do not know about 1990, or 1994, or the Nobel Peace Prize. You know what has happened up to now.

Continuity hook: If you completed the BeInTheRoom scenario in Topic 8.5 — "Positive Action" in Accra, January 1950 — you have stood in a strategist's seat like this before. Nkrumah's Convention People's Party was also deciding whether nonviolent mass action could force a colonial state to yield. The Gold Coast playbook worked — but the British were not willing to fire on crowds the way the apartheid state has demonstrated it is willing to fire. Ask yourself as you work through this scenario: does the Accra precedent apply here? What is different about Sharpeville?

Step 1 of 6 — Choose Your Role
1
Choose Your Role

Each member of the leadership circle carries a different relationship to the nonviolence tradition, a different view of what the state has demonstrated it will do, and a different sense of what the movement can sustain. Your role shapes which pressures you feel most acutely.

2
The Situation Before You
What the ANC Knows as of June 1961

The ANC has been banned for fourteen months. Mass public campaigns are now illegal — organizing them puts unarmed people in front of a state that has demonstrated it will use lethal force. Chief Luthuli won the Nobel Peace Prize this year (announced October 1961), but his movements are restricted and he cannot lead the movement from the front. Nelson Mandela has gone underground, living away from his family, moving between safe houses. The state is using a combination of the Suppression of Communism Act, the Public Safety Act, and emergency powers to arrest and ban anyone associated with the resistance. The movement's mass organizational base — built through the Defiance Campaign of 1952 and the Congress Alliance — still exists underground, but it cannot operate in public without exposing members to arrest and violence.

The Specific Question

A group within the leadership — including Mandela — has been discussing whether to form a separate military wing that would conduct sabotage of infrastructure: power lines, government buildings, communication systems. The explicit commitment: no attacks on people. The strategic theory: sabotage would demonstrate that the apartheid state's economy and infrastructure are vulnerable, creating economic pressure and showing the state that its military response to nonviolent protest has consequences — without crossing the line of targeting civilians. Chief Luthuli has not endorsed this. His position is that even property sabotage abandons the moral ground that makes the movement's case internationally. The question before the circle is whether to proceed.

3
Examine the Evidence

The leadership circle has assembled what it knows. Select at least four chips to read — the evidence you examine shapes how you reason through the decisions ahead.

4
Decision 1 — The Method Question
The choice before the circle: The ANC has been built on the Gandhian tradition of nonviolent resistance — the Defiance Campaign, the bus boycotts, the Congress of the People. Chief Luthuli's leadership has held firmly to that tradition. But the apartheid state has now banned the ANC, jailed and restricted its leadership, and demonstrated at Sharpeville that it will shoot unarmed protesters. The question is whether that changed situation requires a changed method — and if so, what the change should be.
5
Decision 2 — The State's Escalation
The organizational question: The apartheid state has banned public assembly and made the ANC itself illegal. The movement's power for fifteen years came partly from its mass public visibility — marches, rallies, organized noncooperation that showed the scale of opposition. That public visibility is now what exposes unarmed members to arrest and violence. The question is how the movement organizes: in public, accepting the risk; underground, protecting people but losing visibility; or some combination.
6
Decision 3 — The World's Eyes
The international question: Sharpeville produced something the movement has never had before: genuine international outrage. The Security Council passed a resolution calling on South Africa to end apartheid. Newly independent African states at the UN condemned the massacre. International solidarity movements organized boycotts of South African goods. But the Cold War complicates this: Western governments — particularly the United States and Britain — regard the ANC with suspicion because of its ties to the South African Communist Party. The apartheid government successfully brands anti-apartheid resistance as communist subversion, which makes Western governments reluctant to pressure South Africa too hard. The question is what international strategy the movement should pursue.
7
Reflection & Writing

Self-Check: Are these ideas in your thinking?

  • KC-6.2.V.A places the ANC in the category of groups that promoted nonviolence as a method for political change — but the Sharpeville crisis forced the movement to confront the limits of that method against a state willing to use lethal force.
  • KC-6.2.V.C applies to what the apartheid state did: its military response to nonviolent protest (the Sharpeville Massacre, the banning, the state of emergency) did not resolve the underlying conflict — it intensified it by closing off the nonviolent path and generating international outrage.
  • The design boundary matters analytically: Umkhonto we Sizwe was historically committed to avoiding civilian casualties — targeting infrastructure rather than people. This is what distinguishes MK from the movements in KC-6.2.V.D (Shining Path, Al-Qaeda), which deliberately targeted civilians.
  • The continuity hook: in Accra in 1950, Nkrumah's CPP faced a colonial state that might arrest protesters but had not yet fired on crowds. The Gold Coast context made strict nonviolence viable. Sharpeville changed the South African context — the question is whether the same method applies in different conditions.
  • Mandela spent 27 years in prison before South Africa's democratic transition in 1994. Whatever decisions the movement made in 1961 had to be sustainable for decades, not weeks.
