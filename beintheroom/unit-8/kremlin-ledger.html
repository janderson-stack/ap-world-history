The Kremlin Ledger

Moscow, Soviet Union  ·  March 1985

Mikhail Gorbachev became General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union on March 11, 1985. He is 54 years old — the youngest leader the Soviet Union has had since Stalin. After three General Secretaries in three years (Brezhnev died in 1982, Andropov in 1984, Chernenko in 1985), the Politburo elected him to restore dynamism to a system that even its own analysts privately acknowledged was in trouble.

You are a senior advisor preparing the new General Secretary's first strategic review. The ledger before you is not a comfortable document. The defense budget is consuming an estimated 15–20% of GDP — far more than official figures acknowledge — while consumer goods shortages create visible public frustration. The Soviet Union is in its sixth year of war in Afghanistan with no military victory in sight, against a Mujahideen resistance supplied with increasing quantities of American weapons. And in Washington, President Reagan announced in March 1983 a program — the Strategic Defense Initiative — that, if it works, would render the Soviet nuclear arsenal obsolete and require a technological counter-response the economy cannot afford.

You are reasoning from March 1985. You do not know what 1989 will bring, or 1991. You do not know whether Gorbachev's reforms will save the Soviet system or end it. You know what the ledger says today — and you know Gorbachev is waiting for your assessment.

Unit 8 frame inversion: After seven topics of peoples caught between the superpowers — colonies seeking independence, proxy states choosing sides, resistance movements navigating Cold War pressures — you now sit inside one superpower as it strains under the weight of its own commitments. If you completed the 8.4 BeInTheRoom scenario ("The Harvest Report"), you have seen this system from the county level in Henan Province in 1959. Now you see it from the top. The structural problems visible in that harvest report — chronic information distortion, pressure from above overriding reality from below — are still running twenty-six years later at national scale. Topic 8.9 will ask you to weigh these causes formally; this scenario is where you reason through them from the inside.

Choose Your Role

The Situation Before You
What the Ledger Shows — March 1985

Military spending: officially 5–6% of GDP; internally estimated at 15–20%. The gap is not an accounting error — it is a deliberate concealment that runs through the entire Soviet planning system. Defense industry receives priority allocation of the best materials, engineers, and production capacity. Consumer goods receive what is left. The visible result: chronic shortages of meat, dairy, household goods, and electronics. Citizens know the official figures are false — they can see the shelves.

Afghanistan — Year Six

The Soviet intervention began December 1979. The original aim — stabilizing a communist government on the Soviet border — has not been achieved. The Mujahideen control the countryside; Soviet forces hold the cities and main roads. CIA support through Pakistan has increased each year; the newest reports indicate American-supplied weapons are becoming increasingly sophisticated. Casualty figures are not published in the Soviet press, but the coffins arrive home, and veterans talk. A senior military assessment requested by Andropov in 1983 found no realistic path to military victory within a politically sustainable timeframe. Gorbachev has read it.

SDI and the Arms Race

Reagan announced the Strategic Defense Initiative on March 23, 1983. Soviet military scientists have assessed it seriously. The assessment: SDI may not be technically feasible for decades — but it doesn't need to be. The announcement alone has shifted the terms of the arms race toward space-based precision systems and advanced computing, where the Soviet economy is structurally weakest. Every counter-SDI investment the military demands competes with the consumer goods shortages that are generating public frustration. The arms race has found the Soviet economy's weakest seam — and it is pressing on it.

Examine the Evidence

The strategic review has assembled the relevant evidence. Select at least four chips to read — the evidence you examine shapes how you reason through the decisions ahead. You are reasoning from March 1985: no knowledge of what happens after this date.

4
Decision 1 — The Arms Race
The question before the review: Reagan's SDI has shifted the terms of the arms race toward technologies where the Soviet economy is structurally weakest — advanced computing, precision systems, microelectronics. The Soviet military wants to match the U.S. and develop counter-SDI capabilities. But the defense budget is already consuming resources the civilian economy cannot afford to lose. The new General Secretary must decide: compete in the arms race as it is, try to negotiate it down, or find a different path?
5
Decision 2 — Afghanistan
The question before the review: Six years in, the internal military assessment is clear: there is no realistic path to military victory within a politically sustainable timeframe. The Mujahideen are resilient, CIA support is increasing, and Soviet casualties continue mounting. But withdrawal is its own problem: it signals Soviet limits to Eastern European governments watching carefully for whether Moscow will pay any price to defend its positions. What should the new General Secretary do about Afghanistan?
6
Decision 3 — Reform at Home
The question before the review: The Soviet economy has chronic structural problems — stagnant productivity, consumer goods shortages, a widening technological gap with Western economies, and a defense burden that crowds out civilian investment. Every past attempt at significant economic reform has been reversed or failed to produce the growth it promised (Khrushchev's thaw reversed; Kosygin's 1960s reforms blocked by the party apparatus; Brezhnev's "stability" produced stagnation). Gorbachev has ideas — glasnost (openness) and perestroika (restructuring) — but he has not yet defined what these mean in practice. What should the new General Secretary do?

  • The three causal strands interact: SDI pressure made the arms race more expensive, worsening the economic weakness. Afghanistan drained resources needed for both military competition and economic reform. Economic weakness made it impossible to sustain both the arms race and the Afghan war. Gorbachev's reform response to economic weakness (glasnost) released public discontent that had been building for years. Each strand compounded the others.
  • You are reasoning from March 1985. You do not know whether Gorbachev's reforms will save the system or end it. The historical outcome — the dissolution of the Soviet Union in December 1991 — was not foreseeable from March 1985 with any certainty. Historians who argue the collapse was inevitable are projecting backward; those inside the system in 1985 believed they were making decisions that could go multiple ways.

The system that failed at the county level in 1959 is failing at the national level in 1985.
