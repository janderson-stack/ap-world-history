The Bandung Table

Bandung, Indonesia, April 1955. Twenty-nine newly independent and decolonizing nations are gathering for a conference that no superpower convened and no superpower controls. You are a senior advisor in President Sukarno's foreign ministry. The question is not whether to hold the conference — it's what the conference should become. You have three decisions to make, and no perfect answer to any of them.

Historical Situation

The year is 1955. A decade after the end of World War II, the world's political map is changing faster than any previous generation has seen. India won independence from Britain in 1947. Indonesia won independence from the Netherlands in 1949. Ghana — still called the Gold Coast — is marching toward independence under Kwame Nkrumah, who many of you know from the pan-African organizing circles of the 1940s. Dozens of other nations are at various stages of the same process.

The Cold War is already ten years old. The Korean War killed over three million people and ended in a stalemate. The United States offers development aid — but tied to anti-communist alignment. The Soviet Union offers development aid — but tied to ideological solidarity with Moscow. Both superpowers frame their own agenda as the path to freedom and prosperity for the developing world. Both want your loyalty. Neither is asking what you want.

Sukarno has called a conference in Bandung to create space for a different conversation. Twenty-nine nations are coming. The world is watching. You have three decisions to shape before the closing communiqué is written.

Decision (a): Conference Direction

Should Bandung produce a formal, permanent non-aligned bloc with organizational structure — or stay a loose forum of shared principles with no binding commitments?

Decision (b): Superpower Aid

Both the U.S. and Soviet Union are offering development aid to newly independent states. Accept aid with conditions attached — or refuse both and absorb the economic cost of true independence?

Decision (c): The Communiqué

How strongly should the closing statement condemn colonialism "in all its manifestations"? Some delegates argue Soviet domination of Eastern Europe is also colonialism. Others worry that language will alienate the socialist nations in the room.

Step 2

The Situation Before You

You are advising Sukarno's delegation on all three Bandung decisions. But your most important recommendation concerns the fundamental question: what should the Non-Aligned Movement be? Choose the direction you believe newly independent nations should take.

Your answer to this question will shape how you approach all three conference decisions.

Build a Formal Non-Aligned Bloc

Establish Bandung as the founding moment of a permanent, structured organization. Non-alignment needs institutional power to resist superpower pressure. Principles alone are not enough.

Keep Bandung a Principles Forum

Adopt the ten principles of peaceful coexistence and leave Bandung as a moral statement, not an institution. A loose forum is more inclusive and harder for superpowers to co-opt or disrupt.

Conditional Western Alignment

Accept U.S. development aid on the best terms available. Economic development is the priority, and ideological purity cannot feed a country. Negotiate the best deal possible without full military commitment.

Hard-Line Anti-Colonialism

Refuse all superpower aid. Adopt language condemning colonialism in ALL its forms — including Soviet domination of Eastern Europe. True independence means economic and ideological independence from both blocs.

Step Out of the Room: AP Reflection

Now leave the role and write as a historian. Use what you experienced in this scenario to explain broader patterns about ideology, identity, and resistance to existing orders in the Cold War era.

AP Reflection Prompt
Explain what this scenario reveals about how newly independent nations understood the Cold War's ideological struggle and the role of groups and individuals in opposing existing economic, political, and social orders. In your response, use at least two specific historical details from the scenario — including at least one reference to Sukarno or Kwame Nkrumah — and connect them to the Non-Aligned Movement's role as an alternative to the binary Cold War order.
