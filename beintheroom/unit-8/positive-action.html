Positive Action

Accra, Gold Coast, January 1950. Kwame Nkrumah's Convention People's Party is at a crossroads. The British colonial government has offered limited constitutional reforms through the Coussey Committee report. The CPP's answer has been "Self-Government Now" — but that demand must be backed by action. Nkrumah is weighing Positive Action: a campaign of nonviolent strikes and boycotts that could force the British to negotiate, or could give them a pretext to crush the movement. Colonial officials are already branding the CPP as communist-influenced. The question of how the campaign reads in London and Washington matters as much as what happens in Accra. You have seen what is happening in French Indochina. You know what happened to India three years ago. Nkrumah will be arrested if Positive Action launches. You are the strategist who has to tell him whether to pull the trigger.

The Situation Before You

In February 1948, ex-servicemen returning from World War II marched in Accra demanding the government honor its promises — better pensions, economic opportunity, a share of the prosperity they had fought to defend. Colonial police fired on the marchers. Riots spread across the Gold Coast. The colonial government arrested Kwame Nkrumah and five other UGCC leaders and blamed them for the unrest — then watched in alarm as the arrests only deepened Nkrumah's popular support.

Released from detention, Nkrumah broke from the more conservative United Gold Coast Convention in June 1949 and formed the Convention People's Party. Where the UGCC represented educated professionals seeking gradual constitutional reform, the CPP built a mass movement — market women, workers, students, ex-servicemen — with a single demand: Self-Government Now. The CPP organized across the colony, building a network that the colonial administration could not ignore and could not easily suppress.

The Coussey Committee, appointed by the colonial government to recommend constitutional reform, delivered its report in October 1949. It proposed an expanded Gold Coast legislature with elected African representatives — a significant step, but far short of self-government. The CPP rejected the Coussey proposals as insufficient. In December 1949, Nkrumah announced that the CPP would launch "Positive Action" — a campaign of nonviolent strikes, boycotts, and civil disobedience — unless the British immediately agreed to a full national assembly with the power to draft a constitution for an independent Gold Coast.

It is January 1950. The colonial government has not moved. Positive Action is scheduled to begin. Nkrumah is certain to be arrested when it launches — and he knows it. The CPP's organizers are in place across the colony. The question before the room is not whether the CPP wants self-government. The question is whether this specific campaign, launched now, in this specific Cold War climate, against this specific colonial administration, is the right instrument — and if so, how it should be shaped to work.

Note: You are reasoning from January 1950. You know the Indian precedent. You know about the 1948 riots. You know the Cold War is shaping what London and Washington will tolerate. You do not know that Ghana will become independent in 1957, that Nkrumah will be released from prison to become Prime Minister-elect, or that the Positive Action campaign will succeed. Reason from here — no hindsight.

Decision (a): Method — Launch or Hold?

Launch full Positive Action — nonviolent strikes and boycotts colony-wide — or hold back and accept the Coussey constitutional reforms as a platform for continued negotiation?

Decision (b): Response to Arrests

When Nkrumah and other CPP leaders are arrested — as they surely will be — does the movement escalate pressure, hold the nonviolent line, or negotiate a settlement from a position of demonstrated strength?

Decision (c): Cold War Positioning

How should the CPP frame Positive Action so that London and Washington cannot credibly brand it as Soviet-directed subversion — thereby undercutting the colonial government's ability to justify a crackdown?

Step 2

Choose Your Path

Each path represents a different strategic judgment about what the CPP should do in January 1950. Choose the path that best reflects your role's perspective on what is possible, what is right, and what can actually work against British colonial power in this specific moment.

Launch Full Positive Action

Strike. Boycott. Civil disobedience colony-wide. Demonstrate that the colonial government cannot run the Gold Coast without CPP participation. Accept Nkrumah's arrest as part of the plan — and use it to show the British that arresting the CPP's leadership does not break the movement.

Limited Strike with Safety Valves

Launch targeted strike action in Accra only, with clear public exit conditions: if the colonial government agrees to specific minimum demands within 72 hours, the CPP suspends the campaign. This tests the instrument without committing to a colony-wide confrontation that could turn violent or be misread in London as communist-driven chaos.

Accept the Coussey Reforms

Accept the Coussey Committee's constitutional package as a platform and work within the expanded legislature the British are offering. Build CPP electoral power through the new institutions. Use the 1951 elections — if they come — to demonstrate that the CPP has mass mandate, then renegotiate from inside the system.

Threaten Escalation to Armed Resistance

Signal that if Positive Action fails and Nkrumah is imprisoned without a genuine offer of self-government, the next phase will not be nonviolent. Use the threat of armed resistance — not the action — as leverage. Force the British to calculate what a Gold Coast insurgency would cost, given what is already happening in Malaya and Indochina.
