A Line Through Punjab

New Delhi, India  ·  September 1947

India has been independent for thirty-eight days. The Radcliffe Line — drawn in six weeks and announced only on 17 August, three days after independence — has published the boundary between India and Pakistan. Across Punjab and Bengal, millions of people are moving. Hindus and Sikhs crossing east; Muslims crossing west. In Punjab the movement is becoming a catastrophe: columns of refugees stretching miles, intercommunal attacks on the roads, villages burning, trains arriving at stations filled with the dead. The Punjab Boundary Force — the British-commanded unit meant to hold the line — is overwhelmed and will formally disband within weeks.

You are a senior civil servant in the Indian Administrative Service, serving on the Emergency Committee of the Cabinet chaired by Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru. The committee has been in session for hours. The question before you is not whether the crisis is terrible — that is not in dispute. The question is what the Government of India can actually do about it, right now, with the forces and resources it has, and what the consequences of each course of action will be for the hundreds of thousands of people still in motion.

Step 1 of 6 — Choose Your Role
1
Choose Your Role

Each role on the Emergency Committee holds different information, different obligations, and different instincts about what the government should do. Your choice shapes which pressures you feel most directly throughout the scenario.

2
Examine the Evidence

The Emergency Committee has assembled reports, cables, and intelligence summaries on the crisis. Select at least four chips to read. The evidence you choose shapes how you will reason through the decisions ahead.

3
Decision 1 — Protection of the Columns
The immediate crisis: Columns of refugees — some stretching twenty or thirty miles — are moving across Punjab in both directions. Attacks on the columns are killing hundreds of people per day. The army and police available are not sufficient to escort all columns and also maintain order in cities like Amritsar and Lahore simultaneously. The question before the committee is where to concentrate what protection exists.
4
Decision 2 — Evacuee Property
The property problem: Muslims crossing into Pakistan are leaving homes, farms, and businesses in India. Hindus and Sikhs crossing into India are leaving homes, farms, and businesses in Pakistan. In both countries, the arriving communities are desperate and the departing communities' property is being looted or seized by others. The question is what legal framework India should establish immediately for the property of those who have left — which will also determine what India can ask Pakistan to do for the property of those who left Pakistan.
5
Decision 3 — Coordination with Pakistan
The two-country problem: The crisis is happening on both sides of a border between two newly sovereign states that have just separated after a contested partition. Effective response to the refugee emergency requires coordination — people are crossing from one country into the other. But India and Pakistan are simultaneously competitors, have incompatible claims over Kashmir, and each government is under pressure from its own communal majority not to appear to be protecting the other's nationals at the expense of its own. Nehru wants to demonstrate that India's secular-state model can manage this; Pakistani Prime Minister Liaquat Ali Khan faces the same pressures in reverse.
6
Reflection & Writing

Self-Check: Are these ideas in your thinking?

  • The Partition of India and the creation of Pakistan were the direct result of the redrawing of political boundaries as colonial authority withdrew (KC-6.2.III.A.i).
  • The Radcliffe Line — drawn in six weeks by a colonial official who had never been to India — produced the population displacement you just managed. The boundary decision and the human consequences were inseparable (KC-6.2.III.A.ii).
  • The Emergency Committee's decisions had to balance the impossible: protecting all communities, establishing a secular-state legitimacy, managing an acute crisis with inadequate resources, and coordinating with a newly sovereign rival state.
  • The scale: estimates of 10–20 million people displaced; an estimated 200,000 to 2 million killed. These are not statistics — they are the consequence of a political decision (where to draw the line) made in six weeks.
  • Nehru's government had to make permanent decisions about property, resettlement, and coordination that would shape the relationship between India and Pakistan — and between the two communities — for generations.

Look back at your three decisions. Explain the logic of your choices as a set. What theory were you applying about what a newly independent state can and should do in a crisis created by the boundary that defined its independence? What did you prioritize, and what did you accept as the cost?

Part 2 — Connecting to the CED

The College Board says: KC-6.2.III.A.ii "The redrawing of political boundaries in some cases led to conflict as well as population displacement and/or resettlements, including those related to the Partition of India and the creation of the state of Israel."

Based on what you experienced in this scenario, explain specifically how the Radcliffe Line decision produced the crisis you just tried to manage. What was the connection between the political decision (where to draw the boundary) and the human consequences (conflict, displacement, resettlement)? Then explain one way in which your decisions today will shape the resettlement — that is, how government choices in the crisis itself become part of the historical consequence.

Extension — Comparison with Israel/Palestine

The CED also identifies the creation of the state of Israel in 1948 as a case of boundary redrawing leading to conflict and population displacement (the Nakba: approximately 700,000 Palestinian Arabs displaced). Using what you know about both cases, identify one similarity and one difference in how boundary redrawing produced conflict and displacement in the Partition of India (1947) and the creation of Israel (1948). What does the comparison reveal about the pattern the CED is pointing to?
