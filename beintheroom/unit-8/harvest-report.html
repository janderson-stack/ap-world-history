The Harvest Report

Henan Province, China, autumn 1959. You are the county party secretary. This spring, your county reported a record harvest to the province — numbers that were inflated, but no more inflated than every other county in Henan. The provincial government set your grain procurement quota based on those numbers. Now it is autumn, and the real harvest is in. It is far below what you reported. The state will come to collect what you said you produced. If the villages surrender that much grain, there will be nothing left to eat — and no seed grain for next year's planting. If you report the truth, you may become the next Peng Dehuai.

The Situation Before You

The Great Leap Forward launched in 1958 with a simple promise: China would surpass Britain's steel production within fifteen years, and the countryside would simultaneously deliver record grain harvests. Communes replaced individual farms. Quotas replaced markets. Party secretaries at every level faced pressure from above to demonstrate their ideological commitment through ever-higher production reports.

This spring, your county reported a harvest figure to Henan Province that was ambitious — but no more ambitious than what neighboring counties reported. Everyone was inflating. Everyone knew the game. Provincial officials compiled the county numbers into provincial totals; the provincial totals fed into national targets that Mao cited as proof the Leap was working.

At the Lushan Conference in July and August 1959, Defense Minister Peng Dehuai sent Mao a private letter questioning whether the Great Leap Forward's numbers were real. Mao treated it as a political attack, circulated the letter, and had Peng condemned as a rightist and purged. The signal reached every county party secretary in China: report honest numbers, and this is what happens to you.

Now it is autumn 1959. The real harvest is in. Your county produced approximately 40% of what you reported to the province this spring. The provincial grain procurement quota — the amount the state will physically extract from your county — is based on the inflated spring figure. If your county surrenders the requisitioned grain, the villages will have nothing to eat through the winter. Some of them may not have even seed grain for spring planting.

Note: You are reasoning from autumn 1959. You know what is happening in your county. You do not know the full scale of what is happening across China — that tens of millions are dying, that the famine is already national. You know your county. You know the party's political expectations. You know what happened to Peng Dehuai.

Decision (a): What Do You Report?

File an honest harvest figure — showing the real shortfall — or file a revised figure that still exceeds reality but is lower than the spring report, buying some margin without exposing the full truth?

Decision (b): What Do You Submit to Quota?

Comply fully with the provincial grain procurement quota — extracting what the state demands from your villages, regardless of what that leaves — or resist the quota by submitting less grain and documenting the food security situation?

Decision (c): What Do You Tell the Villages?

Alert village leaders to the food security situation and authorize emergency grain retention — or maintain party discipline and silence, trusting that the system will correct itself before people start dying in your county?

Step 2

Choose Your Path

Your most important decision concerns what you report and what you submit to the grain procurement quota. These are connected: the harvest report determines what the state expects; the procurement submission determines how much the villages actually lose. Choose the path that best reflects your role's position and what you believe is the right — or survivable — thing to do.

File the Honest Report

Submit an accurate autumn harvest figure to the province — even though it reveals your spring numbers were inflated, and even though Peng Dehuai was just purged for doing something similar. Document the food security situation. Let the system know the truth, whatever it costs you.

File a Revised but Still Inflated Report

Submit a figure lower than your spring report but still above actual production — a "correction" that brings the quota down from catastrophic to merely severe, while avoiding the appearance of having falsified your spring numbers. Protect the villages partially, protect yourself from full exposure.

Comply Fully with the Quota

Extract the full requisitioned amount from your villages — exactly what the provincial quota demands — and report successful fulfillment to the province. Demonstrate ideological commitment. Trust that the party will address the food situation through other channels, or that you cannot survive the political consequences of non-compliance.

Delay and Request an Inspection

Request that a provincial agricultural inspector visit your county before procurement is finalized — creating a paper record that the food security situation is known to higher authorities. Buy time without directly reporting honest numbers. Risk that the inspection takes too long, or that the inspector reports you as a problem cadre.

Step 6

Step Out of the Room: AP Reflection

Now leave the role and write as a historian. Use what you experienced in this scenario to explain what the Great Leap Forward reveals about how communist government control of the national economy produced repressive policies — and what those policies meant for the people who lived inside them.

AP Reflection Prompt
Explain how the Great Leap Forward represented communist government control of China's national economy. In your response, use at least two specific historical details from the scenario — including at least one that explains the mechanism by which repressive policies produced negative repercussions for the population — and connect them to the CED key concept: "In communist China, the government controlled the national economy through the Great Leap Forward, often implementing repressive policies, with negative repercussions for the population." Then explain what this scenario reveals about the causes of China's communist revolution: would the peasants who supported Mao in 1949 have predicted the harvest report in 1959? What does the gap reveal about the relationship between revolutionary promises and the consequences of revolutionary government?
