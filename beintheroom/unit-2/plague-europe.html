The Plague Has Arrived

It is 1347. Ships from the Black Sea have docked in Sicily, and sailors are dead or dying, covered in black swellings that weep blood and pus. Within weeks it is clear: something has arrived from the east, carried along the same trade routes that brought silk and spices. In the months that follow, one-third of Europe's population will die. Everyone in this scenario must decide what to do — and almost no one will choose correctly.

Historical Situation

You are living through one of the worst catastrophes in human history. The Black Death does not care about rank, wealth, or virtue. It kills clergy and peasants, rulers and merchants. It arrives along the same routes that brought prosperity. Your task is not to find the correct policy response — there is no correct response in 1347. Your task is to reason from inside a historical position and explain what you do when the world is ending and you don't know why.

Your task is to choose a historical role, describe your response, defend your reasoning with evidence, consider a tradeoff, respond to an AI coach, and then step out of character to write an AP-aligned reflection.

Knowledge vs. FearMedieval medicine cannot explain the plague. The gap between what people know and what is happening fills with fear, religion, and scapegoating. What happens when knowledge fails?
Connectivity vs. SafetyThe same trade routes that built prosperity now carry death. Closing the ports might stop the plague. It would also stop everything else. Which do you choose?
Survival vs. CommunityPeople who flee save themselves. People who stay to care for the sick often die. What obligations do you have to others when survival is uncertain?

Step 2

Your Situation

You cannot choose whether the plague arrives. You can only choose how you respond. There is no correct answer — only choices and consequences.

Each option has benefits and consequences. There is no simple correct answer. Your response must fit your role and use historical evidence.

Impose Strict Quarantine and Close the Ports

Close the city gates and ports. Cut trade connections. Accept the economic consequences to stop the spread. Know that this also cuts the food supply and isolates the sick from outside help.

Maintain Medical and Religious Care at Personal Risk

Stay and care for the sick — as physician, cleric, or neighbor. Accept the personal risk. Understand that without caregivers, the social fabric completely collapses.

Flee the City to Survive

Leave the city before the plague reaches full intensity. Protect yourself and those you can take with you. Know that fleeing may carry the disease to wherever you go.

Seek Explanations — Religious, Medical, or Scientific

Invest in understanding what the plague is and how it spreads. Challenge the wrong explanations — including scapegoating — even when doing so is dangerous and unpopular.
