The Ships in the Harbor at Messina

The Routes That Carry Everything Also Carry This

Messina, Sicily, October 1347 CE AP Skill: Causation LO-K KC-3.1.IV

You are Marco di Catania, Port Captain and public health officer of Messina. Twelve Genoese ships have arrived from Caffa on the Black Sea. Most of the sailors are dead. The rats are already in the grain warehouses. The merchants want their cargo. The council is watching. You have to issue an order.

Step 1 — Enter the Room

Historical Situation

Twelve Genoese trading ships arrived in the harbor of Messina three days ago. They came from Caffa, on the Black Sea — a Genoese trading colony on the far eastern edge of the Mediterranean world, the terminus of the overland routes that run all the way to China.

The harbormaster who processed their arrival sent you a message before dawn. He would not go near the ships again.

You have now seen what he saw. Most of the sailors are dead. The ones still alive are covered in black swellings — at the armpits, the groin, the neck — that weep blood and pus. The ships reek. The rats that came off the mooring lines before anyone could stop them are already in the grain warehouses near the docks.

You are Marco di Catania, Port Captain and public health officer of Messina. You have been managing this harbor for eleven years. You have seen plague ships before — the standard protocol is a forty-day quarantine period outside the harbor, quarantina, during which the crew either recovers or dies without infecting the city. But the quarantina protocol has never been tested against something like this.

The Genoese merchants who own the cargo aboard these ships are already at your office. They are not sick. They arrived in a separate vessel two days ahead of the fleet and have been lodging in the city since. Their cargo — spices, silk, and a quantity of porcelain — is worth a fortune, and they want it unloaded. They are threatening to go to the municipal council and argue that you are exceeding your authority.

The municipal council is already nervous. Messina is a trading city. Its wealth comes from the harbor. Every week a ship is quarantined outside the walls is a week of lost revenue, and the merchants of Messina have powerful friends. One council member has already sent you a note suggesting that if the sailors look healthy enough to be questioned, they look healthy enough to dock.

You have one more piece of information the council does not yet have. A letter arrived this morning from the port of Catania, forty miles south. Three ships from the same fleet — which stopped there first — have been impounded. The city of Catania reports that several dock workers who handled the mooring lines have fallen ill with swellings. The letter was written four days ago.

The rats are already in the grain warehouses.

What do you do?

Step 2 — Your Role

Marco di Catania, Port Captain and Public Health Officer of Messina

Eleven years managing this harbor Authority subject to municipal council Incomplete information

You are not a doctor. You are a logistics manager and an administrator, and your authority in this matter is real but limited. The municipal council can override you. The merchants have lawyers. And you have no way to know — no one does — exactly what you are looking at.

What you know:

  • The trade routes that made Messina wealthy are the same routes that brought these ships here. Caffa sits at the end of the overland Silk Roads from China. What travels those routes does not stop at the goods in the hold.
  • The forty-day quarantine protocol, the quarantina, was developed after previous plague ships. It has never been tested against a disease of this apparent speed or severity.
  • The Genoese merchants are prominent figures in Mediterranean trade. Offending them has consequences far beyond this harbor.
  • The city of Catania is forty miles away and already reporting illness. The letter took four days to arrive.
  • The rats are in the grain warehouses.

What you cannot know:

  • How many people in Messina were already exposed before you took action.
  • Whether the quarantina protocol would have stopped this even if applied immediately on arrival.
  • What is happening in the rest of Sicily — or in Genoa, or in North Africa, or in the ports of France, toward which other ships from Caffa are already sailing.

You will make a decision with incomplete information. That is what this job requires.

Step 3 — The Decision

What Does Port Captain di Catania Order?

Should the Genoese ships be removed from the harbor and all contact with the city suspended — or should the merchants' cargo be allowed to move under conditional restrictions?

Choose the position you can best defend with evidence from the documents. A conditional middle course is acceptable but must be clearly justified.

Position A — Full Quarantine and Removal

The evidence is not ambiguous. The sailors are visibly dying. The city of Catania has already reported illness in dock workers who had contact with the same fleet. The rats — the probable vector — are already ashore.

Whatever this disease is, it travels with the ships, and the routes that brought these ships from Caffa to Messina brought it from somewhere much further east. The forty-day quarantina protocol exists for exactly this situation. Messina's long-term value as a trade city depends on it being a safe city. No merchant will dock here if the harbor is known to kill.

Evidence: KC-3.1.IV — trade routes as vectors for pathogen diffusion

Position B — Conditional Access with Restrictions

The cargo is already in Messina, in a sense — the Genoese merchants themselves are walking the streets. A full quarantine that extends to the cargo and the docks will destroy this season's trade and set a precedent that makes Messina's harbor unpredictable to every future merchant.

The sick sailors should be isolated. The cargo, sealed in crates in the holds, poses less risk than the merchants already ashore. Controlled unloading, with dock workers kept away from the crew, is a reasonable middle course. The quarantina protocol may be excessive for what is, so far, a localized outbreak.

Evidence: KC-3.1.IV · KC-3.3.II — trade disruption and city decline

Step 4 — Read the Evidence

Primary Source Documents

Read all four documents. Your written order must cite evidence from at least two. Strong arguments name the source and explain why it supports your decision — not just what it says.

Document 1

Report from the Harbormaster, Messina, October 1347

"Captain. I have inspected the Genoese ships at anchor and I will not return to them. The crew of the lead vessel numbers perhaps forty men; I counted perhaps twelve still standing when I called across the water. Those who answered me showed black swellings at the neck and beneath the arms, weeping fluid. Several told me they had sailed from Caffa after a siege by Mongol forces who were themselves dying of this same sickness — and that the Mongols had thrown their dead over the walls into the city before withdrawing. I do not know what this is. I know I would not touch a mooring line from these ships for any cargo in the Mediterranean."
— Written and delivered to the Port Captain's office before dawn, three days after the fleet's arrival
AP Connection: KC-3.1.IV — the plague's point of origin on the Central Asian trade routes; the Mongol siege of Caffa as the mechanism of transmission into the Mediterranean trading network.
Document 2

Petition from the Genoese Merchants' Guild, Messina, October 1347

"Port Captain di Catania. The cargo aboard our fleet represents eighteen months of trade across the Black Sea and overland routes to the east. The spices, silks, and porcelain in our holds are contracted to buyers in Messina, Palermo, and Naples. We arrived in good health and have had no contact with the sick sailors since leaving Caffa. The city's quarantine protocols apply to crew, not cargo, and our lawyers are prepared to argue this distinction before the council. We ask only that our cargo be unloaded. The sailors may remain aboard under whatever restrictions you deem necessary."
— Delivered to the Port Captain and copied to the municipal council
AP Connection: The luxury goods trade (silk, spice, porcelain) driving the commercial practices that sustained Afro-Eurasian exchange networks; the entanglement of commercial interest and disease spread.
Document 3

Letter from the Port Authority of Catania, Sicily, October 1347

"To the authorities of Messina. We write to inform you that three vessels of the Genoese fleet from Caffa stopped in this harbor before proceeding north. We were unaware of the condition of their crew until two days after docking. Six of our dock workers who handled the mooring lines and assisted with unloading have now fallen ill with swellings of the neck and groin, high fever, and the blackening of extremities. Two have died. We have impounded the ships and suspended dock operations. We urge all ports receiving vessels from this fleet to take immediate precautionary measures. God protect us all."
— Written six days after the first ships docked at Catania; received in Messina four days after writing
AP Connection: KC-3.1.IV — the plague spreading along established Mediterranean trade routes from port to port; the speed of transmission relative to the speed of information.
Document 4

Sicilian Agricultural Census Record, c. 1340 (excerpted)

"The grain stores of Messina support an estimated population of 35,000 persons within the city walls. The city imports additional foodstuffs — citrus fruits from North African trade partners, dried fish from Adriatic suppliers, spices from eastern routes via Genoa and Venice. The city's food security depends on the continued operation of its harbor and the trade networks it connects. Disruption of harbor operations for more than thirty days has historically produced food price instability and shortages in the winter months."
— From regional administrative records of the Kingdom of Sicily, summarized for the Port Captain's reference files
AP Connection: KC-3.1.IV — the same trade networks that diffused crops (citrus, new foodstuffs) into the Mediterranean diet now serve as the vector for pathogen spread; the environmental consequences of connectivity cut in both directions.
Step 5 — Issue the Order

Your Task: Issue the Order

The council is waiting. The merchants are waiting. The ships are in the harbor. Write your decision and your justification — the order you are issuing as Port Captain and the reasoning you will use to defend it.

Your response should:

  1. State your decision clearly — quarantine and remove the ships, allow conditional unloading, or another defensible course of action. You must commit to something.
  2. Explain the causes of the plague's spread along trade routes. How did this disease get from Central Asia to Messina? What features of the trade networks made that possible?
  3. Identify at least two effects of the plague's spread on human societies — economic, demographic, social, or political. Use evidence from the documents.
  4. Address the tradeoff — what does Messina lose if you quarantine? What does it risk if you don't?
Target length: 150–250 words • You are writing an official order, not a reflective essay. Be direct. Name the stakes. You are not just managing a harbor — you are making a decision that will affect 35,000 people.
