Unit 2 Topic 2.2 BeInTheRoom

At the Court of
the Ilkhan

Tabriz, Ilkhanate · c. 1275 CE · The Mongol Empire and the Making of the Modern World

The Room

"The lamp oil smells like almonds."

You are sitting in an antechamber of the Ilkhan's palace in Tabriz — a city that, thirty years ago, your grandfather helped rebuild after the Mongols sacked it. The walls around you are covered in Persian tilework: deep blue, turquoise, the color of a clear sky after rain. The craftsmen who laid these tiles were Persian. The script above the doorway is Persian. But the man who commissioned all of it — Ilkhan Abaqa, grandson of Hülegü, great-grandson of Chinggis Khan — is Mongol.

This is the strangeness you live inside every day.

It is 1275 CE. The Mongol Empire that once stretched from Korea to Poland has fractured into four khanates. The Ilkhanate controls Persia, Iraq, and much of Anatolia. Abaqa is not a savage — he is a pragmatist. He employs Persian administrators, subsidizes Persian scholars, and has allowed the great libraries that survived the sack of Baghdad to be rebuilt. His court at Tabriz is becoming, improbably, a center of intellectual life.

But there is trouble.

Two problems have landed on your desk this week. First: a delegation of Mongol generals has arrived, angry that Persian governors in several provinces are collecting taxes using old Abbasid-era methods — methods the generals call corrupt and insufficiently loyal to the Yam postal and supply system the Mongols use to hold the empire together. They want the Persian administrators replaced with Mongol-appointed officials who can be trusted to feed the relay stations and the armies.

Second: a group of Persian scholars and physicians — men who saved medical texts when the House of Wisdom in Baghdad burned — are asking for a formal royal charter to establish a new academy in Tabriz. They argue that the knowledge they carry could make the Ilkhanate more powerful than any military conquest. They want resources. They want protection. They want the Ilkhan's name on their founding document.

Abaqa has summoned you to advise him. He can only act on one petition today. The other will have to wait — and waiting, in a court like this, can mean dying.

"Which petition do I grant?" he asks. "And what does my answer say about what kind of empire this will be?"

Your Role

Rashid al-Mawsili

Deputy Administrator of Records — Daftar-Nayib · Ilkhanate Court, Tabriz

Background

You were trained in Mosul as a physician and logician before the Mongol invasions disrupted everything. You survived. You found work translating Arabic and Persian medical texts into forms the Mongol administrators could use, and your usefulness to the court grew from there. You now hold the title of daftar-nayib — deputy administrator of records — which means you see everything: tax rolls, military supply requests, diplomatic correspondence, and the petitions that pile up outside the Ilkhan's door.

You are not Mongol. You are not fully trusted by the Mongol generals. But you are not fully trusted by the Persian nobility either — you work for the conquerors. You live in the space between.

What You Know

  • The Yam system — the Mongols' relay network of postal stations — is genuinely brilliant. It allows the Ilkhan to communicate across vast distances in days, not weeks. But it requires a constant supply of horses, food, and labor from the local population. When governors stop feeding it, the whole communication network degrades.
  • The Persian administrative traditions are old and sophisticated. Abbasid-era tax collection methods were refined over centuries. They are not inherently corrupt — but they prioritize local stability over imperial extraction, which creates friction with Mongol military demands.
  • The scholars asking for a royal charter carry something irreplaceable. You have read some of what they saved from Baghdad: Greco-Islamic medical texts, mathematical treatises, astronomical tables. This knowledge does not exist anywhere else in quite this form. If it is not institutionalized — given a home, students, resources — it will die with the men who carry it in their heads.
  • You have seen what happens when Mongol rulers refuse to adapt to local institutions: revolts, economic collapse, the slow erosion of legitimacy. You have also seen what happens when they adapt too eagerly: the generals grow resentful, call the ruler weak, and the succession crises begin.

Loyalties & Limits

  • You serve the Ilkhanate because it is the power that exists. But you are a Persian scholar at heart. You believe that institutions outlast armies.
  • You have never commanded soldiers or managed a military supply chain. Your knowledge of the Yam system comes from paperwork, not experience.
  • The generals see you as a civilian bureaucrat. The scholars see you as a collaborator with the conquerors. Both assessments are partly right.

Your Task

Ilkhan Abaqa has asked you to advise him on which petition to grant — and what that choice reveals about what kind of empire the Ilkhanate will become. Write your counsel.

Your response must do all of the following:

  1. 1
    Take a clear position. Which petition do you recommend granting, and why? You may argue for one, or propose a specific condition under which you would support one over the other. But you must commit to a recommendation. Abaqa cannot act on a hedge.
  2. 2
    Use at least two specific pieces of evidence drawn from what you know about the Mongol Empire and its relationship to the peoples it conquered. Name systems, institutions, cultural transfers, or historical patterns. Vague claims about "the Mongols being powerful" will not satisfy the Ilkhan.
  3. 3
    Acknowledge your role. Rashid al-Mawsili sees this situation through a particular lens. How does who you are — a Persian scholar-administrator caught between two worlds — shape what you can and cannot see clearly? Name it explicitly.
  4. 4
    Name one tradeoff. Whatever you recommend, something is given up. What does the Ilkhan lose by following your advice? Be honest about the cost.
  5. 5
    Address the opposing view. The generals who want to replace the Persian administrators would say what, exactly? The scholars who want their charter would say what? Engage the stronger of the two objections directly and explain why it does not change your recommendation.

There is no single correct answer. The Ilkhan will judge your reasoning, not just your conclusion.

Evidence

Use these cards to build your argument. Each one gives you historical context Rashid al-Mawsili would know. Tap a card to expand it.

The Mongols' most underappreciated innovation was not military — it was administrative. The Yam system was a vast relay network of postal stations stretching across the empire, positioned roughly every twenty to forty miles along major routes. Each station maintained fresh horses, food, and riders. An imperial messenger could cross thousands of miles in days. The Yam system was the nervous system of the empire: without it, the Ilkhan in Tabriz could not reliably communicate with his subordinates in Baghdad, coordinate military movements, or collect tax revenues from distant provinces.

But the Yam required constant inputs from the local population — horses, grain, labor. When conquered peoples refused or were unable to supply these stations, the system degraded. This is why the Mongols were fiercely protective of their supply lines and deeply suspicious of local administrative systems that diverted resources toward local stability rather than imperial extraction.

The Mongols also adopted the Uyghur script to write their own language — a practical act of standardization that allowed documents and orders to move across the empire in a consistent form, even when the spoken languages of administrators varied widely.

Consider: What does the Yam system tell you about what the Mongols valued most in governance? What does it require from the peoples they conquered — and what happens when those requirements conflict with local traditions?

The Mongol conquests were genuinely catastrophic. The sack of Baghdad in 1258 — when Hülegü's forces destroyed the Abbasid Caliphate — killed hundreds of thousands of people, burned the House of Wisdom, and ended a five-century tradition of Islamic political power in the Arab world. The destruction of cities across Central Asia, Persia, and China reshaped demographics for generations.

And yet: the Mongols were also, within their own lifetimes, among the most significant facilitators of cross-cultural exchange in Eurasian history. Once conquest was complete, the Mongols typically allowed conquered peoples to continue their own religious practices, employed local administrators who knew how to run complex bureaucracies, and actively moved scholars, artisans, physicians, and engineers across the empire to places where their skills were needed.

This apparent paradox — destroy, then integrate — was not accidental. It reflected a Mongol logic: conquest demonstrated power, but administration required expertise that the Mongols themselves often did not have. The Ilkhanate in Persia was a laboratory for this tension. Hülegü's successors patronized Persian poets, employed Persian administrators, and allowed Islamic legal traditions to function alongside Mongol customary law.

Consider: How do you explain a single empire that both destroys the House of Wisdom and rebuilds institutions of learning a generation later? What does this tell you about the relationship between military power and long-term governance?

One of the most consequential effects of the Mongol Empire was the acceleration of knowledge transfer across Afro-Eurasia. The Mongols themselves were less interested in ideas than in practical results — they employed scholars because scholars were useful, not because the Mongols were intellectually curious in the abstract. But the effects of that pragmatism were profound.

Greco-Islamic medical knowledge — much of it preserved in Arabic translation from ancient Greek originals — moved westward along Mongol trade and communication routes into Europe, where it was eventually translated into Latin and integrated into European university medicine. Islamic numerical systems (including what Europeans would later call "Arabic numerals" and the concept of zero) also traveled westward during this period, transforming European mathematics and commerce. These were not gifts freely given — they were byproducts of the violent connectivity the Mongol Empire created.

The scholars in Tabriz who are asking for a royal charter are the living nodes of this transfer network. They carry texts, methods, and intellectual traditions that were rescued from the destruction of Baghdad. If they are institutionalized — given students, resources, protection — they become a transmission point. If they are not, this knowledge remains trapped in individual memory, one death away from disappearing.

Consider: The Mongol Empire is often described as both destroyer and transmitter of learning. How can both be true? And who benefits from which version of this story being emphasized?

Before You Write

1

Commit to a recommendation

Abaqa needs an answer, not a meditation. State your recommendation in your first sentence, then defend it. A response that builds slowly to a conclusion reads as uncertain — and uncertain advisors don't last long in Mongol courts.

2

Evidence names the thing

"The Mongols were good at governance" is not evidence. "The Yam relay system allowed the Ilkhan to communicate across the empire in days, but required constant local provisioning that Persian governors were diverting" is evidence. Name the system. Name the practice. Name the place.

3

Your role is a lens, not a costume

You aren't Rashid al-Mawsili to pretend — you are him to think. He sits between two worlds. That position gives him unusual insight into both military and institutional logic. It also creates real blind spots. Acknowledging what you can't see from where you stand is precision, not weakness.

4

Tradeoffs are honesty

Every recommendation costs something. If you advise the scholars' charter, you are implicitly deprioritizing the generals' supply chain concerns. If you advise replacing the Persian administrators, you are sacrificing institutional knowledge built over centuries. Name the cost — Abaqa needs to know what he is actually choosing.

5

The opposing view has real weight

The generals are not stupid. The scholars are not naive. Before you dismiss either side, ask: what is the strongest version of the argument you are rejecting? If you can't explain why the other side is plausible, you haven't understood the problem.
