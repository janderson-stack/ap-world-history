At the Harbor of Kilwa

Where Every Wind Is a Decision

Kilwa Kisiwani · c. 1310 CE AP Skill: Causation LO E · F · G Scenario 3 of 3 · Unit 2

The monsoon has brought gifts this week. Three dhows from Hormuz arrived at dawn — Persian merchants bearing glass, copper ingots, and a new shipment of cotton cloth from Gujarat. By midday, a vessel from Calicut had docked with pepper and cinnamon. And now, as the afternoon wind shifts and the harbor haze softens the skyline, an extraordinary ship sits anchored at the outer harbor.

She is Chinese. The largest vessel you have seen in twenty years of harbor work — a three-masted junk with red-lacquered railings and a hull deep enough to carry a fortune in porcelain. Her captain, a man named Chen Wulin, arrived at the customs house this morning bearing silk samples, a letter of introduction from a trading house in Quanzhou, and a proposal that has left you sitting quietly for the better part of an hour. Chen Wulin mentioned in passing that his trading house has expanded its Indian Ocean operations since the overland routes through Central Asia became — his word — “uncertain.”

Chen Wulin wants a long-term partnership. Not a single season’s trade — a formal arrangement. Kilwa would become the Chinese vessel’s designated port of call on the East African coast. In exchange: guaranteed access to Chinese porcelain, silk, and copper at fixed prices for five years. Exclusive berth rights. And a promise that Chen’s trading house would bring three additional vessels next season.

It is extraordinary. And it is a problem.

The Arab and Persian merchants who built this harbor — who have traded here for generations, whose diasporic communities have shaped Kilwa’s culture, its mosques, its language — are watching. Their representative, a Shirazi merchant named Hassan al-Farsi, has already sent word to your offices: if the sultan signs an exclusive arrangement with the Chinese, the Hormuz network will take its trade to Mombasa.

Sultan Ibrahim ibn Sulayman has summoned you. He trusts your knowledge of the winds, the routes, the merchants, and the math. The decision is his. The counsel is yours.

Enter the Room

Historical Situation

You are entering one of the most consequential trade decisions in fourteenth-century East Africa. Kilwa Kisiwani sits at the center of the Indian Ocean world — connected by monsoon winds to Arabia, Persia, India, and now China. The city's wealth flows from controlling the gold trade out of the African interior. Its political legitimacy rests on being a reliable, open port for every merchant who arrives with the seasonal winds.

In this scenario, you are not choosing a role. You are Amara ibn Sulayman — harbor master, trade counselor, the person the sultan trusts most. Your task: read the situation, study the evidence, select a position, and write an advisory response to Sultan Ibrahim ibn Sulayman. Your argument must be historically grounded, acknowledge real tradeoffs, and demonstrate causal reasoning about the forces that made this moment possible.

Your Role

Amara ibn Sulayman

Chief Harbor Master & Trade Counselor to Sultan Ibrahim ibn Sulayman of Kilwa

You are the sultan's most trusted trade advisor — the person who knows which ships carry what cargo, which merchants can be trusted, and which winds bring opportunity. Your family has served the harbor for three generations.

You understand something that many advisors miss: Kilwa's wealth is not just about goods. It is about position. Kilwa sits at the junction of the monsoon routes that connect East Africa to Arabia, Persia, India, and now China. Every season, the north monsoon brings ships from the north and east; the south monsoon reverses the flow and carries them home. Your harbor operates because every merchant in the Indian Ocean knows that Kilwa is reliable, neutral, and prosperous.

That reputation took generations to build. What happens to it if the sultan makes the wrong choice today?

  • You serve the sultan — your recommendation must account for Kilwa's long-term political stability, not just this season's profit.
  • The Arab-Persian merchants are not just trading partners. Many have lived in Kilwa for decades. Their mosques, their language, their marriages into Swahili families — they are part of this city.
  • Chinese technology is genuinely impressive. You have seen the junk's navigation instruments. You know Chen Wulin's ship could carry five times what a dhow carries.
  • Hassan al-Farsi's threat to move the Hormuz network to Mombasa is credible. Mombasa is growing.

Step 1 — Choose Your Position

The Central Question

Should Sultan Ibrahim grant Chen Wulin’s Chinese trading house a long-term exclusive partnership — or should Kilwa protect its relationship with the established Arab-Persian trade network?

Read both positions below. You may advocate for Position A, Position B, or propose a justified middle path — but you must defend your choice with specific historical evidence. Select the position closest to what you will argue.

Position A

Accept the Chinese Partnership

Kilwa’s growth depends on expanding its reach and diversifying its trading relationships. Chinese technology — larger ships, superior compass navigation, new cargo capacity — represents the next stage of Indian Ocean development. The monsoon winds already bring Chinese vessels this far; formalizing that relationship gives Kilwa first-mover advantage on the East African coast. The Arab-Persian merchants may threaten to leave, but Kilwa’s harbor, its geographic position, and its fresh water make it indispensable. They will return.

Position B

Protect the Arab-Persian Network

Kilwa’s wealth, its political legitimacy, and its cultural identity were built through decades of integration with Arab and Persian merchants. The diasporic communities these merchants established are not just economic assets — they are the social fabric of the city. An exclusive Chinese arrangement could rupture those relationships permanently. Kilwa’s power comes from being a reliable node in an existing network, not from gambling on a single new partner. Take the Chinese goods as seasonal trade, but do not sign an exclusive agreement.

Harbor Records Excerpt
From Kilwa’s customs house records, c. 1305–1310
In the past five seasons, the port of Kilwa has received vessels from: Hormuz (Persia), Aden (Yemen), Calicut (India), Quilon (India), and on two occasions, Quanzhou (China). The trade in gold from the interior — secured through the overland routes from Great Zimbabwe — continues to attract Persian and Indian buyers. Letter from Hassan al-Farsi
Delivered to the harbor master’s office the morning of Chen Wulin’s arrival
“To Amara ibn Sulayman, greetings. I write as representative of the Shirazi and Hormuz merchants who have traded at Kilwa for forty years. We have built mosques here. Our children were born here. We are not visitors — we are residents. I must inform you plainly: if the sultan grants exclusivity to the Chinese, the merchants of Hormuz, Aden, and Siraf will redirect their routes to Mombasa. We do not make this threat lightly. We make it because Kilwa’s value to us is as an open port, not a Chinese satellite.”

Chen Wulin’s Proposal (translated excerpt)
Presented at the Kilwa customs house, written in Arabic with a Chinese seal

In exchange, we request: (1) guaranteed berth priority for our vessels, (2) a fixed customs rate of 4% for the duration of the agreement, and (3) first right of refusal on Kilwa’s gold and ivory exports. Document 4
Seasonal Wind Chart (Descriptive)
From the harbor master’s reference notes, maintained across generations
“The northeast monsoon arrives in November and carries ships from Arabia, Persia, and India southward along the coast. It reverses in April, becoming the southwest monsoon, which carries vessels northward and homeward. A ship from Quanzhou, China, can reach Kilwa by sailing with the northeast monsoon from the Straits of Malacca to Ceylon to the Malabar Coast to the East African coast — a journey of several months. The return journey reverses the route entirely. Any merchant who does not understand the monsoon calendar cannot operate profitably on these routes.”

Step 3 — Write Your Advisory

Advise the Sultan

You have read the documents. You know the trade winds. You know what is at stake. Write your advisory response to Sultan Ibrahim. Your voice should be deliberate and grounded — you are advising a sultan, not writing a modern essay.

  1. State your position clearly — Accept the Chinese partnership, or protect the Arab-Persian network? If you propose a middle path, you must justify it with evidence.
  2. Identify at least two causes of Indian Ocean trade growth that make Kilwa’s decision significant. What forces created this moment?
  3. Use specific evidence from at least two of the documents above.
  4. Acknowledge the tradeoff — what does Kilwa risk with your recommendation? Who might oppose it, and why?
