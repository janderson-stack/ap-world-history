At the Court of Mali: Gold, Salt, and the Desert Road

It is the early 14th century. Mali is the wealthiest empire in the world, built on the gold-salt trade that crosses the Sahara. Mansa Musa sits on the throne — and he is planning a pilgrimage to Mecca that will announce Mali's wealth and his faith to the entire Muslim world. Everyone in this court has something at stake in what he does next.

Historical Situation

You are at the court of the most powerful ruler in West Africa. Mali's wealth flows from the gold-salt trade crossing the Sahara — and from the political and religious choices that make that trade possible. Mansa Musa's pilgrimage to Mecca is not just a religious act. It is a political announcement. Everyone in this court must decide what they want from it, and what they fear it will bring.

Your task is to choose a historical role, recommend a policy, defend your reasoning with evidence, consider a tradeoff, respond to an AI coach, and then step out of character to write an AP-aligned reflection.

Wealth vs. StabilityMali's gold makes it powerful. But wealth attracts rivals, creates inflation when dispersed carelessly, and requires constant management of the trade routes that produce it.
Islam vs. Local TraditionMansa Musa's Islam is sincere and politically useful. But Mali governs diverse peoples whose traditional religious practices predate Islam. How much conversion can you demand?
Trade vs. ControlThe trans-Saharan routes make Mali rich. But those routes also bring outside merchants, scholars, and ideas that challenge local authority. Openness and control are in constant tension.

Step 2

Central Dilemma

The court must decide how Mali should project its power, wealth, and identity to the wider world. What approach best serves Mali's long-term interests — and your role's position within the empire?

Each option has benefits and consequences. There is no simple correct answer. Your recommendation must fit your role and use historical evidence.

Invest in Islamic Scholarship and Timbuktu

Fund mosques, madrasas, and the scholars of Timbuktu. Position Mali as the intellectual center of West African Islam and attract scholars from across the Muslim world.

Expand and Protect Trade Routes

Invest in camel technology, caravan infrastructure, and route security. The gold-salt trade is Mali's foundation — protect and expand it.

Use the Hajj to Project Mali's Power

Use Mansa Musa's hajj as a diplomatic spectacle — distribute gold generously across North Africa and Arabia, announce Mali's wealth, and build relationships with rulers from Egypt to Mecca.

Balance Islamic Governance with Local Traditions

Govern through Islamic law in cities while allowing traditional practices in rural areas. Use both Islamic scholars and traditional leaders as local intermediaries.
