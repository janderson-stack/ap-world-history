The Weight of Empire — Governing the Inca Road

You are an apu — a provincial governor — in Tawantinsuyu, the Inca Empire. The state at Cusco has issued a new mit'a obligation: your province must supply labor for a major expansion of the Qhapaq Ñan, the Inca road system. Roads tie the empire together — they move armies, messages, goods, and officials across thousands of miles of mountains and valleys. But the draft falls during planting season, and your communities depend on agricultural labor to fill state storehouses and feed their families.

You must decide how to fulfill the state obligation while managing the burden on local people. The wrong choice could mean famine, revolt, or a report to Cusco that ends your authority. The right choice — if there is one — requires you to balance state loyalty against community survival.

Step 2

Central Dilemma

The apu must decide: How should you, as provincial governor, fulfill the state's mit'a demand for road labor while managing its impact on your communities?

Each option has benefits and consequences. There is no simple correct answer. Your recommendation must fit your role and use historical evidence.

Full State Compliance

Deliver the full mit'a quota on schedule as Cusco demands. Road construction proceeds at full capacity.

Stagger the Draft

Rotate labor obligations across communities in shifts, so no single community loses all its agricultural hands at once.

Appeal to Cusco for an Adjusted Quota

Send a quipu report explaining the agricultural timing conflict and request a reduced or delayed obligation.

Redirect Labor to Local Storehouses First

Prioritize filling state storehouses with local agricultural surplus before releasing labor for the road.
