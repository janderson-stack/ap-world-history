First & 10 Reading

Running an Empire

Between c. 1450 and c. 1750, the Ottoman, Mughal, Qing, and other land-based empires faced a shared challenge: how do you govern millions of diverse subjects across vast territories without modern communications, a reliable bureaucracy, or the loyalty of local elites? Each empire developed distinctive administrative innovations — but all combined coercion with accommodation, recognizing that incorporating existing power structures was often more effective than replacing them.

1

Contextualization

The Challenge of Governing Diverse Empires

Imagine you are an emperor in c. 1500, ruling an empire that spans hundreds of thousands of square miles. Your subjects speak dozens of languages, follow different religions, and owe their primary loyalty to local lords, tribal leaders, and community elders — not to you. You have no telephone, no telegraph, no printing press producing loyalist newspapers, and no professional civil service staffed by university graduates trained in imperial governance. How do you hold it all together?

This was the central challenge of every land-based empire between c. 1450 and c. 1750. The Ottoman Empire stretched from Algeria to Iraq, from the Danube River to Yemen. The Mughal Empire governed the Indian subcontinent — hundreds of millions of people across multiple religions, castes, and languages. The Qing Dynasty ruled China, the largest economy on earth, alongside Manchu, Mongol, Tibetan, and Turkic populations across Inner Asia. Each empire needed to collect taxes, recruit soldiers, administer justice, and prevent revolt — all without the tools of modern states.

The solutions each empire developed were not random. They were responses to specific geographic, demographic, and political challenges. Understanding why each administrative system looked the way it did is more important for AP analysis than simply memorizing its name.

AP Thinking — Contextualization

Before analyzing specific administrative systems, place them in context: all empires c. 1450–1750 faced the same fundamental problems of scale, diversity, and loyalty. Administrative innovation was a response to these shared challenges — not arbitrary invention. When you explain devshirme or mansabdar on an AP exam, connect the system to the problem it solved.

2

Key Concept

Bureaucracy and Taxation: The Backbone of Empire

Every empire needed revenue. Armies required pay. Courts required luxury goods to demonstrate imperial power. Infrastructure — roads, fortifications, irrigation — required investment. The fundamental problem of taxation was not how to extract resources from subjects but how to do so without triggering revolt or allowing tax collectors to accumulate independent wealth.

The Ottoman timar system granted military commanders the right to collect taxes from a designated agricultural district (a timar) in exchange for providing cavalry soldiers and maintaining order. Timars were not inheritable property — they were conditional grants that returned to the sultan when the holder died or was reassigned. This kept revenue flowing to the imperial center and prevented local commanders from becoming hereditary nobles whose loyalty to the sultan might waver over generations.

The Mughal jagir system operated on a similar principle. Jagirs were revenue assignments — the right to collect taxes from a territory — granted to ranked imperial officials (mansabdars) in exchange for maintaining military forces. Like Ottoman timars, jagirs were non-hereditary: they reverted to the emperor and were redistributed when a mansabdar died or fell out of favor. The key insight is that non-hereditary revenue rights prevented the rise of a permanently entrenched noble class that might challenge imperial authority.

Empires also used tax farming, a system in which private individuals purchased the right to collect taxes in a region, paid a fixed sum to the imperial treasury up front, and kept any surplus they collected. Tax farming was practical — it required less imperial administrative capacity than direct collection — but it was also exploitative. Tax farmers had every incentive to squeeze as much revenue as possible from the population, sometimes causing hardship and resentment that threatened imperial stability.

AP Thinking — Causation

The non-hereditary design of timar and jagir systems was not accidental. Rulers deliberately structured revenue rights to flow through imperial appointment rather than birth — because hereditary rights created independent nobles who threatened imperial power. When you explain these systems on an AP exam, explain the cause: rulers needed loyal officials more than they needed satisfied nobles.

3

Key Concept

Military Recruitment and Loyalty

Armies were the ultimate instrument of imperial power, but they were also the ultimate threat to it. A general with a loyal army and independent resources could overthrow an emperor. The central problem of military recruitment was how to build effective fighting forces whose loyalty ran to the emperor personally, not to a military aristocracy, tribal network, or local lord who might redirect that loyalty elsewhere.

The Ottoman devshirme system solved this problem through deliberate social engineering. Every few years, Ottoman officials traveled through the Balkan provinces — Christian territories conquered in the 14th and 15th centuries — and selected boys, typically between eight and twenty years old, to be taken from their families, converted to Islam, taught Ottoman Turkish and Islamic law, and educated in the palace schools. The most talented became administrators — including grand viziers, the chief ministers who ran the empire on the sultan's behalf. The rest became janissaries, the elite infantry soldiers who formed the core of the Ottoman military.

The devshirme system was coercive — these boys did not volunteer — but it also created genuine opportunity. A devshirme recruit who rose to become grand vizier or a high-ranking administrator had power and wealth unimaginable to most people of his birth. The system worked for the sultan because devshirme officials had no family connections, inherited wealth, or tribal loyalties inside the empire. Their entire status depended on the sultan. That made them more reliably loyal than hereditary nobles who had independent bases of power.

The Qing Banner system took a different approach. The Manchu founders of the Qing Dynasty organized their military into "banners" — units defined by ethnicity and region — that maintained a distinct Manchu identity separate from the Chinese civilian bureaucracy. The Qing also continued the Chinese civil service examination system, which required aspiring officials to master Confucian texts and demonstrate literary skill through written exams. This created a bureaucracy that was Chinese in culture and language but operated under Manchu imperial oversight. The Banner system gave the Qing a Manchu military force loyal to ethnic identity; the examination system gave them a Chinese bureaucracy loyal to Confucian order and imperial hierarchy.

AP Thinking — Comparison

The Ottoman devshirme and Qing Banner/examination systems both solved the loyalty problem — but through different mechanisms. Devshirme created loyalty by cutting recruits off from independent family connections. The Qing examination system created loyalty by making official status depend on imperial appointment rather than birth. In both cases, the key was making officials depend on the emperor rather than on independent resources or family networks.

4

Key Concept

Accommodation: Working With Local Elites

Direct imperial control was impossible across the full geographic extent of any early modern empire. There were simply not enough trained administrators, enough loyal officials, or enough imperial soldiers to replace every local lord, tribal leader, religious authority, and community elder with someone directly answerable to the emperor. The practical response was accommodation — incorporating existing local elites into the imperial system rather than eliminating them.

The most striking example is Akbar's treatment of Rajput Hindu nobles in the Mughal Empire. The Mughals were Muslim rulers governing a majority-Hindu population. Aurangzeb, Akbar's great-grandson, would later impose strict Islamic governance — reimposing the jizya tax on non-Muslims and destroying Hindu temples — and his reign was marked by rebellion and instability. Akbar took the opposite approach. He abolished the jizya, married into Rajput royal families, recruited Rajput nobles into the mansabdar system at high ranks, and allowed them to maintain their Hindu religious practices. The result was that many of the most effective Mughal military commanders were Hindu Rajputs — loyal to the Mughal emperor not because they were forced to be but because the system gave them status, revenue, and a stake in imperial success.

The Ottoman millet system operated on a similar principle of structured accommodation. The Ottomans recognized several non-Muslim religious communities — Greek Orthodox Christians, Armenian Christians, and Jews — as semi-autonomous millets with the right to govern their own internal affairs. Each millet maintained its own courts, religious schools, charitable institutions, and community leaders. In exchange, millet leaders were responsible for collecting taxes from their communities and maintaining order. The Ottomans gained stability across an enormously diverse population without having to directly administer millions of non-Muslim subjects; the communities gained protection, self-governance, and religious freedom that was often greater than what they had experienced under previous rulers.

"The most stable empires were not necessarily the most directly controlled — they were the ones that made local elites into stakeholders in imperial success."

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Was accommodation a sign of imperial strength or weakness? Strong AP responses engage this question directly. Accommodation could be strength: it maintained stability across vast territories without the cost of direct control. It could also be weakness: it meant surrendering real power to local elites who might defect if conditions changed. The best arguments acknowledge both possibilities and use specific evidence — Akbar versus Aurangzeb, or Ottoman millet stability versus later challenges — to support a clear claim.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

The empires of c. 1450–1750 faced a common problem: how to govern millions of diverse subjects without enough reliable administrators and soldiers. Each empire developed distinctive solutions — the Ottomans recruited enslaved administrators through devshirme, the Mughals ranked nobles through mansabdar, the Qing relied on a Confucian examination bureaucracy. All three combined coercion with accommodation, recognizing that incorporating existing elites was often more effective than replacing them. The key AP skill: connect the design of each system to the specific problem — loyalty, revenue, or stability — it was built to solve.
