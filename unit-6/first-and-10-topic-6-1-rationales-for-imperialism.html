Why it matters

The Learning Objective asks you to explain how ideologies contributed to the development of imperialism from 1750 to 1900. To do that, you have to understand the four rationales — and how they reinforced one another.

01

Setting the Stage

The World Begins to Shrink

By the mid-1800s, something unprecedented was happening to the map. Steam-powered ships now crossed oceans in weeks instead of months. Telegraph cables made communication nearly instantaneous. Factories in Britain, France, and Belgium were churning out textiles, iron rails, and manufactured goods faster than domestic markets could absorb them.

Industrial powers needed two things the rest of the world had in abundance: raw materials to feed their factories, and markets to sell what those factories produced. The logic of expansion became hard to resist. But raw military and economic power alone rarely satisfies a nation for long. People — ordinary citizens, newspaper editors, politicians, clergy — want a story. They want to believe that what their country is doing is not just profitable, but right.

The 19th century gave them four stories. Together, those stories became the ideological architecture of modern imperialism.

AP Thinking — Developments and Processes

Economic and technological change created the opportunity for empire. As you read, watch how each ideology turns that opportunity into something that feels justified — even noble.

02

Rationale One

Survival of the Fittest (Applied to Nations)

Charles Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859. Within a decade, European thinkers had stripped his biological framework of its scientific grounding and applied it to human societies. The result was Social Darwinism — the argument that "survival of the fittest" applied not just to species, but to races and civilizations.

The claim was brutal in its simplicity: some peoples were more advanced than others, and this hierarchy was natural and inevitable. European dominance, in this framework, was not a choice or a crime — it was evidence. The fact that European powers could conquer vast territories in Africa and Asia seemed, to Social Darwinists, to prove their superiority. Weakness was its own condemnation.

This ideology was extraordinarily useful because it made conquest feel like natural law rather than political decision. It also created a moral cover for violence: if certain populations were inherently less developed, then dominating them was simply the world working as it was designed to work.

AP Thinking — Developments and Processes

Social Darwinism took a scientific idea and bent it to political ends. Be ready to explain exactly what it claimed about race and civilization — and how that claim rationalized control over other peoples.

03

Rationales Two and Three

The Flag and the Mission

Nationalism gave imperialism a second engine. As European states consolidated in the 19th century — Germany unifying in 1871, Italy doing the same — national prestige became a political currency. Colonies were a scoreboard. The more territory a nation controlled, the more powerful it appeared to rivals and to its own citizens.

This competition turned imperial expansion into something that felt like patriotism. Citizens who might have questioned the human cost of conquest could be rallied around the flag. Press coverage of military campaigns in Africa or Asia framed them as national achievements. Not acquiring colonies began to feel like national weakness.

Running alongside nationalism was the civilizing mission — the idea that European powers had a duty, not just a right, to transform the societies they controlled. Roads, schools, courts, Christianity: these were offered as gifts from a more advanced civilization to those deemed incapable of governing themselves. The paternalism was thorough. Colonial administrators genuinely believed, in many cases, that they were helping.

What made the civilizing mission particularly effective as an ideology was its moral vocabulary. It transformed extraction into benevolence. The fact that colonized peoples had complex, functioning civilizations of their own — that they were not asking to be transformed — rarely disturbed the logic.

AP Thinking — Causation

Nationalism supplied the competitive drive; the civilizing mission supplied the moral language. Be ready to explain how these two separate ideologies reinforced each other.

04

Rationale Four

The Cross and the Colony

Missionary movements gave imperialism its most explicitly moral framing. Protestant and Catholic missionaries poured into Africa, Asia, and the Pacific throughout the 19th century. Their stated goal was salvation: converting indigenous peoples to Christianity.

Missionaries were often genuinely motivated by religious conviction rather than political calculation. Many lived among colonized peoples for decades, learned local languages, and built schools and hospitals. But their presence served imperial purposes whether they intended it to or not. Mission stations often preceded — and sometimes invited — formal colonial control. The disruption of local religious practices weakened existing social structures, making communities more vulnerable to outside authority.

The desire for religious conversion was not cynical in the way Social Darwinism was. But it produced similar effects: it framed colonized peoples as incomplete, in need of what European civilization uniquely possessed. Whether the deficit was racial, cultural, or spiritual, the conclusion was the same — European presence was justified, perhaps required.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Sincere belief and imperial usefulness are not opposites. Be ready to argue how missionary movements could serve empire even when individual missionaries had no political motive.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Imperialism didn't just happen — it was sold, using four ideologies that made conquest feel like science (Social Darwinism), patriotism (nationalism), duty (the civilizing mission), and salvation (religious conversion).
