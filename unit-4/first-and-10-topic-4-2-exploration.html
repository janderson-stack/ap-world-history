First & 10 Reading

Why They Sailed

Between 1415 and 1522, European sailors mapped the African coast, reached India by sea, found an entirely unknown pair of continents, and sailed all the way around the world. None of this happened by accident. Behind every voyage was a tangle of motives — religious, commercial, political — and behind every motive was state money, state ships, and state power.

Contextualization

Why Europe? The Reconquista and the Desire for Trade

By c. 1450, European merchants and monarchs faced a commercial problem that had been building for decades. Spices — pepper from India, cloves from the Maluku Islands, nutmeg from the Banda Islands, cinnamon from Sri Lanka — were among the most valuable commodities in the medieval European economy. They preserved food, disguised the taste of meat that was turning, and functioned as medicine and luxury goods. European aristocrats and wealthy merchants competed to acquire them. But every spice that reached a European market had passed through multiple middlemen: Arab traders in the Indian Ocean, Italian merchants in the eastern Mediterranean, Ottoman merchants after 1453 — and each middleman took a cut.

The Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453 intensified this problem. The Ottomans now controlled the principal land and sea routes through which goods moved from Asia to Europe. This did not block trade entirely — the Ottomans were willing to trade — but it meant European merchants were dependent on Ottoman goodwill and Ottoman pricing. For Portugal and Spain, positioned at the western edge of Europe with Atlantic coastlines, there was an obvious theoretical alternative: sail around the Ottomans entirely by finding a direct sea route to Asia.

The Reconquista — the centuries-long campaign by Christian kingdoms of the Iberian Peninsula to reclaim territory from Muslim rulers — provided both the ideology and the institutions for this outward push. By 1492, the last Muslim kingdom in Iberia (Granada) had been conquered by Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain. The Reconquista had created a warrior culture, a crusading religious ideology that framed expansion as the spread of Christianity, military orders experienced in fighting Muslim opponents, and a nobility accustomed to the idea that conquest brought wealth, land, and social status. These institutions and attitudes were easily redirected toward Atlantic exploration — the vocabulary was the same (fighting for God, claiming land for the crown) even if the direction was different.

Portugal's geographic position reinforced these motivations. Jutting into the Atlantic as the westernmost point of continental Europe, Portugal had long-standing fishing and coastal trading traditions. Portuguese fishermen regularly ventured into the Atlantic to fish the rich waters off the African coast. Prince Henry the Navigator's court at Sagres became a center for gathering geographic knowledge, bringing together sailors, mapmakers, astronomers, and translators from across the Mediterranean and Atlantic worlds.

AP Thinking — Contextualization

European exploration was not inevitable. It required a specific combination of circumstances: disrupted trade routes raising the price of Asian goods, a crusading religious ideology that could justify expansion, state capacity to fund voyages, and existing maritime expertise. Contextualization means explaining WHY this happened HERE and NOW — in Portugal and Spain, after 1450, in the Atlantic. Without these specific conditions, the same technologies might have existed without producing exploration.

Key Concept

The Portuguese Model — Down Africa and Around to India

Prince Henry the Navigator (1394–1460) is one of the most important figures in the history of exploration — not because he sailed anywhere himself, but because of what he organized. At his court in Sagres on the southwestern tip of Portugal, Henry assembled the best geographic knowledge available, funded expeditions year after year, and established a systematic approach to exploration: each voyage would go a little further than the last, gather information about coastlines and currents, and return to add to the collective knowledge base. This was institutional knowledge-building on a scale that had never been applied to ocean exploration before.

Henry's motivations were multiple and intertwined. He wanted to find a direct sea route to the gold of West Africa, bypassing the trans-Saharan caravan routes controlled by Islamic merchants. He wanted to find the legendary Christian kingdom of Prester John, believed to lie somewhere in Africa or Asia — a potential ally against the Islamic world that surrounded Christendom. And he wanted to find a sea route to the spice-producing regions of Asia. These were not contradictory motives — they all pointed in the same direction: south along the African coast and then, perhaps, east toward India.

The progress was slow but relentless. Portuguese explorers pushed further south each decade: the Azores in the 1430s, the Cape Verde Islands in the 1460s, the mouth of the Congo River in the 1480s. In 1488, Bartolomeu Dias rounded what he called the "Cape of Storms" at the southern tip of Africa — renamed by the Portuguese king the Cape of Good Hope to encourage further exploration. Dias had proven that a sea route to the Indian Ocean existed. Ten years later, Vasco da Gama completed the journey, arriving at Calicut (Kozhikode) on the southwest coast of India in 1498. He brought back enough spices to cover the entire cost of his expedition sixty times over.

The strategic implications were immediate. Portugal had found a direct sea route to the spice trade that bypassed every Islamic and Italian middleman. Within a decade, the Portuguese had established armed trading posts (feitorias) along the African and Indian coasts, using naval power to control the sea lanes and force Asian merchants to trade through Portuguese-controlled ports. This was not the peaceful exchange of goods — it was commercial empire enforced by cannon.

AP Thinking — Causation

The Portuguese voyages were caused by a combination of economic incentive (spice profits), religious motivation (crusading and conversion), state power (royal sponsorship), and technological readiness (the caravel). No single cause explains Portuguese expansion — the AP expects you to identify MULTIPLE causes and explain how they worked together. When asked for causation, describe the mechanism: how did each cause contribute, and why did they reinforce each other?

Key Concept

Columbus, the Caribbean, and the Encounter

Christopher Columbus was a Genoese sailor who had spent years trying to find a royal sponsor for an audacious proposal: reach Asia by sailing west across the Atlantic. Columbus's calculation was based on a significant underestimate of the earth's circumference — he believed Asia was roughly where the Americas actually are, making a western route to Asia seem feasible. Most educated Europeans knew the earth was round; the dispute was about its size. Columbus was wrong about the size. The Portuguese rejected his proposal because their geographers correctly calculated that Asia was far too distant for a westward voyage to be practical. Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain, newly unified after the Reconquista and looking for ways to compete with Portugal, agreed to fund him in 1492.

On October 12, 1492, Columbus landed in the Bahamas, where he encountered the Taíno people — a prosperous, organized society who grew crops, fished, traded among islands, and had never seen a European or a horse. Columbus believed he had reached islands off the coast of Asia (hence "the Indies" and "Indians") and spent three more voyages trying to find the wealthy Asian civilizations he expected. He never acknowledged that he had found something entirely new — continents that no living European knew existed, home to tens of millions of people with sophisticated civilizations.

Spain moved quickly to follow up Columbus's voyages with conquest. The encomienda system — grants of indigenous labor to Spanish colonists — was established within years of Columbus's first landing. Spanish conquistadors, armed with steel weapons, horses (unknown in the Americas), and firearms, began systematically conquering the Caribbean islands and then the mainland. Hernán Cortés destroyed the Aztec Empire between 1519 and 1521; Francisco Pizarro conquered the Inca Empire between 1532 and 1572. The speed and totality of these conquests shocked even the Spanish themselves — and they were made possible not primarily by military technology, but by the devastating effects of European diseases on populations that had never been exposed to them.

The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494), brokered by Pope Alexander VI, divided the non-European world between Spain and Portugal along a line drawn through the Atlantic Ocean — everything west of the line to Spain, everything east to Portugal. The treaty reflected the assumption that two Catholic kingdoms could divide the entire world between them, with no reference to the wishes or existence of the hundreds of millions of people who actually lived in those territories. Other European powers — England, France, the Dutch — rejected this arrangement, but it shaped the initial pattern of European colonization for decades.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Columbus's "discovery" of the Americas is one of the most contested phrases in history. The continents were not unknown — they were home to tens of millions of people with complex civilizations. The AP skill is to recognize multiple perspectives: from the European perspective, this was "discovery"; from the Taíno perspective, it was the beginning of catastrophe. Strong historical thinking acknowledges both perspectives and uses precise language — "Columbus initiated sustained European contact with the Americas" is more accurate than "Columbus discovered America."

Key Concept

Magellan's Circumnavigation and What It Proved

Ferdinand Magellan, a Portuguese navigator sailing under the Spanish flag, led the expedition that completed the first circumnavigation of the globe between 1519 and 1522. The voyage answered the question that Columbus's miscalculation had left open: were the Americas a separate landmass, or were they connected to Asia? Magellan's crossing of the Pacific — a voyage of more than three months across an ocean vastly larger than Columbus had imagined — made the answer undeniable. The Americas were an entirely separate pair of continents, and the Pacific was not a narrow channel but the largest ocean on earth.

The human cost of the circumnavigation was enormous. Magellan departed with five ships and 270 crew members. He was killed in the Philippines in April 1521, caught up in a local political conflict he had inserted himself into while attempting to convert indigenous people to Christianity. His surviving captain, Juan Sebastián Elcano, completed the voyage and limped back to Spain in September 1522 with one ship and eighteen surviving crew members. The spices they carried were enough to cover the entire cost of the expedition and generate a profit — but 252 men had died.

The circumnavigation had enormous strategic implications. Spain now understood the true size and shape of the world, and recognized the Pacific as a potential route to the Asian spice trade. The Philippines — named for the Spanish prince who would become Philip II — became Spain's base in Asia, establishing a Pacific trade route that would eventually connect Spanish America to Chinese markets via the Manila Galleon trade. The world was now, for the first time, truly connected: a single network of sea routes that circled the entire globe.

The broader significance was profound. For thousands of years, the Eastern and Western Hemispheres had developed in complete isolation from each other — separate civilizations, separate biological systems, separate histories. Magellan's circumnavigation was the final proof that these separate worlds were in fact one world, connected by a continuous ocean. The consequences of that connection — the exchange of plants, animals, diseases, and people that historians call the Columbian Exchange — were already transforming every continent on earth. The age of truly global history had begun.

AP Thinking — Continuity & Change

Before 1492, no regular contact existed between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres. After Magellan's circumnavigation, the globe had been connected for the first time in human history. This was one of the most significant changes in world history — it created the preconditions for the Columbian Exchange, the Atlantic slave trade, and global capitalism. Identifying the scale and permanence of this change — and connecting it to subsequent developments — is the core CCOT skill for this period.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

European exploration after c. 1450 was driven by THREE sets of motives — God (converting souls, crusading), Glory (national prestige, competition with rivals), and Gold (profit from trade, especially spices) — and made possible by THREE enabling factors: maritime technology (caravel, compass, astrolabe), state sponsorship (royal patronage), and existing geographic knowledge (portolan charts, Arab navigational traditions). The Portuguese went east around Africa; the Spanish went west and found continents no European knew existed.
